01Why This Unit Matters

Atomic Structure is the lens through which all of chemistry is understood. Electron configurations explain reactivity, periodic trends, bonding preferences, and oxidation states. Every organic mechanism in Phase 3 depends on knowing where electrons live and how easily they move.

📊 JEE Appearance: Almost Every Year
Atomic Structure appears in JEE Main virtually every year — typically 2–3 questions. The Bohr model formula, spectral series identification, quantum number validity, and Cr/Cu exceptions are the four most-tested sub-topics.
✅ High ROI Unit
Unlike Organic mechanisms that require broad context, Atomic Structure questions are self-contained formula/rule applications. 6 hours of focused study → near-certain 4–6 marks per attempt.

02Atomic Models — From Thomson to Bohr

Thomson's Plum Pudding (1904)
  • Atom = positive sphere with electrons embedded
  • Explains neutrality but not atomic spectra
  • Disproved by: Rutherford's gold foil experiment (1909)
Rutherford's Nuclear Model (1911)
  • Dense positive nucleus; electrons orbit far away
  • Most atom = empty space
  • Flaw: Orbiting electrons should spiral in (Maxwell's equations) — model predicts atoms shouldn't exist
Bohr's Model (1913)
  • Electrons occupy fixed energy levels — no radiation while in orbit
  • Energy emitted/absorbed only when jumping between levels
  • Limitation: Works only for H and H-like ions (He⁺, Li²⁺); fails for multi-electron atoms
⚠ JEE Trap
Rutherford's model failed because classical EM theory says an accelerating charge radiates energy — circular orbit = centripetal acceleration = energy loss. Bohr "fixed" this by postulate, not by physics. JEE sometimes asks why Rutherford's model is inadequate.

03Bohr Model — All Equations

All Bohr equations apply to hydrogen-like species: H (Z=1), He⁺ (Z=2), Li²⁺ (Z=3), Be³⁺ (Z=4). Plug the correct Z in.

04Atomic Spectra & Spectral Series

When an electron jumps from a higher energy level (n₂) to a lower level (n₁), a photon is emitted with wavelength given by the Rydberg equation:

Rydberg Equation

1/λ = RH (1/n₁² − 1/n₂²)

RH = 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹  |  n₂ > n₁  |  λ in metres

SeriesTransition (ends at)RegionJEE Tip
Lymann → 1Ultraviolet (UV)Highest energy series
Balmern → 2Visible (400–700 nm)Only visible series — most tested
Paschenn → 3Infrared (IR)Near-IR
Brackettn → 4Infrared (IR)Mid-IR
Pfundn → 5Infrared (IR)Far-IR
⚠ Most Common JEE Question Type
"Electron falls from n=5 to n=1 — how many spectral lines?" Answer: n(n−1)/2 = 5×4/2 = 10 lines. This formula counts all possible transitions among n levels.
💡 Series Limit
The series limit (n₂ → ∞) gives the minimum wavelength (maximum energy) of each series. For Lyman series limit: 1/λ = RH(1/1²) → λ = 91.2 nm.

05Wave-Particle Duality — de Broglie

Louis de Broglie (1924) proposed that all matter has wave properties. The wavelength associated with a particle is:

de Broglie Wavelength

λ = h/mv = h/p

h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s  |  m = mass (kg)  |  v = velocity (m/s)

Key Relationships
  • λ ∝ 1/m — heavier particles have shorter wavelength
  • λ ∝ 1/v — faster particles have shorter wavelength
  • For electron accelerated through potential V: λ = h/√(2meV)
  • Bohr's quantisation condition: 2πr = nλ (orbit circumference = n wavelengths)
JEE Problems
  • Find λ for electron at 2×10⁶ m/s: λ = 6.626×10⁻³⁴ / (9.11×10⁻³¹ × 2×10⁶) ≈ 0.364 nm
  • Macroscopic objects (cricket ball, car) have negligible λ → wave nature unobservable
  • Compare λ of e⁻, p⁺, neutron at same speed → λ ∝ 1/m → electron has largest λ

06Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

It is fundamentally impossible to simultaneously determine both the exact position and exact momentum of a particle:

Uncertainty Principle

Δx · Δp ≥ h/4π

Δx = uncertainty in position  |  Δp = m·Δv = uncertainty in momentum

Also: ΔE · Δt ≥ h/4π (energy-time uncertainty)

💡 Why Bohr's Model Fails Here
Bohr assigned a definite orbit (position) and definite velocity to electrons — this directly violates Heisenberg's principle. Modern quantum mechanics replaces "orbits" with "orbitals" — probability regions where electrons are likely to be found.
⚠ JEE Calculation Tip
Given Δx, find minimum Δv: Δv = h/(4π·m·Δx). Always use m = 9.11×10⁻³¹ kg for electron. Make sure units are consistent (Δx in metres, Δp in kg·m/s).

07All 4 Quantum Numbers

Every electron in an atom is uniquely described by a set of four quantum numbers. Pauli's Exclusion Principle: no two electrons in the same atom can have all four quantum numbers identical.

  • Number of orbitals in shell n: n²

08Shapes of Orbitals

s Orbital (l=0)
  • Spherically symmetric
  • 1 orbital per subshell, max 2 electrons
  • 1s is smallest; 2s, 3s larger with radial nodes
  • Number of radial nodes = n − l − 1
p Orbital (l=1)
  • Dumbbell shape along x, y, z axes
  • 3 orbitals: px, py, pz
  • Max 6 electrons per p subshell
  • Have one nodal plane through nucleus
d Orbital (l=2)
  • 5 orbitals, complex shapes (4-lobed + doughnut)
  • dxy, dyz, dxz, dx²−y², dz²
  • Max 10 electrons per d subshell
  • Key for transition metal chemistry
💡 Nodes Formula
Total nodes = n − 1. Angular (nodal planes) = l. Radial nodes = n − l − 1. Example: 3p orbital → total nodes = 2; angular = 1; radial = 3−1−1 = 1.

09Electron Configuration — Rules & Exceptions

Three Rules for Filling Electrons
  1. Aufbau Principle: Fill orbitals in increasing energy order: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d…
  2. Pauli Exclusion Principle: Max 2 electrons per orbital, with opposite spins
  3. Hund's Rule: In degenerate orbitals (same energy), fill one electron in each before pairing. All unpaired electrons have parallel spins.
⚠ CRITICAL: Cr and Cu Exceptions — Appear Every Year

Half-filled and fully-filled d subshells are extra stable due to exchange energy and symmetry.

  • Cr (Z=24): Expected [Ar]3d⁴4s² → Actual [Ar]3d⁵4s¹ (half-filled d⁵ is stable)
  • Cu (Z=29): Expected [Ar]3d⁹4s² → Actual [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹ (fully-filled d¹⁰ is stable)
  • Mo (Z=42): [Kr]4d⁵5s¹ (same as Cr logic)
  • Ag (Z=47): [Kr]4d¹⁰5s¹ (same as Cu logic)

When asked for config of Cr²⁺ or Cu⁺ — first write the neutral atom config, then remove electrons from the 4s orbital first (not 3d).

10Periodic Trends

Atomic Radius
  • Across period (→): Decreases — more protons, same shell, electrons pulled closer
  • Down group (↓): Increases — more shells, more shielding
  • Exception: Ga < Al (d-block contraction)
Ionisation Energy (IE)
  • Across period (→): Generally increases — smaller atom, electrons harder to remove
  • Down group (↓): Decreases — electrons farther, more shielding
  • Exception: IE(B) < IE(Be); IE(O) < IE(N) — half-filled stability
Electronegativity
  • Across (→): Increases; Down (↓): Decreases
  • Order: F > O > N > Cl > Br > S > C > H
  • Fluorine = most electronegative element
  • Noble gases excluded (don't form bonds)
⚠ IE Anomalies — JEE Favourite

Be has higher IE than B: Be has filled 2s² (stable) vs B has 2p¹ (easier to remove).

N has higher IE than O: N has half-filled 2p³ (extra stable) vs O has 2p⁴ where one orbital has paired electrons (easier to remove the paired one).

Memorise the order: Li < Be > B < C < N > O < F < Ne

12Worked Examples

Example 1 — Spectral Lines (Easy)

Q: How many spectral lines are possible when an electron in hydrogen falls from n=5 to n=1?

Step 1: Number of lines = n(n−1)/2 where n = number of levels involved = 5

Step 2: Lines = 5 × 4 / 2 = 10

Transitions: 5→4, 5→3, 5→2, 5→1, 4→3, 4→2, 4→1, 3→2, 3→1, 2→1

Example 2 — Bohr Energy (Medium)

Q: Find the wavelength of radiation emitted when electron in hydrogen jumps from n=4 to n=2.

Step 1: 1/λ = RH(1/n₁² − 1/n₂²) = 1.097×10⁷ × (1/4 − 1/16)

Step 2: = 1.097×10⁷ × (4/16 − 1/16) = 1.097×10⁷ × 3/16

Step 3: 1/λ = 2.057×10⁶ m⁻¹ → λ = 486 nm

Answer: 486 nm — this is the blue Balmer line (H-β line)

Example 3 — Quantum Numbers (Medium)

Q: Which set of quantum numbers is NOT valid: (a) (2,1,0,+½) (b) (3,3,0,+½) (c) (2,0,0,−½)?

Check (a): n=2, l=1 (valid, l < n), ml=0 (valid, |ml| ≤ l) → VALID

Check (b): n=3, l=3 — but l must be 0 to (n−1) = 0 to 2. l=3 is NOT allowed → INVALID

Check (c): n=2, l=0, ml=0 (only allowed value for l=0) → VALID

Answer: (b) is invalid because l cannot equal n.

Example 4 — Electron Config of Ion (Hard)

Q: Write the electron configuration of Cr³⁺ (Z=24).

Step 1: Cr (neutral) = [Ar]3d⁵4s¹ (exception — half-filled d)

Step 2: Remove 3 electrons from Cr. Always remove from highest energy level first: remove 4s¹ first (1 electron), then 2 from 3d.

Step 3: Cr³⁺ = [Ar]3d³

Note: Many students wrongly write [Ar]3d²4s¹ — don't leave 4s electrons when forming ions.

PYQ — JEE Main 2023

Q: The number of orbitals with n=5, ml=+2 is:

Solution: ml=+2 requires l ≥ 2. For n=5: l can be 2, 3, 4 (and l=2 gives ml up to ±2; l=3 gives up to ±3; l=4 gives up to ±4 — all include ml=+2). So orbitals: 3d, 4f, 5g → 3 orbitals.

PYQ — JEE Main 2022

Q: The energy of an electron in the second Bohr orbit of the hydrogen atom is −3.4 eV. The energy of the electron in the Bohr orbit of He⁺ with the same principal quantum number is:

Solution: En(He⁺) = En(H) × Z² = −3.4 × 4 = −13.6 eV

14Common Traps & Mistakes

❌ Trap 1: Forgetting Z in Bohr formulas for ions
En(He⁺, n=1) = −13.6 × 4 / 1 = −54.4 eV, NOT −13.6 eV. He⁺ has Z=2.
❌ Trap 2: Removing electrons from 3d before 4s when forming ions
When forming cations (Fe²⁺, Cu²⁺), always remove from 4s first, then 3d. Fe: [Ar]3d⁶4s² → Fe²⁺: [Ar]3d⁶ (not [Ar]3d⁴4s²).
❌ Trap 3: Counting spectral lines as n levels instead of transitions
"Falls from n=5 to n=1" — there are 5 levels (1,2,3,4,5), giving 5×4/2 = 10 transitions. Don't say "5 lines."
❌ Trap 4: Applying Bohr model to multi-electron atoms
Bohr's En = −13.6Z²/n² formula ONLY works for H, He⁺, Li²⁺ (one-electron species). For Na, Mg, etc., this formula gives wrong answers.
❌ Trap 5: IE anomalies — B vs Be, O vs N
IE₁(Be) > IE₁(B) even though B comes after Be (higher Z) — Be's 2s² is harder to remove than B's 2p¹. Similarly IE₁(N) > IE₁(O) due to N's half-filled 2p³.
