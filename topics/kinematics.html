01Foundations: Position, Distance, Displacement

Every kinematics problem starts with a frame of reference and an origin. Once those are fixed, three quantities describe where a particle has been:

  • Position — vector from origin to particle. Symbol r; SI unit metre.
  • Distance — total path length travelled. Scalar, always non-negative.
  • Displacement — vector from initial position to final position. Δr = rf − ri.
⚠ Distance ≠ |Displacement|
A runner completing one lap of a 400 m track has distance = 400 m but displacement = 0. JEE loves this distinction in 1D back-and-forth motion problems.
A particle moves A → B → C along a curved path. Distance = total path length. Displacement = straight arrow A → C.

02Speed vs Velocity

Speed is a scalar (rate of distance), velocity is a vector (rate of displacement). Each has an average form (over an interval) and an instantaneous form (at one instant).

Speed (scalar) Average: v̄ = total distance / total time
Instantaneous: |v(t)| — magnitude of velocity vector at instant t.
Velocity (vector) Average: v̄ = Δr / Δt — direction = direction of displacement
Instantaneous: v = dr/dt — direction tangent to path.
📌 JEE rule
Average speed ≥ |Average velocity|, always. They're equal only when motion is in a straight line without reversal.

The instantaneous formula

Position is a function of time, r(t). Velocity is its time-derivative:

v(t) = dr/dt Instantaneous velocity

If you have x(t) = 3t² − 2t, then v(t) = 6t − 2. At t = 4 s, v = 22 m/s.

03Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity, not of speed. A car turning a corner at constant speed is still accelerating — its velocity direction is changing.

a(t) = dv/dt = d²r/dt² Instantaneous acceleration

Three useful identities

From the chain rule, when v is given as a function of position rather than time:

a = v·(dv/dx) Useful when a depends on x

Three ways to read these:

  • If v is given vs t: a = dv/dt.
  • If x is given vs t: a = d²x/dt².
  • If v is given vs x: a = v·dv/dx.
⚠ Speed-vs-velocity acceleration trap
"Object is decelerating" is ambiguous. It usually means speed is decreasing, but in vector language it just means a opposes v. In circular motion at constant speed, the centripetal acceleration is perpendicular to v — neither accelerating nor decelerating in the everyday sense.

04The Three SUVAT Equations

For motion in one direction with constant acceleration, three equations relate u (initial velocity), v (final), a, t, and s (displacement):

v = u + at No s
s = ut + ½ a t² No v
v² = u² + 2as No t

Why they exist

Start from a = dv/dt. Integrate: v = u + at. Now v = dx/dt; integrate again: x = ut + ½at². Eliminate t between these two to get v² = u² + 2as.

🛑 They only work for constant a
If acceleration changes (variable force, air resistance, gravitational variation), these equations do not apply. Use calculus or graphical methods instead.

05Free Fall

Free fall is just SUVAT with a = g ≈ 10 m/s² downward. The trick is sign convention:

🎯 Sign convention rule
  1. Pick a positive direction (usually upward for vertical problems).
  2. Then g = −10 m/s² (since gravity pulls in the negative direction).
  3. Initial velocity, position, and final velocity all signed accordingly.

Worked example: A ball thrown up at 20 m/s. When does it return to the launch point? (g = 10)

Up-positive: u = +20, a = −10, s = 0 (back at start).

s = ut + ½at² → 0 = 20t − 5t² → t(20 − 5t) = 0 → t = 0 or t = 4 s.

So it returns at t = 4 s. Time to peak = 2 s by symmetry.

06Motion Graphs (x-t, v-t, a-t)

Graphs encode kinematics geometrically. Two relations matter for JEE:

Slope = derivative Slope of x-t graph = velocity.
Slope of v-t graph = acceleration.
Area = integral Area under v-t graph = displacement.
Area under a-t graph = change in velocity.
For motion with constant a: x is parabolic in t, v is linear in t, a is constant.

Reading common graphs

  • Horizontal line on x-t: object at rest.
  • Straight diagonal on x-t: uniform velocity.
  • Curved x-t: non-zero acceleration.
  • Straight diagonal on v-t: uniform acceleration.
  • v-t crosses zero: object reverses direction.

072D Motion — Decomposition

Two-dimensional motion is just two independent 1D motions, one along x and one along y. Velocity, acceleration, and SUVAT all apply per-axis.

r(t) = (x(t), y(t))  ·  v(t) = (vx, vy)  ·  a(t) = (ax, ay)

The two components don't interact — vertical gravity does not affect horizontal velocity. The only link is shared time.

📌 The independence principle
Two free particles released at the same instant — one dropped, one fired horizontally — hit the ground at the same time, regardless of horizontal speed. Their vertical motion is identical because vertical acceleration is the same (g down).

08Projectile Motion

A projectile is launched with speed u at angle θ above the horizontal, on flat ground. After launch, the only force is gravity (no air resistance — JEE convention).

Initial: ux = u·cos θ  ·  uy = u·sin θ

Three formulas to memorise

Time of flight: T = 2u·sin θ / g
Maximum height: H = u² sin²θ / (2g)
Range: R = u² sin(2θ) / g

Equation of trajectory (eliminating t):

y = x·tan θ − g x² / (2 u² cos²θ) Parabola in x

Useful identities

  • Maximum range on flat ground: at θ = 45°, Rmax = u²/g.
  • Two angles → same range: θ and 90° − θ give equal R but different T and H.
  • R = 4H · cot θ — handy for quick checks.

09Non-standard Projectiles

Projectile from a height

Launched from height h at angle θ. Vertical: y(t) = h + u sinθ · t − ½gt². Land when y = 0 — solve quadratic for t.

Horizontally projected from a cliff

Special case: θ = 0, height h. Time to ground: T = √(2h/g). Horizontal range: R = u · T = u·√(2h/g).

Projectile up an incline (angle α)

Choose axes along the incline (down-slope: gravity component g sin α; perpendicular: g cos α). Launch at angle β from the incline.

  • Time of flight along incline: T = 2u sinβ / (g cos α).
  • Range along incline: R = 2u² sinβ cos(α+β) / (g cos²α).
  • Max range at β = 45° − α/2.
⚠ Incline trap
Don't measure β from horizontal — measure it from the incline surface. The "max-range angle 45°" rule does not apply on inclined ground.

10Relative Motion

Velocity is always relative to a frame. The velocity of A relative to B is:

vAB = vA − vB Vector subtraction

River-boat problem

A boat with velocity vb (relative to water) crossing a river flowing at vr (ground frame). Three classic JEE questions:

  • Min time across: point boat directly perpendicular to current. Time = w / vb; drift = (vr / vb) · w.
  • Min drift (zero drift): point upstream so that horizontal component cancels current. Requires vb > vr; angle θ = sin⁻¹(vr/vb); time = w / √(vb² − vr²).
  • Min time to cross with given drift: calculus problem.

Rain–man problem

A pedestrian walks at vm. Rain falls vertically at vr. The umbrella angle θ from vertical satisfies:

tan θ = vm / vr

The man tilts his umbrella forward (against motion) by angle θ to keep dry.

11Circular Motion

For a particle moving in a circle of radius r, two angular quantities matter:

  • Angular velocity ω = dθ/dt (rad/s). Linear: v = ω r.
  • Angular acceleration α = dω/dt. Tangential: at = α r.

Centripetal acceleration

Even at constant speed, a particle on a circle has acceleration pointing toward the centre:

ac = v² / r = ω² r Centripetal — always exists in circular motion

Non-uniform circular motion

If speed changes too, total acceleration has two perpendicular components:

|a| = √(ac² + at²)
  • ac — radial (toward centre), magnitude v²/r. Changes direction.
  • at — tangent to path, magnitude dv/dt. Changes speed.

13Worked Examples — Easy → Hard

EasyExample 1 — Average velocity

A car travels 60 km north in 1 h, then 40 km south in 0.5 h. Find average speed and average velocity.

Total distance & displacement

Distance = 100 km. Displacement = 60 − 40 = 20 km north. Total time = 1.5 h.

Compute

Average speed = 100/1.5 ≈ 66.7 km/h. Average velocity = 20/1.5 ≈ 13.3 km/h, north.

Speed 66.7 km/h · Velocity 13.3 km/h N
EasyExample 2 — Free fall

A stone is dropped from a 80 m cliff. (g = 10) Find time to land and impact speed.

Apply SUVAT (down-positive)

s = ½gt² → 80 = 5t² → t = 4 s. v = gt = 40 m/s.

t = 4 s, v = 40 m/s
ModerateExample 3 — Distance in n-th second

A particle starts from rest with constant a = 4 m/s². Find the distance covered in the 5th second.

Use sn = u + a(n − ½)

s5 = 0 + 4·(5 − 0.5) = 18 m.

18 m
ModerateExample 4 — Two angles, same range

A projectile is fired at 30° with speed 50 m/s. What other angle gives the same range? Compare H and T. (g = 10)

Range invariance

Range is unchanged for θ' = 90° − 30° = 60°.

H and T comparison

H ∝ sin²θ, so H(60°)/H(30°) = (sin²60°)/(sin²30°) = (3/4)/(1/4) = 3. The 60° launch goes 3× higher.

T ∝ sin θ, so T(60°)/T(30°) = sin60°/sin30° = √3.

θ' = 60°. H × 3, T × √3.
HardExample 5 — River boat (zero drift)

A river is 100 m wide; current = 3 m/s. A boat moves at 5 m/s in still water. To reach directly opposite, find (i) the angle to point upstream and (ii) crossing time.

Zero drift condition

Horizontal: vb sin θ = vr → sin θ = 3/5 → θ = 37° upstream.

Crossing time

Vertical (across) speed = vb cos θ = 5 · 4/5 = 4 m/s. Time = 100/4 = 25 s.

θ = 37° upstream, t = 25 s
HardExample 6 — Position-dependent acceleration

A particle's velocity at position x is given by v(x) = 4√x m/s. Find its acceleration at x = 4 m.

Use a = v · dv/dx

dv/dx = 4 · ½ x⁻¹/² = 2/√x. At x = 4: v = 8, dv/dx = 1.

Multiply

a = v · (dv/dx) = 8 · 1 = 8 m/s².

a = 8 m/s²

14JEE Previous-Year Questions

JEE Main · 2020 Easy
A ball is thrown vertically upward with velocity 20 m/s. Time taken to reach the maximum height is: (g = 10)
▼ Show

At max height, v = 0. v = u − gt → 0 = 20 − 10t → t = 2 s.

2 s
JEE Main · 2021 Moderate
A particle moves with velocity v = 3t² − 4t + 2 m/s. Acceleration at t = 2 s is:
▼ Show

a = dv/dt = 6t − 4. At t = 2: a = 8 m/s².

8 m/s²
JEE Main · 2019 Moderate
A particle is projected from ground at 60° with speed 20 m/s. Maximum height reached: (g = 10)
▼ Show

H = u²sin²θ/(2g) = 400·(3/4)/20 = 15 m.

15 m
JEE Main · 2022 Moderate
Two cars start from the same point, going east at 60 km/h and north at 80 km/h. Their relative velocity is:
▼ Show

Vectors are perpendicular. |vrel| = √(60² + 80²) = 100 km/h.

100 km/h
JEE Main · 2023 Hard
A stone is thrown horizontally from a tower with velocity 10 m/s. It lands 40 m from the base. Find the height of the tower. (g = 10)
▼ Show

Horizontal: x = vx t → 40 = 10·t → t = 4 s. Vertical: h = ½gt² = 5·16 = 80 m.

80 m
JEE Main · 2018 Moderate
A particle moves in a circle of radius 5 m at constant speed 10 m/s. Magnitude of its acceleration is:
▼ Show

Pure centripetal: a = v²/r = 100/5 = 20 m/s².

20 m/s²
JEE Main · 2024 Hard
An object's velocity satisfies v² = 25 − 16x in SI. Find acceleration as a function of x, then evaluate at x = 1.
▼ Show

Differentiate w.r.t. x: 2v · dv/dx = −16 → v · dv/dx = −8.

But a = v · dv/dx = −8 m/s² — independent of x.

a = −8 m/s² (constant)
JEE Advanced · 2020 Hard
A boat with maximum speed v in still water crosses a river of width d, current speed u (u < v). The minimum time to cross and reach a point exactly opposite the start is:
▼ Show

Boat aims upstream at angle θ = sin⁻¹(u/v) so horizontal component cancels current.

Across-river speed: v cos θ = √(v² − u²). Time = d / √(v² − u²).

t = d / √(v² − u²)

15Common Traps & Mistakes

⚠
Using SUVAT for variable acceleration. v = u + at only works for constant a. For variable a, integrate a(t) or use a = v dv/dx.
⚠
Confusing distance with |displacement|. Always different when motion reverses or curves.
⚠
Missing the sign of g. Pick a positive direction and stick with it. If up is +, then g = −10 (not +10).
⚠
Mixing 2D components. Horizontal and vertical motion are independent. Write x(t) and y(t) on separate lines.
⚠
Forgetting circular motion has acceleration even at constant speed. Centripetal v²/r never goes away.
⚠
Reading slope as area on graphs. Slope of x-t = velocity. Area under v-t = displacement. Don't swap them.
⚠
Treating projectile range formula as universal. R = u²sin2θ/g only works for launch and landing at the same height.
⚠
Adding speeds for relative motion. Velocities are vectors — subtract, don't add scalars unless they're collinear.
