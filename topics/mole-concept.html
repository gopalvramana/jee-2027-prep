01Why Mole Concept is the Foundation

In JEE Chemistry, mole concept is not just one topic — it is the language every other Physical Chemistry topic speaks. Equilibrium constants (Kp, Kc) are ratios of molar concentrations. Electrochemistry uses Faraday's law in moles of electrons. Colligative properties calculate molality. Thermochemistry sums enthalpy per mole of reaction.

If you skip or rush C1, every downstream topic becomes harder than it needs to be because you are simultaneously learning new concepts and fighting mole arithmetic under pressure.

⚠ Shaky on C1 → C5, C6, C7 all become guesswork
Equilibrium (C5), Ionic Equilibrium (C6), and Electrochemistry (C7) each assume fluent mole arithmetic. Students who lost marks in those units very often trace the root cause back to not having drilled C1 enough.
💰 8 hours of work → 5–7 marks secured all year
Mole concept appears in about 2–3 direct questions every JEE Main paper, plus it underlies 4–6 more in other topics. Master it once; collect the marks in every mock and every attempt.

JEE tests C1 in three concrete ways:

  1. Direct questions — "How many atoms in X g of Y?", "Find moles of product from a balanced equation."
  2. Limiting reagent problems — 1–2 per paper, often numerical type (no negative marking).
  3. Concentration interconversion — converting molarity to molality, or finding mole fraction from a given solution composition.

02The Mole & Avogadro's Number

A mole is simply a counting unit — like a "dozen" means 12, a "mole" means exactly 6.022 × 10²³. That number is Avogadro's constant (Nₐ). One mole of any substance contains Nₐ formula units of that substance.

03Molar Mass, Formula Mass & STP Volume

The molar mass of a substance is the mass of exactly one mole of it, expressed in g/mol. Its numerical value equals the molecular weight (formula weight) in amu.

How to calculate molar mass

Sum up: (atomic mass of each element) × (subscript in formula)

H₂SO₄ H: 2 × 1.008 = 2.016
S: 1 × 32.06 = 32.06
O: 4 × 16.00 = 64.00
Total: 98.08 g/mol
Ca₃(PO₄)₂ Ca: 3 × 40.08 = 120.24
P: 2 × 30.97 = 61.94
O: 8 × 16.00 = 128.00
Total: 310.18 g/mol

STP volume — the critical caveat

⚠ 22.4 L/mol applies at 0°C and 1 atm ONLY
At 25°C (298 K) and 1 bar (IUPAC standard), 1 mole of ideal gas occupies 24.5 L, not 22.4 L. JEE Main almost always says "at STP" and expects 22.4 L — use it unless the problem specifies a different temperature.

Atoms in a compound — a common trap

When counting atoms (not molecules), multiply moles by Nₐ and by the number of atoms of that element per formula unit.

📌 Example
How many H atoms are in 36 g of H₂O?
n(H₂O) = 36/18 = 2 mol  →  molecules = 2 × 6.022×10²³ = 1.204×10²⁴
Each molecule has 2 H atoms  →  H atoms = 2 × 1.204×10²⁴ = 2.408×10²⁴

04Empirical & Molecular Formulas

The empirical formula (EF) gives the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms. The molecular formula (MF) gives the actual count. MF = n × EF, where n = (given molar mass) ÷ (EF molar mass).

🎯 5-Step Method: % Composition → Empirical Formula
  1. Assume 100 g sample — % values become grams directly.
  2. Convert grams → moles for each element (divide by atomic mass).
  3. Divide all mole values by the smallest mole value.
  4. Round to integers. If any ratio ends in ~0.5, multiply ALL by 2. If ~0.33 or ~0.67, multiply by 3.
  5. Write the EF. To find MF, divide the given molar mass by the EF molar mass to get n, then multiply subscripts by n.

Worked Example — Glucose derivation

A compound contains 40.0% C, 6.67% H, 53.33% O by mass. Molar mass = 180 g/mol. Find MF.

Combustion Analysis

A CₓHᵧOᵤ compound is burned. Measure CO₂ produced (gives C) and H₂O produced (gives H). The oxygen in the compound = mass of sample − mass of C − mass of H.

📌 Combustion shortcut
n(C) = n(CO₂)  ·  n(H) = 2 × n(H₂O)  ·  n(O) = [mass of sample − m(C) − m(H)] / 16

05Stoichiometry — Mole Ratios from Equations

A balanced chemical equation is a recipe in moles. The coefficients are the mole ratios. Once you have moles of any species, you can find moles of any other species by simple proportion.

🎯 4-Step Stoichiometry Method
  1. Write the balanced equation — every atom on both sides must balance.
  2. Convert given quantity → moles (use n = m/M, or n = V/22.4 at STP).
  3. Use mole ratio from the balanced equation to find moles of the desired species.
  4. Convert moles → asked unit (grams, particles, litres at STP).

Worked Example

How many grams of H₂O are produced when 4 g of H₂ reacts with excess O₂?

Equation: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

  1. n(H₂) = 4 / 2 = 2 mol
  2. Ratio: 2 mol H₂ produces 2 mol H₂O → n(H₂O) = 2 mol
  3. m(H₂O) = 2 × 18 = 36 g
📌 Gas volume at STP version
If the question asks for volume of a gaseous product at STP, just multiply the moles of that gas by 22.4 L/mol at the end. Example: 2 mol H₂O vapour at STP = 2 × 22.4 = 44.8 L.

06Limiting Reagent — The Definitive Method

The limiting reagent (LR) is the reactant that runs out first, stopping the reaction. The amount of product is determined entirely by the LR. The other reactants are in excess — some is left over.

🎯 The one correct method — never fail a LR question
  1. Convert all given masses/volumes to moles.
  2. Divide moles of each reactant by its stoichiometric coefficient.
  3. The reactant with the smallest quotient is the LR.
  4. Use the LR's moles (× ratio) to find moles of product. Convert to grams.
  5. Find excess: moles of excess = moles given − moles consumed (from LR stoich).
❌ The most common wrong approach — comparing raw moles
Many students simply compare the moles of each reactant and call the smaller one the LR. This is wrong whenever the stoichiometric coefficients are unequal. Example: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃. With 1 mol N₂ and 2 mol H₂ — raw moles say N₂ is smaller, but the correct test says: N₂ ratio = 1/1 = 1, H₂ ratio = 2/3 = 0.667 → H₂ is the LR.

Full worked example — N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

07Yield Calculations

Stoichiometry gives the theoretical yield — the maximum possible product if the reaction goes 100% to completion. In practice, the actual yield is always less due to side reactions, losses, and incomplete conversion.

TermDefinitionFormula
Theoretical yieldMaximum product from stoichiometryFrom balanced equation
Actual yieldProduct actually collectedGiven in problem
% YieldEfficiency of the reaction% yield = (actual / theoretical) × 100

Worked Example

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O. Starting with 4 g of CH₄, 8 g of CO₂ is actually collected. Find % yield.

  1. n(CH₄) = 4/16 = 0.25 mol
  2. Theoretical n(CO₂) = 0.25 mol (1:1 ratio) → theoretical mass = 0.25 × 44 = 11 g
  3. % yield = (8/11) × 100 = 72.7%
⚠ Reverse yield problems
JEE sometimes gives the % yield and asks for the actual or theoretical mass. Reverse:
actual = (% yield / 100) × theoretical  ·  theoretical = actual / (% yield / 100)

08All 6 Concentration Terms Side-by-Side

Six different ways to express "how much solute is in how much solution." JEE tests all of them — often asking you to convert between two in the same problem.

TermFormulaUnitsTemp dependent?Main JEE use
Molarity (M) M = n_solute / V(L) mol/L Yes ⚠ Most calculations, titrations
Molality (m) m = n_solute / kg_solvent mol/kg No ✓ Colligative properties (ΔTb, ΔTf)
Mole fraction (X) X_A = n_A / (n_A + n_B) Dimensionless No ✓ Raoult's law, vapour pressure
Normality (N) N = n-factor × M eq/L Yes ⚠ Acid-base and redox titrations
% w/w (m_solute / m_solution) × 100 % No ✓ Commercial concentrations (lab bottles)
% w/v (m_solute (g) / V(mL)) × 100 % Yes ⚠ Pharmaceutical/lab reagents

Dilution

When you add water to a solution, moles of solute are conserved even though volume changes.

M₁V₁ = M₂V₂

M₁, V₁ = initial molarity and volume  ·  M₂, V₂ = final molarity and volume. Works for normality too: N₁V₁ = N₂V₂.

n-factor — what it means

The n-factor is the number of equivalents per mole of solute. It is reaction-dependent, not just compound-dependent.

For acids n-factor = number of H⁺ ions furnished per molecule
HCl → 1  ·  H₂SO₄ → 2  ·  H₃PO₄ → 3
For redox reactions n-factor = change in oxidation state per formula unit
KMnO₄ in acid → 5 (Mn: +7→+2)
KMnO₄ in neutral → 3 (Mn: +7→+4)
⚠ KMnO₄ n-factor changes with medium
This is a favourite JEE trap. Always check whether the medium is acidic, neutral, or basic before assigning n-factor to KMnO₄, K₂Cr₂O₇, or similar oxidising agents.

09Interconverting Concentration Units

The most useful conversion is Molarity ↔ Molality, which requires the solution density. The key is always to pick a basis (1 L of solution, or 1 kg of solvent) and work from there.

Molarity → Molality

m = (1000 × M) / (1000ρ − M × M_w)

ρ = density of solution in g/mL  ·  M_w = molar mass of solute in g/mol  ·  M = molarity in mol/L

Mole Fraction ↔ Molality

For solute A in solvent B:   X_A = (m × M_B) / (1000 + m × M_B)   where M_B is molar mass of solvent (g/mol).

🎯 Worked Conversion: 2 M H₂SO₄, density 1.18 g/mL
  1. Basis: 1 L of solution = 1000 mL.
  2. Mass of 1 L solution = 1000 × 1.18 = 1180 g.
  3. Mass of H₂SO₄ = 2 mol × 98 g/mol = 196 g.
  4. Mass of water (solvent) = 1180 − 196 = 984 g = 0.984 kg.
  5. Molality m = 2 mol / 0.984 kg = 2.03 mol/kg.
  6. Mole fraction X(H₂SO₄): n(H₂SO₄)=2, n(H₂O)=984/18=54.67  →  X = 2/(2+54.67) = 0.0353.
📌 Quick check
Molality is always slightly larger than molarity for aqueous solutions (because molality uses kg of solvent only, while molarity uses total volume of solution). If your answer shows m < M, check your arithmetic.

11Worked Examples — Easy → Hard

🟢 Easy — Particles from mass

Q: How many atoms are in 4.25 g of NH₃?

Solution
  1. M(NH₃) = 14 + 3 = 17 g/mol
  2. n(NH₃) = 4.25 / 17 = 0.25 mol
  3. Molecules = 0.25 × 6.022×10²³ = 1.506×10²³
  4. Each NH₃ has 4 atoms (1 N + 3 H) → total atoms = 4 × 1.506×10²³ = 6.022×10²³ atoms = Nₐ

🟡 Medium — Limiting reagent with Fe₂O₃

Q: Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂. If 160 g of Fe₂O₃ and 84 g of CO are used, find the mass of Fe produced.

Solution
  1. n(Fe₂O₃) = 160/160 = 1 mol  ·  n(CO) = 84/28 = 3 mol
  2. LR test: Fe₂O₃ ratio = 1/1 = 1  ·  CO ratio = 3/3 = 1  →  equal ratio — neither is limiting, both run out exactly
  3. n(Fe) = 1 × 2 = 2 mol  →  m(Fe) = 2 × 56 = 112 g

🔴 Hard — Concentration interconversion chain

Q: A H₂SO₄ solution has molarity 9.8 M and density 1.48 g/mL. Find: (a) % w/w, (b) molality, (c) mole fraction of H₂SO₄.

Solution (basis: 1 L solution)
  1. Mass of 1 L solution = 1000 × 1.48 = 1480 g
  2. Mass of H₂SO₄ = 9.8 × 98 = 960.4 g
  3. (a) % w/w = 960.4/1480 × 100 = 64.9%
  4. Mass of water = 1480 − 960.4 = 519.6 g = 0.5196 kg
  5. (b) m = 9.8 / 0.5196 = 18.86 mol/kg
  6. n(H₂O) = 519.6/18 = 28.87 mol
  7. (c) X(H₂SO₄) = 9.8 / (9.8 + 28.87) = 0.253

🔴 Hard — Combustion analysis + % yield chained

Q: A hydrocarbon CₓHᵧ weighing 1.6 g is burned. 4.4 g of CO₂ and 2.7 g of H₂O are collected. If molar mass = 16 g/mol, identify the compound. If 0.08 mol of this compound gives only 0.07 mol of CO₂, what is the % yield?

Solution
  1. n(CO₂) = 4.4/44 = 0.1 mol → n(C) = 0.1 mol → mass C = 1.2 g
  2. n(H₂O) = 2.7/18 = 0.15 mol → n(H) = 0.3 mol → mass H = 0.3 g
  3. Check: 1.2 + 0.3 = 1.5 g ≈ 1.6 g (small rounding) → no O in compound ✓
  4. C:H ratio = 0.1 : 0.3 = 1 : 3 → EF = CH₃ (EF mass = 15). MF mass = 16 → n = 16/15 ≈ 1 (round) → CH₄ (methane)
  5. Reaction: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O. Theoretical n(CO₂) from 0.08 mol CH₄ = 0.08 mol
  6. % yield = 0.07/0.08 × 100 = 87.5%

12JEE Previous-Year Questions

PYQ 1 — Moles & Particles (JEE Main style)

Q: The number of atoms in 4.25 g of NH₃ is approximately: (Nₐ = 6.023 × 10²³)

Approach: n = 4.25/17 = 0.25 mol → molecules = 0.25Nₐ → each has 4 atoms → total = Nₐ

Answer: 6.023 × 10²³

PYQ 2 — Limiting Reagent

Q: A + 2B → 3C. If 3 moles of A and 5 moles of B are taken, the moles of C formed are:

Approach: A ratio = 3/1 = 3 · B ratio = 5/2 = 2.5 → B is LR → moles of C = 2.5 × 3 = 7.5 mol

PYQ 3 — Molarity Calculation

Q: 4 g of NaOH is dissolved to make 500 mL of solution. Find the molarity.

Approach: n(NaOH) = 4/40 = 0.1 mol · V = 0.5 L → M = 0.1/0.5 = 0.2 mol/L

PYQ 4 — Empirical Formula

Q: A compound contains 75% C and 25% H by mass. Find its empirical formula.

Approach: n(C) = 75/12 = 6.25 · n(H) = 25/1 = 25 · ratio C:H = 6.25:25 = 1:4

Answer: CH₄

PYQ 5 — Dilution

Q: 20 mL of 0.5 M HCl is diluted to 200 mL. What is the new molarity?

Approach: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ → 0.5 × 20 = M₂ × 200 → M₂ = 0.05 M

PYQ 6 — % Yield

Q: In a reaction A → B, 2 mol of A gives theoretical yield 88 g of B (M = 44 g/mol). If actual yield is 66 g, what is % yield?

Approach: Theoretical = 2 × 44 = 88 g · % yield = 66/88 × 100 = 75%

13Common Traps & Mistakes

🪤 Trap 1 — Using 22.4 L at the wrong temperature
22.4 L/mol is valid only at 0°C (273 K) and 1 atm. At 25°C, the molar volume is 24.5 L/mol. Always check the temperature in the problem.
🪤 Trap 2 — Comparing raw moles to find the limiting reagent
The LR is NOT always the one with fewer moles. Always divide moles by the stoichiometric coefficient. In N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃, with 2 mol N₂ and 5 mol H₂: ratios are 2 and 1.67 → H₂ is LR, even though there are more moles of it.
🪤 Trap 3 — n-factor is reaction-specific, not compound-specific
H₂SO₄ has n-factor = 2 in most neutralisations, but = 1 when it acts as a monobasic acid (e.g., with excess NaOH in NaHSO₄ formation context). KMnO₄ has n-factor 5 in acid, 3 in neutral, 1 in strongly alkaline medium.
🪤 Trap 4 — Confusing empirical and molecular formula
CH₂O, C₂H₄O₂, and C₆H₁₂O₆ all have the same empirical formula CH₂O. Without molar mass information, you cannot determine the molecular formula.
🪤 Trap 5 — Using molarity when molality is needed (colligative properties)
Elevation of boiling point (ΔTb) and depression of freezing point (ΔTf) use molality, not molarity. The formula is ΔTb = Kb × m. Using molarity gives a wrong answer.
🪤 Trap 6 — Counting molecules instead of atoms
"Atoms in 18 g H₂O" ≠ Nₐ. n(H₂O) = 1 mol → molecules = Nₐ → but atoms = 3Nₐ (2H + 1O per molecule). Read the question carefully.
