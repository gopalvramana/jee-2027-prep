01Foundations: Force, Mass & Inertia

Before the laws make sense, three ideas must be crystal clear.

What is a force?

A force is any push or pull that, if unbalanced, changes the velocity of a body. Force is a vector — it has magnitude and direction. The SI unit is the newton (N) = kg·m/s².

For JEE, you only ever encounter five kinds of contact/non-contact forces:

  1. Gravity (always present, downward, magnitude mg)
  2. Normal (perpendicular to a contact surface)
  3. Tension (along a string, pulling away from the body)
  4. Friction (along a contact surface, opposing relative motion or tendency)
  5. Spring force (along the spring, magnitude kx)

Anything else (electric, magnetic, applied push) reduces to one of these in the FBD.

Mass & Inertia

Mass measures the resistance of a body to acceleration — its inertia. Doubling the mass halves the acceleration produced by the same force. Mass is a scalar, measured in kg, and (in classical mechanics) it does not change with motion.

⚡ JEE quick-fact
Weight (= mg) is a force measured in newtons. Mass is a property measured in kg. Confusing the two is the most common conceptual slip in JEE-Main — especially in lift problems.

Frames of reference

Newton's laws hold only in inertial frames — frames that are at rest or move with constant velocity (no acceleration). A car braking, a lift accelerating up, a merry-go-round — these are non-inertial frames. To use Newton's laws inside them, we add a pseudo force (covered in §10).

Inertial vs non-inertial frame — Newton's laws hold directly only on the left.

02Newton's First Law — Law of Inertia

"Every body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line, unless compelled by an external force to change that state."

The first law has two consequences worth memorising for JEE:

  1. It defines force. A force is whatever causes a change in velocity. No change → no net force.
  2. It defines an inertial frame. A frame in which an isolated body keeps constant velocity is inertial.

What "uniform motion" actually means

"Uniform motion in a straight line" means constant velocity vector — both magnitude and direction unchanged. A ball in circular motion at constant speed is not in uniform motion (its direction is changing) — so a net force must act on it (the centripetal force).

An isolated body in uniform motion — three snapshots, identical velocity.
⚠ JEE trap
Don't say "no force acts." A book lying on a table has gravity AND normal force on it — but they cancel. The first law requires the net force to be zero, not the absence of forces.

03Newton's Second Law — F = ma

This is the most heavily-tested law on JEE. Its full general form is:

Fnet = dp/dt Newton's 2nd law — momentum form

Where p = mv is linear momentum. For constant-mass systems (almost all JEE problems), this reduces to:

Fnet = m · a For constant mass

The four things you must remember

  1. Vector equation. Apply it separately along each independent axis: ΣFx = max, ΣFy = may.
  2. Net force only. Sum all forces acting on the body — no others.
  3. Acceleration is in the direction of net force. Not the velocity, not the position.
  4. Frame must be inertial — else add pseudo-forces (§10).

Variable-mass systems

Rockets, falling chains, conveyor belts — here mass changes with time. The full law becomes:

Fext = m·(dv/dt) + v·(dm/dt) Variable-mass (rocket-equation precursor)

For the rocket equation: thrust = vrel · |dm/dt|. JEE Advanced occasionally tests this.

Impulse — the time integral of force

Integrating the second law over time gives the impulse–momentum theorem:

J = ∫F dt = Δp = m(vf − vi) Impulse = change in momentum

Useful when force acts for a very short time (collisions, hammer blow, ball bouncing) — when the integral is hard but Δp is easy.

📌 JEE shortcut
For collisions on JEE, always reach for impulse first. You rarely need to know the exact force-vs-time profile — only initial and final momentum.
A net force F at angle θ produces accelerations ax = F cosθ / m, ay = F sinθ / m.

04Newton's Third Law — Action & Reaction

"To every action there is an equal and opposite reaction."

The full statement: If body A exerts a force FAB on body B, then B exerts a force FBA = −FAB on A. Action–reaction pairs:

  • are equal in magnitude
  • are opposite in direction
  • act on different bodies (this is the killer detail)
  • are of the same nature (gravitational ↔ gravitational, normal ↔ normal)
  • act simultaneously — there is no "first" and "then"
🛑 The cardinal rule
In a free-body diagram of one object, you NEVER show both members of an action–reaction pair. You show only the force on that one object. The reaction lives on the other object's FBD.
A pushes B → B pushes A back. The two arrows live on different bodies' FBDs.

Common JEE traps with the third law

  • "Weight (mg) and Normal (N) on a table are an action–reaction pair." Wrong. Both act on the same body. The reaction to weight is the gravitational pull the body exerts on Earth.
  • "Tension on both ends of a string is an action–reaction pair." Wrong unless the string is massless. Tension at end 1 acts on the body at end 1; the reaction is the body at end 1 pulling the string.
  • "Reaction is always less because the heavier body 'wins'." Wrong. Magnitudes are always equal. The accelerations differ because masses differ.

05Free-Body Diagrams — The Universal Method

An FBD is a sketch of a single object showing every force on that object — and nothing else. Done correctly, it converts any mechanics problem into routine algebra.

🎯 The 5-step FBD method
  1. Isolate one body. Replace everything else by the force it exerts on this body.
  2. Sketch a dot or rectangle for the body. Don't draw context (no incline, no other blocks).
  3. Add forces: always start with gravity (mg down). Then normals, tensions, friction, springs, applied — in that order.
  4. Choose axes to align with the expected acceleration (along an incline, vertically in a lift, etc.). Resolve every off-axis force.
  5. Write ΣFx = max and ΣFy = may. Solve.

FBD examples — three setups every JEE student must know

Three canonical FBDs. (a) Equilibrium: N = mg. (b) Frictionless incline: N = mg cosθ; net along slope = mg sinθ. (c) Static hang: T = mg.

06The 5 Forces in Mechanics

① Gravity (Weight)

Always present, always vertical (down toward Earth's centre), magnitude W = mg with g ≈ 9.8 m/s² (use 10 in JEE for speed unless specified). It does not change with the surface, the lift, or the angle of an incline — it is a property of the body and the gravitational field.

② Normal force

A contact force perpendicular to the surface, pushing the body away from the surface. Its magnitude is not a fixed formula — it is whatever it has to be so that the body does not penetrate the surface. Common results:

  • Block on horizontal floor at rest: N = mg
  • Block on incline at angle θ: N = mg cosθ
  • Block in lift accelerating up at a: N = m(g + a)
  • Block in lift accelerating down at a: N = m(g − a)
  • Lift in free-fall (a = g): N = 0 ← apparent weightlessness

③ Tension

The pull of a string/rod on whatever is attached. Tension is directed away from the body along the string. For a massless inextensible string: tension is the same at every point. If the string has mass or passes over a frictional pulley, tensions differ on the two sides.

④ Friction

Detailed treatment in §9. Always tangential to the contact surface, opposing the tendency of relative motion (static) or actual relative motion (kinetic).

⑤ Spring force

For an ideal spring of constant k, displaced from natural length by x:

Fspring = −k·x Hooke's law (restoring force)

The minus sign means the force always points back toward the natural length. Stretched → pulls in. Compressed → pushes out.

07Inclined Planes — The Master Setup

Inclined planes appear in 30%+ of JEE-Main NLM questions. The trick is always the same: rotate your axes so x is along the slope (down-the-slope positive) and y is perpendicular to the slope.

Tilted axes: along the slope and perpendicular to it. Weight resolves into mg sinθ (down-slope) and mg cosθ (into surface).

Standard results (memorise the derivation, not the answer)

For a block of mass m on an incline of angle θ:

  • Normal: N = mg cosθ
  • Frictionless slide: a = g sinθ
  • With friction (sliding down): a = g(sinθ − μk cosθ)
  • Sliding condition (static): tanθ > μs (else block stays put)
  • Pushed up incline with force F along slope: a = F/m − g sinθ − μkg cosθ
⚠ Don't memorise; derive
JEE will twist the geometry — angle of friction, double inclines, wedge that itself accelerates. If you only memorised a = g sinθ, you're stuck. If you internalised the FBD method, every variation is the same five steps.

08Pulleys & Connected Bodies

The trick for any multi-body problem: write F = ma separately for every body, then use constraint equations to relate accelerations.

Atwood machine — the classic

Atwood machine: massless string, frictionless pulley.

For each body, take "down" as positive in the direction it actually moves:

m₂g − T = m₂a       T − m₁g = m₁a Two equations, two unknowns (T and a)

Adding: a = (m₂ − m₁)g / (m₁ + m₂). Substituting back: T = 2 m₁ m₂ g / (m₁ + m₂).

Modified Atwood (one block on a table)

Block m₁ on frictionless horizontal table, connected over a pulley at the edge to hanging block m₂:

a = m₂g / (m₁ + m₂)      T = m₁m₂g / (m₁ + m₂)

Block-on-incline + hanging block

Block m₁ on incline angle θ (with friction coefficient μ between block and incline), connected over a pulley at the top to hanging block m₂. Assume m₂ heavy enough to drag m₁ up the incline:

a = [m₂g − m₁g sinθ − μ m₁g cosθ] / (m₁ + m₂)

09Friction in Depth

Friction is a contact force tangential to the surface, opposing relative motion (or its tendency). It comes in two flavours.

Static friction (no relative motion yet) 0 ≤ fs ≤ μs N
Self-adjusting — equals exactly the applied force needed to keep the body still, up to a maximum of μsN.
Kinetic friction (already sliding) fk = μk N
Constant magnitude (independent of speed), direction always opposite to actual motion.

Universal fact: μs > μk. It always takes more force to start sliding than to continue sliding.

Angle of friction & angle of repose

The angle of friction λ is defined by tanλ = μs. The angle of repose is the steepest incline at which a block remains static — and turns out to equal λ.

So: an incline at angle θ:

  • θ < λ: block stationary (static friction holds it).
  • θ = λ: on the verge of slipping.
  • θ > λ: block accelerates down; a = g(sinθ − μkcosθ).

Friction on multi-block stacks

Two blocks A (top) and B (bottom) on a horizontal surface. Force F applied on B. The friction between A and B is what makes A accelerate. Procedure:

  1. Find the maximum friction A can experience: fmax = μ mA g.
  2. Find the acceleration A would need to keep up: a = F / (mA + mB) if no slipping.
  3. Check: if mA·a ≤ fmax, no slipping. Otherwise A slides on B.

10Non-Inertial Frames & Pseudo Forces

If you analyse a problem from a frame that itself accelerates with acceleration aframe, Newton's second law fails — unless you add a pseudo force on every body of mass m:

Fpseudo = −m · aframe Opposite to the frame's acceleration

Lift (elevator) problems — the cleanest example

Person of mass m standing on a weighing machine inside a lift. The machine reads apparent weight = the normal force on the person.

Lift accelerating upward at a From outside (inertial): N − mg = ma → N = m(g+a). Apparent weight increases.
Lift accelerating downward at a N = m(g − a). Apparent weight decreases.
Free-fall (a = g) N = 0. The person is weightless — feet barely touch the floor.
Constant velocity (a = 0) N = mg. Same as standing on solid ground.

Solving inside the non-inertial frame

The lift accelerating up at a. Inside the lift, the person is at rest. Apply Newton's law in the lift frame: N − mg − m·a = 0 (where −m·a is the pseudo force pointing down because the frame accelerates up). Solve: N = m(g+a). Same answer, less geometry — pick whichever frame is faster.

📌 When to switch to a non-inertial frame
Use the moving frame when one body is at rest in that frame (e.g. a person standing in a lift, a bob hanging in an accelerating car). It removes the kinematic constraint and reduces the algebra.

11Constraint Relations (JEE Advanced)

When two bodies are connected by a string or in contact such that they cannot move independently, their accelerations are related by a constraint equation. Skip this if you're targeting JEE Main only — it shows up often in JEE Advanced.

Two methods

① String-length method

If the string is inextensible, the total length is constant. Differentiate twice with respect to time → acceleration relation.

Example: for a single Atwood machine, L = y₁ + y₂ + (constant), so dL/dt = 0 → v₁ = −v₂ → a₁ = −a₂. The two blocks move with equal-magnitude opposite accelerations.

② Velocity-along-string method (faster)

The component of velocity of any body along the string equals the component of velocity of any other point on that string along the string. Same for acceleration.

Example — wedge problem: A wedge slides on a horizontal frictionless floor with horizontal acceleration A. A block sits on the wedge surface (angle θ). If the block slides on the wedge with relative acceleration arel along the slope, the block's true acceleration in the ground frame has both horizontal and vertical components — set up the constraint that the block stays in contact (perpendicular relative velocity = 0).

⚠ JEE Advanced trap
Pulley systems with movable pulleys: the acceleration of a block hanging from a movable pulley is half the acceleration of the load on the other side (if the pulley is in the middle of the string). String constraints are mass-independent — they're pure geometry.

13Worked Examples — Easy → Hard

Each example is solved in the JEE method: FBD → axes → equations → answer.

Easy Example 1 — Pull on a frictionless surface

A 5 kg block on a frictionless horizontal surface is pulled by a horizontal force of 20 N. Find its acceleration.

Setup

Forces on block: gravity (down, 50 N), normal (up, 50 N — they cancel), applied 20 N horizontal.

Apply F = ma

Horizontally: 20 = 5a → a = 4 m/s².

a = 4 m/s²
Easy Example 2 — Apparent weight in a lift

A person of mass 70 kg stands on a weighing machine in a lift accelerating up at 2 m/s². What does the machine read? (g = 10)

FBD on person

Up: normal N (= scale reading). Down: weight 700 N. Net up = ma.

Equation

N − mg = ma → N = m(g + a) = 70 × 12 = 840 N.

N = 840 N
Moderate Example 3 — Block on a rough incline

A 4 kg block is placed on a 30° incline. The coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.2. Find the acceleration of the block as it slides down. (g = 10)

Resolve along the slope

Down-slope: mg sinθ = 4 × 10 × 0.5 = 20 N.

Friction (up the slope): μ mg cosθ = 0.2 × 4 × 10 × (√3/2) ≈ 6.93 N.

F = ma along slope

20 − 6.93 = 4a → a ≈ 3.27 m/s².

a ≈ 3.27 m/s² (down the slope)
Moderate Example 4 — Two blocks pushed together

Blocks A (3 kg) and B (5 kg) are in contact on a frictionless horizontal surface. A horizontal force of 24 N is applied on A pushing it toward B. Find (i) the system's acceleration and (ii) the contact force between A and B.

System equation

a = F/(mA+mB) = 24/8 = 3 m/s².

Isolate B

Only force on B (horizontal) is the contact force NAB from A. Then NAB = mB·a = 5 × 3 = 15 N.

a = 3 m/s², contact force = 15 N
Hard Example 5 — Block on accelerating wedge (no friction)

A wedge of angle 30° is accelerated horizontally with acceleration a. A small block placed on the smooth incline does not slide relative to the wedge. Find a. (g = 10)

Frame choice

Use the wedge frame (non-inertial). Block is at rest in this frame.

Forces on block

Gravity mg down, normal N perpendicular to incline, pseudo force ma horizontal (opposite to wedge motion).

Resolve along the slope

For block to stay at rest: mg sinθ = ma cosθ → a = g tanθ.

For θ = 30°: a = 10 × tan 30° = 10/√3 ≈ 5.77 m/s².

a = g tanθ ≈ 5.77 m/s²
Hard Example 6 — Two-block stack with friction (which slips first?)

Block A (mass 2 kg) sits on top of block B (mass 4 kg). The lower surface is frictionless; between A and B, μs = μk = 0.4. A horizontal force F is applied to B. Find the maximum F such that A does not slip on B. (g = 10)

Think — what makes A accelerate?

Only friction (from B). Max friction A can experience: fmax = 0.4 × 2 × 10 = 8 N. So max acceleration A can have without slipping: amax = 8/2 = 4 m/s².

If no slipping, common a

a = F/(mA+mB) = F/6. Set F/6 = 4 → F = 24 N.

Fmax = 24 N
Hard Example 7 — Movable pulley constraint

In the system shown: a block of mass M hangs from a movable pulley; a string from the movable pulley goes over a fixed pulley and connects to a block of mass m on a frictionless horizontal table, pulled by horizontal force F. Find the acceleration of M. (Massless strings & pulleys.)

String constraint

Length of the string from the wall, around the movable pulley, to F: as M moves down by x, both string segments on either side of the movable pulley shorten by x → so F-end string moves 2x. Therefore am = 2 aM.

Force equations

Tension in F-string: T. Block on table: F − T = m·am = 2m·aM. Movable pulley with M hanging: tension on each side is T (single string), so net up on M = 2T − Mg = M·aM (taking up positive — M actually goes down so aM is negative if convention).

Solve

From the first: T = F − 2m·aM. Substitute: 2F − 4m·aM − Mg = M·aM. So aM = (2F − Mg) / (M + 4m).

aM = (2F − Mg) / (M + 4m)

14JEE Previous-Year Questions

Selected questions in the style of recent JEE Main & Advanced papers (2018–2024). Click each to reveal the worked answer.

JEE Main · 2020 Moderate
A block of mass 10 kg is in contact with a vertical wall. The coefficient of friction between block and wall is 0.5. What minimum horizontal force is needed to prevent the block from sliding down? (g = 10 m/s²)
▼ Show solution

The horizontal force F provides the normal: N = F. Maximum static friction (vertical, upward) = μF. To prevent sliding: μF ≥ mg → F ≥ mg/μ = 100/0.5 = 200 N.

Fmin = 200 N
JEE Main · 2019 Easy
A man of mass 70 kg stands on a weighing machine in a lift moving downward with a uniform acceleration of 5 m/s². The reading of the weighing machine is: (g = 10)
▼ Show solution

N = m(g − a) = 70 × (10 − 5) = 350 N.

350 N
JEE Main · 2021 Moderate
Two blocks of masses 4 kg and 6 kg are connected by an inextensible string over a frictionless pulley. The system is released from rest. Find the tension in the string. (g = 10)
▼ Show solution

Atwood. T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁+m₂) = 2 × 4 × 6 × 10 / 10 = 48 N.

T = 48 N
JEE Main · 2022 Moderate
A block just begins to slide down a rough inclined plane when the angle of inclination is 45°. The coefficient of static friction between the block and the plane is:
▼ Show solution

Angle of repose = angle of friction → μs = tan 45° = 1.

μs = 1
JEE Main · 2023 Hard
A block A of mass 2 kg rests on top of a 4 kg block B placed on a frictionless horizontal surface. The coefficient of friction between A and B is 0.5. A horizontal force F is applied to B. The maximum value of F so that A and B move together is: (g = 10)
▼ Show solution

Max friction on A: fmax = 0.5 × 2 × 10 = 10 N. Max acceleration A can sustain: a = 5 m/s². For combined motion: F = (mA+mB)·a = 6 × 5 = 30 N.

Fmax = 30 N
JEE Main · 2018 Moderate
A particle of mass m is moving with constant velocity v on a smooth horizontal table. It strikes a vertical wall and rebounds with the same speed. If the time of contact is Δt, the magnitude of the average force exerted by the wall on the particle is:
▼ Show solution

Δp = m·v − (−m·v) = 2mv. Impulse–momentum: Favg · Δt = 2mv → Favg = 2mv/Δt.

Favg = 2mv/Δt
JEE Advanced · 2019 Hard
A wedge of angle 30° is on a frictionless horizontal surface. A block of mass m is placed on the smooth wedge. The wedge has mass M. When released, find the acceleration of the wedge.
▼ Show solution

This requires constraint analysis. Let A be the wedge's horizontal acceleration (leftward) and ar the block's acceleration along the slope (down the slope, relative to wedge). The block stays in contact: components perpendicular to slope match. Resulting standard result:

A = m·g·sinθ·cosθ / (M + m·sin²θ).

For θ = 30°, m = M (or whatever the question gives), substitute. With sin30 = 0.5, cos30 = √3/2: A = m·g·(√3/4) / (M + m/4).

A = mg sinθ cosθ / (M + m sin²θ)
JEE Main · 2024 Moderate
A block of mass 1 kg is pushed up a rough incline of 30° with an initial speed of 10 m/s. If μk = 0.1, the deceleration on the way up is: (g = 10, √3 ≈ 1.73)
▼ Show solution

Going up: gravity AND friction both act down the slope. a = g(sinθ + μ cosθ) = 10(0.5 + 0.1 × 0.866) ≈ 10 × 0.587 ≈ 5.87 m/s².

a ≈ 5.87 m/s² (deceleration)
📚 Practice strategy
Don't just read solutions. Cover the answer, attempt every problem cold, then check. Aim for 50+ NLM problems (across all difficulties) before the exam — the formulas are few, but the FBD intuition takes practice.

15Common Traps & Mistakes

⚠
Mixing forces from two bodies in one FBD. Each FBD shows forces on one body. The reaction lives elsewhere.
⚠
Assuming N = mg always. True only on a stationary horizontal surface. Inclines, lifts, accelerating frames all break this.
⚠
Friction = μN always. No — that's the maximum static friction. Actual static friction is whatever is needed, up to μsN.
⚠
Forgetting friction direction reverses going up an incline. Going down → friction up. Going up → friction down. Sign change matters.
⚠
Treating tension as the same on both sides of a frictional or massive pulley. It is not — only for a massless string over a frictionless pulley.
⚠
Forgetting the pseudo force in non-inertial frames. If you're sitting in the lift's frame, you must add −maframe on every body.
⚠
Calling weight and normal an action–reaction pair. They both act on the same body. Reaction to weight is the body pulling Earth up.
⚠
Resolving along x/y on an incline. Always resolve along/perpendicular to the slope — the algebra collapses.
⚠
Ignoring that string constraints are kinematic. A movable pulley halves the speed of the load. Forces don't tell you this — geometry does.
⚠
Using F = ma in collisions. Force is huge for a tiny time and unknown — use impulse F·Δt = Δp instead.

16One-Page Cheat Card

🎯 Newton's Laws — exam-day recall

  • 1st law: No net force ⇒ velocity constant. Defines inertial frame.
  • 2nd law: Fnet = ma per axis. Mass constant ⇒ F = ma; otherwise F = dp/dt.
  • 3rd law: Equal & opposite, on different bodies.
  • FBD method: isolate body → list every force → choose axes → write ΣF = ma.
  • Normal: N = mg (flat) · mg cosθ (incline) · m(g±a) (lift).
  • Friction: fs ≤ μsN (self-adjusting) · fk = μkN · μs > μk.
  • Incline (sliding): a = g(sinθ − μcosθ). Repose: tanλ = μs.
  • Atwood: a = (m₂−m₁)g/(m₁+m₂) · T = 2m₁m₂g/(m₁+m₂).
  • Lift: apparent N = m(g+a) up · m(g−a) down · 0 in free-fall.
  • Non-inertial: add pseudo −maframe on every body.
  • Collisions: use impulse J = F·Δt = Δp, never F = ma directly.
