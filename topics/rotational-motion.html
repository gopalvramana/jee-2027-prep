1. Translational ↔ Rotational Analogues

TranslationalSymbolRotationalSymbol
DisplacementsAngular displacementθ (rad)

MassmMoment of inertiaI (kg·m²)

The rotational equations are IDENTICAL in form to translational ones. Replace m→I, F→τ, v→ω, a→α, s→θ. Learn the pattern, not 9 separate formulas.

2. Torque

τ = r × F = rF sinθ
r = distance from axis to point of force application · θ = angle between r and F · τ is a vector (direction by right-hand rule)
Torque — Key Points
  • Moment arm = r sinθ = perpendicular distance from axis to line of action of force
  • Force through the axis: τ = 0 (no rotation produced)
  • Force parallel to r (along the line joining axis and point): τ = 0 (sinθ = 0)
  • Maximum torque: force perpendicular to r (θ = 90°): τ = rF
Torque and Energy have the same dimensions [ML²T⁻²] but are completely different quantities. Torque is N·m (a moment), not Joules (energy). JEE favourite trap!

3. Rotational Newton's 2nd Law

τ_net = Iα
Net torque about an axis = moment of inertia about that same axis × angular acceleration about that axis.
Both τ and I must be about the SAME axis. This is the most common rotational error — using MOI about one axis and torque about another.

5. Moment of Inertia

I = Σmᵢrᵢ² (discrete) or I = ∫r² dm (continuous)
r = perpendicular distance of each mass element from the rotation axis
Properties of MOI
  • MOI is always positive (sum of positive terms)
  • Depends on mass distribution AND choice of axis
  • MOI is MINIMUM about the axis through the centre of mass
  • Unit: kg·m² — do NOT confuse with torque (N·m)

7. Parallel Axis Theorem

I = I_cm + Md²
I = MOI about any axis parallel to the CM axis · d = perpendicular distance between the two axes
Applying Parallel Axis Theorem

Step 1: Find I_cm (MOI about axis through centre of mass)

Step 2: Measure d = distance from CM to the new parallel axis

Step 3: Add Md²

Example: Rod about one end = ML²/12 + M(L/2)² = ML²/12 + ML²/4 = ML²/3 ✓

IMPORTANT: One of the two axes MUST be through the centre of mass. You cannot use the parallel axis theorem between two arbitrary parallel axes.

8. Perpendicular Axis Theorem

I_z = I_x + I_y
Only for thin flat laminas (2D objects). z is perpendicular to the lamina. x and y are in the plane.
Application

Disk: I_z (through centre, ⊥ to disk) = ½MR²

By symmetry: I_x = I_y (any two perpendicular diameters)

So: ½MR² = I_x + I_y = 2I_x → I_x = ¼MR²

This is why disk's diameter MOI = ¼MR².

9. Angular Momentum

CaseFormula
Rigid body rotatingL = Iω
ParticleL = r × p = mvr sinθ
Torque-angular momentum relationτ = dL/dt
τ = dL/dt is the rotational analogue of Newton's 2nd law F = dp/dt. If net torque = 0, angular momentum is constant (conserved).

10. Conservation of Angular Momentum

If τ_ext = 0: L = Iω = constant → I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂
When the moment of inertia changes (by changing mass distribution), ω changes inversely to keep L constant.

11. Rolling Without Slipping

v_cm = Rω (rolling condition — contact point has zero velocity relative to ground)
QuantityFormula
Total KE (rolling)KE = ½mv² + ½Iω² = ½mv²(1 + I/mR²)
Solid sphereKE_rot/KE_total = 2/7 (rotation is 2/7 of total)
Solid disk/cylinderKE_rot/KE_total = 1/3
Hollow sphereKE_rot/KE_total = 2/5
Hollow cylinder (ring)KE_rot/KE_total = 1/2

12. Rolling Down an Incline

a = g sinθ / (1 + I/mR²)
Rolling acceleration on incline of angle θ. Lower I/mR² ratio → higher acceleration → reaches bottom first.
ObjectI/mR²a = g sinθ / (1 + I/mR²)Speed at bottom
Solid sphere2/55g sinθ/7Fastest — arrives first
Solid disk1/22g sinθ/3
Hollow sphere2/33g sinθ/5
Hollow cylinder (ring)1g sinθ/2Slowest — arrives last
Energy Method for Rolling on Incline

mgh = ½mv² + ½Iω² = ½mv²(1 + I/mR²)

v = √[2gh/(1 + I/mR²)]

Lower moment of inertia coefficient → higher speed at bottom. Sphere always beats disk, disk beats hollow cylinder.

13. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Flywheel Stopping

A flywheel (I = 4 kg·m²) spinning at 600 rpm is brought to rest by a braking torque of 4π N·m. How long does it take?

ω₀ = 600 rpm = 600 × 2π/60 = 20π rad/s
τ = Iα → −4π = 4 × α → α = −π rad/s²
ω = ω₀ + αt → 0 = 20π − π×t → t = 20 s
Example 2 — Skater Pulls in Arms

A skater with I₁ = 5 kg·m² spins at ω₁ = 4 rad/s. By pulling arms in, I₂ = 1.25 kg·m². Find ω₂ and ratio of KE.

L conserved: I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂
ω₂ = 5×4/1.25 = 16 rad/s
KE₁ = ½×5×16 = 40 J
KE₂ = ½×1.25×256 = 160 J
KE ratio = 4:1 (energy INCREASED — skater does work pulling arms in)
Example 3 — Solid Sphere Rolling

A solid sphere rolls down a 10 m long incline (30°) from rest. Find speed at bottom and time taken.

h = 10 sin30° = 5 m
v = √[2gh/(1 + 2/5)] = √[2×10×5/1.4] = √(100/1.4) ≈ 8.45 m/s
a = g sin30°/(1 + 2/5) = 10×0.5/1.4 = 3.57 m/s²
s = ½at² → 10 = ½×3.57×t² → t = √(20/3.57) ≈ 2.37 s

14. Common JEE Traps

Trap 1: Torque [N·m] and energy [J] have same dimensions [ML²T⁻²] but are not the same. Never convert N·m to Joules in rotation.
Trap 2: τ = Iα requires τ and I about the SAME axis. Torque about one axis ≠ Iα about another axis.
Trap 3: Parallel axis theorem requires one axis to be through the CM. Cannot use it between two arbitrary axes.
Trap 4: Perpendicular axis theorem is ONLY for thin flat laminas — NOT for 3D objects like spheres or cylinders.
Trap 5: Rolling without slipping: v = Rω at the CM. Contact point has ZERO velocity. Top of the object has velocity 2v (2× the CM velocity).
Trap 6: When a disk drops on a rotating disk (no external torque): I₁ω₁ = (I₁+I₂)ω_f. Kinetic energy is NOT conserved (there's slipping = friction = energy loss).
Trap 7: The order of objects reaching bottom of incline (fastest to slowest): solid sphere → solid disk → hollow sphere → hollow cylinder. This depends only on shape (I/mR² ratio), not mass or radius.
