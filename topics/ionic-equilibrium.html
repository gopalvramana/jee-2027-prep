01Why This Unit Matters

Ionic equilibrium directly builds on C5 (Chemical Equilibrium) — it's just equilibrium applied to ions in solution. The Ka/Kb framework is the same ICE table approach you already know. JEE tests acid-base chemistry in nearly every paper, often with 2 direct questions and further application in electrochemistry.

📊 JEE Pattern: Numericals Dominate
Most C6 JEE questions are calculation-based: find pH of weak acid/base, buffer pH after adding strong acid/base, molar solubility from Ksp. The formulas are few and the pattern is always the same — recognise it and the marks are nearly automatic.
✅ Four Formulas to Master
1. [H⁺] = √(Ka·C) for weak acids  |  2. pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]) for buffers  |  3. Ksp = (s)^a (ns)^b for solubility  |  4. Ka × Kb = Kw for conjugate pairs. These four handle ~80% of questions.

02Acid-Base Theories

Arrhenius (1884)
  • Acid: produces H⁺ in water
  • Base: produces OH⁻ in water
  • Limited to aqueous solutions
  • Examples: HCl (acid), NaOH (base)
Brønsted-Lowry (1923)
  • Acid: proton (H⁺) donor
  • Base: proton acceptor
  • Works in non-aqueous solvents too
  • Conjugate pairs: HA/A⁻ and BH⁺/B
  • NH₃ is a base (accepts H⁺ → NH₄⁺)
Lewis (1923)
  • Acid: electron pair acceptor
  • Base: electron pair donor
  • Broadest definition — includes reactions with no proton transfer
  • BF₃ is a Lewis acid (accepts from NH₃)
  • AlCl₃, Fe³⁺, H⁺ are Lewis acids
💡 JEE Application of Lewis Theory
Lewis acid-base concept is tested in questions about: (1) why BF₃ reacts with NH₃ (Lewis acid-base pair), (2) why AlCl₃ is a catalyst in Friedel-Crafts reactions (Lewis acid), (3) ligand-metal bonding in coordination compounds (ligands = Lewis bases; metal = Lewis acid).

03Kw, pH and pOH

Water Self-Ionisation

H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + OH⁻

Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C

Pure water: [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 10⁻⁷ M → pH = 7 (neutral at 25°C)

  • pH = −log[H⁺]  |  pOH = −log[OH⁻]
  • pH + pOH = pKw = 14 (at 25°C only)
  • pKa + pKb = pKw = 14 (for conjugate pair)
⚠ Kw is Temperature Dependent
Kw = 10⁻¹⁴ only at 25°C (298 K). At higher temperatures Kw increases, so neutral pH < 7. At 37°C (body temp), Kw ≈ 2.4×10⁻¹⁴, neutral pH ≈ 6.8. JEE sometimes asks about this — "neutral" means [H⁺]=[OH⁻], not pH=7.

04Strong Acids & Bases — pH Calculations

Species[H⁺] or [OH⁻]Example
Strong monoprotic acid (HCl, HNO₃)[H⁺] = C (full dissociation)0.01 M HCl → [H⁺]=0.01 M → pH=2
Strong diprotic acid (H₂SO₄ dilute)[H⁺] = 2C0.01 M H₂SO₄ → [H⁺]=0.02 M → pH=1.7
Strong monobase (NaOH, KOH)[OH⁻] = C → [H⁺]=Kw/C0.01 M NaOH → pOH=2 → pH=12
Strong dibase (Ba(OH)₂)[OH⁻] = 2C0.001 M Ba(OH)₂ → [OH⁻]=0.002 → pOH=2.7 → pH=11.3
💡 Very Dilute Strong Acid — Trap
For very dilute HCl (say 10⁻⁸ M), [H⁺] ≠ 10⁻⁸ M. Water's own [H⁺] contribution (10⁻⁷ M) becomes significant. Total [H⁺] = 10⁻⁸ + √(10⁻⁸ × 10⁻⁷ + 10⁻¹⁴) — but JEE rarely goes here. The key insight: pH can never exceed 7 for any acid solution.

05Weak Acids & Bases — Ka, Kb, α

Weak acids partially dissociate. Apply ICE table with approximation when valid:

Weak Acid (HA) ICE Table

IC≈00

EC−xxx

Ka = x²/(C−x) ≈ x²/C when α << 1 (i.e., Ka << C)

→ [H⁺] = x = √(Ka·C)  |  α = x/C = √(Ka/C)  |  pH = ½(pKa − log C)

Approximation valid when α < 5% (i.e., Ka/C < 0.0025). Otherwise solve quadratic: x² + Ka·x − Ka·C = 0

Enter Ka and initial concentration to find pH, [H⁺], and degree of dissociation:

Weak Base (B) — Analogous Treatment

B + H₂O ⇌ BH⁺ + OH⁻  |  Kb = [BH⁺][OH⁻]/[B]

[OH⁻] = √(Kb·C) → pOH = ½(pKb − log C) → pH = 14 − pOH

Example: 0.1 M NH₃, Kb=1.8×10⁻⁵ → [OH⁻]=√(1.8×10⁻⁶)=1.34×10⁻³ → pOH=2.87 → pH=11.13

06Conjugate Pairs & Ka · Kb = Kw

Every acid has a conjugate base (acid minus one H⁺) and vice versa. The stronger the acid, the weaker its conjugate base.

Conjugate Pair Relationship

Ka × Kb = Kw = 10⁻¹⁴

For a conjugate acid-base pair (e.g. CH₃COOH / CH₃COO⁻)

CH₃COOH1.8×10⁻⁵CH₃COO⁻5.6×10⁻¹⁰4.74 + 9.26 = 14
NH₄⁺5.6×10⁻¹⁰NH₃1.8×10⁻⁵9.26 + 4.74 = 14

💡 Relative Acid-Base Strength
Strong acid (large Ka) → weak conjugate base (small Kb). Example: HCl is a strong acid (Ka→∞) → Cl⁻ is an extremely weak base (essentially no base character). This is why NaCl solution is neutral — Cl⁻ does not hydrolyse.

07Ostwald's Dilution Law

Dilution Increases Dissociation

α = √(Ka/C)     Ka = Cα²/(1−α) ≈ Cα²

As C decreases (dilution), α increases → more dissociation

This is why: adding water to acetic acid increases its % dissociation, but decreases [H⁺] overall (fewer moles per unit volume despite higher α). Therefore pH increases (less acidic) on dilution.

⚠ JEE Trap: Dilution Increases α but Decreases [H⁺]
Students often say "diluting weak acid makes it more acidic because more dissociates." Wrong. α increases but [H⁺] = √(Ka·C) decreases with dilution. Both α and [H⁺] cannot increase simultaneously.

08Buffer Solutions & Henderson-Hasselbalch

A buffer resists pH change on addition of small amounts of strong acid or base. Acidic buffer = weak acid + its conjugate base (its salt with strong base).

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])

For basic buffers: pOH = pKb + log([salt]/[base])

  • [A⁻]/[HA] = 1 → log(1) = 0 → pH = pKa (best buffer point)
  • Buffer range: pH = pKa ± 1 (effective buffering region)
  • Buffer capacity: highest when [A⁻] = [HA] (equal moles)
Adding Strong Acid/Base to Buffer — Method
  1. Write the reaction: added acid reacts with A⁻ (conjugate base); added base reacts with HA (weak acid)
  2. Calculate new moles of HA and A⁻ after the reaction
  3. Apply Henderson-Hasselbalch with new moles (volume cancels if same volume)

Example: 0.1 mol CH₃COOH + 0.1 mol CH₃COONa in 1L (pKa=4.74). Add 0.01 mol HCl:

HCl + CH₃COO⁻ → CH₃COOH + Cl⁻  |  New: [HA]=0.11, [A⁻]=0.09

pH = 4.74 + log(0.09/0.11) = 4.74 − 0.087 = 4.65 (was 4.74 — very small change!)

09Salt Hydrolysis

When salts dissolve in water, the ions may react with water (hydrolyse) to make the solution acidic or basic.

💡 Hydrolysis Constant Kh
For A⁻ + H₂O ⇌ HA + OH⁻:   Kh = Kw/Ka
For BH⁺ + H₂O ⇌ B + H₃O⁺:   Kh = Kw/Kb
Larger Kh → stronger hydrolysis → more extreme pH deviation from 7.

⚠ Comparing Solubilities Using Ksp
You can only compare Ksp directly for salts with the SAME formula type (e.g. all MX salts). For different types, calculate s from the formula above and compare. AgCl (Ksp=1.8×10⁻¹⁰, s=1.34×10⁻⁵) vs Ag₂CrO₄ (Ksp=1.12×10⁻¹², 4s³ formula, s=6.54×10⁻⁵). Despite higher Ksp for AgCl, Ag₂CrO₄ is more soluble!

11Common Ion Effect

Adding an ion that is already part of the equilibrium shifts the solubility equilibrium backwards, reducing solubility. This is Le Chatelier's principle applied to Ksp.

Worked: Solubility of AgCl in 0.1 M NaCl

AgCl(s) ⇌ Ag⁺ + Cl⁻   Ksp = 1.8×10⁻¹⁰

NaCl provides [Cl⁻] = 0.1 M before AgCl dissolves.

Ag⁺Cl⁻
Initial00.1
Change+s+s
Equil.s0.1+s ≈ 0.1

Ksp = s × 0.1 = 1.8×10⁻¹⁰  →  s = 1.8×10⁻⁹ M

Compare: in pure water, s = √(1.8×10⁻¹⁰) = 1.34×10⁻⁵ M — common ion reduces solubility ~10,000×!

12Ionic Product vs Ksp — Precipitation

The ionic product (IP) is the product of ion concentrations raised to stoichiometric powers — same form as Ksp but at current (not equilibrium) concentrations.

Precipitation Rule
IP > Ksp
Precipitate forms (supersaturated → ions combine until equilibrium)
IP = Ksp
Saturated — at equilibrium, no more precipitate
IP < Ksp
Unsaturated — no precipitate; more salt can dissolve
💡 Mixing Problem — Remember Dilution
When two solutions are mixed, concentrations change due to dilution before any precipitation occurs. New concentration = (moles from each solution) / (total volume). Then compare IP to Ksp.

14Worked Examples

Example 1 — Weak Acid pH (Easy)

Q: Find pH of 0.1 M acetic acid. Ka = 1.8×10⁻⁵.

[H⁺] = √(1.8×10⁻⁵ × 0.1) = √(1.8×10⁻⁶) = 1.34×10⁻³ M

pH = −log(1.34×10⁻³) = 3 − log(1.34) = 3 − 0.127 = 2.87

Check α: 1.34×10⁻³/0.1 = 1.34% << 5% ✓ approximation valid

Example 2 — Back-calculating Ka from pH (Medium)

Q: pH of 0.1 M weak monobasic acid is 3. Find Ka and α.

[H⁺] = 10⁻³ M  |  Ka = [H⁺]²/C = (10⁻³)²/0.1 = 10⁻⁶/0.1 = Ka = 10⁻⁵

α = [H⁺]/C = 10⁻³/0.1 = α = 0.01 = 1%

Example 3 — Mg(OH)₂ Solubility & pH (Medium)

Q: Mg(OH)₂ Ksp = 1.8×10⁻¹¹. Find molar solubility and pH of saturated solution.

Mg(OH)₂ ⇌ Mg²⁺ + 2OH⁻  |  (s, 2s)  |  Ksp = s·(2s)² = 4s³

4s³ = 1.8×10⁻¹¹  →  s³ = 4.5×10⁻¹²  →  s = (4.5×10⁻¹²)^(1/3) = 1.65×10⁻⁴ M

[OH⁻] = 2s = 3.3×10⁻⁴  →  pOH = −log(3.3×10⁻⁴) = 3.48  →  pH = 10.52

Example 4 — NH₄Cl Salt Hydrolysis (Hard)

Q: Find pH of 0.1 M NH₄Cl. Kb(NH₃) = 1.8×10⁻⁵, Kw = 10⁻¹⁴.

NH₄⁺ is acidic. Ka(NH₄⁺) = Kw/Kb = 10⁻¹⁴/1.8×10⁻⁵ = 5.56×10⁻¹⁰

[H⁺] = √(Ka·C) = √(5.56×10⁻¹⁰ × 0.1) = √(5.56×10⁻¹¹) = 7.45×10⁻⁶

pH = −log(7.45×10⁻⁶) = 5.13 (acidic, as expected for salt of strong acid + weak base)

PYQ — JEE Main 2023

Q: The pH of a 0.1 M solution of NH₄OH (Kb = 1.8×10⁻⁵) is:

[OH⁻] = √(1.8×10⁻⁵ × 0.1) = √(1.8×10⁻⁶) = 1.34×10⁻³

pOH = 2.87 → pH = 14 − 2.87 = 11.13

PYQ — JEE Main 2022

Q: Ksp of BaSO₄ = 1.08×10⁻¹⁰. What is the molar solubility of BaSO₄ in pure water?

BaSO₄ ⇌ Ba²⁺ + SO₄²⁻  |  Ksp = s²  |  s = √(1.08×10⁻¹⁰) = 1.04×10⁻⁵ M

16Common Traps & Mistakes

❌ Trap 1: Using [H⁺]=√(Ka·C) when approximation is invalid
If Ka/C > 0.0025 (α > 5%), the approximation fails. For a 10⁻⁴ M solution with Ka=10⁻⁵, α=√(Ka/C)=√(0.1)=31.6% — must solve quadratic.
❌ Trap 2: Including water in Kw or Ksp expressions
[H₂O] is not included in Kw. [solid salt] is not included in Ksp. Only dissolved ionic species appear.
❌ Trap 3: Comparing Ksp directly for different salt types
AgCl (Ksp=1.8×10⁻¹⁰, type MX) has higher Ksp than Ag₂CrO₄ (Ksp=1.12×10⁻¹², type M₂X), but Ag₂CrO₄ is more soluble. Must calculate s from the formula for each type.
❌ Trap 4: Forgetting dilution when mixing solutions for IP test
If 100 mL of 0.001 M Ba²⁺ is mixed with 100 mL of 0.001 M SO₄²⁻, each concentration halves to 5×10⁻⁴ M. IP = (5×10⁻⁴)² = 2.5×10⁻⁷. Compare to Ksp(BaSO₄)=1.08×10⁻¹⁰ → IP >> Ksp → precipitate forms.
❌ Trap 5: pH = 7 means neutral (temperature dependent)
Neutral means [H⁺]=[OH⁻], which equals pH=7 only at 25°C. At higher temperatures, Kw increases so neutral pH < 7.

17One-Page Cheat Card

🧪 Ionic Equilibrium — Rapid Reference

pH Formulas

  • [H⁺] = √(Ka·C) → pH = ½(pKa − log C)
  • pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
  • Ka × Kb = 10⁻¹⁴  |  pH + pOH = 14
  • α = √(Ka/C)  |  valid when α < 5%

Salt Hydrolysis

  • Strong + Strong → neutral (NaCl)
  • Weak acid + Strong base → basic (CH₃COONa)
  • Strong acid + Weak base → acidic (NH₄Cl)
  • Kh = Kw/Ka or Kw/Kb

Ksp & Solubility

  • MX: s=√Ksp  |  MX₂: s=(Ksp/4)^(1/3)
  • Common ion → solubility decreases
  • IP > Ksp → precipitate forms
  • Dilute before comparing IP to Ksp

Key pKa Values

  • CH₃COOH: pKa = 4.74
  • NH₄⁺: pKa = 9.26 (pKb NH₃ = 4.74)
  • H₂CO₃: pKa₁ = 6.35, pKa₂ = 10.33
