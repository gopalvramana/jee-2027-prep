01Why This Unit Matters

Electrochemistry is one of the highest-density scoring units in JEE Chemistry. It packs four distinct sub-topics — galvanic cells, Nernst equation, Faraday's electrolysis, and conductance — each of which yields 1–2 independent questions. A student who masters all four reliably scores 6–8 marks.

✅ Four Independent Sub-Topics = Four Independent Mark Blocks
Even if one sub-topic confuses you, the other three still give marks. Study each as a separate unit with its own formula set. Unlike Organic where everything connects, electrochemistry sub-topics are largely independent.
📊 Connections to Other Units
Electrochemistry links thermochemistry (ΔG° = −nFE°), equilibrium (log K = nE°/0.0592), and ionic equilibrium (conductance → Ka). Mastering it reinforces C4 and C5 simultaneously.

02Redox Reactions — Quick Review

Oxidation
  • Loss of electrons (OIL — Oxidation Is Loss)
  • Oxidation state increases
  • Occurs at the anode in all electrochemical cells
  • Reducing agent is oxidised (loses electrons)
Reduction
  • Gain of electrons (RIG — Reduction Is Gain)
  • Oxidation state decreases
  • Occurs at the cathode in all electrochemical cells
  • Oxidising agent is reduced (gains electrons)
💡 Balancing Redox — Half-Reaction Method
Split the reaction into oxidation half-reaction and reduction half-reaction. Balance each for atoms (add H₂O for O, H⁺ for H), then balance for charge (add electrons). Multiply to equalise electrons, then add half-reactions. In basic solution, add OH⁻ to neutralise H⁺ at the end.

03Galvanic Cells — Parts & Notation

A galvanic (voltaic) cell converts spontaneous chemical energy into electrical energy. The Daniel cell (Zn-Cu) is the standard example.

Galvanic Cell Structure
Anode (−ve electrode)
  • Oxidation occurs here
  • Electrons leave from here → external circuit
  • Mass decreases (metal dissolves)
  • Anions from salt bridge migrate here
  • Daniel cell: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻
Cathode (+ve electrode)
  • Reduction occurs here
  • Electrons arrive here from external circuit
  • Mass increases (metal deposits)
  • Cations from salt bridge migrate here
  • Daniel cell: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
Cell Notation (IUPAC)

Anode | anode solution || cathode solution | Cathode

Zn(s) | Zn²⁺(aq) || Cu²⁺(aq) | Cu(s)

  • Single vertical bar | = phase boundary (solid/liquid interface)
  • Double vertical bar || = salt bridge (usually KCl or KNO₃ in agar)
  • Left side always = anode (oxidation); Right side always = cathode (reduction)
💡 Salt Bridge Function
The salt bridge completes the circuit by allowing ion migration without mixing the two electrode solutions. Anions (Cl⁻) migrate to the anode; cations (K⁺) migrate to the cathode — maintaining electrical neutrality in each half-cell.

04Standard Reduction Potentials

All electrode potentials are measured relative to the Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE): H⁺(1M) + e⁻ → ½H₂(g), E° = 0.00 V by definition.

💡 Reading the Table
Higher E° (more positive) → better oxidising agent (more easily reduced). Lower E° (more negative) → better reducing agent (more easily oxidised). The species at the bottom left of the table are the strongest reducing agents.

05E°cell Calculations

Standard Cell Potential

E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode

Both values are taken as standard reduction potentials (from the SRP table)

  • The electrode with higher E° (reduction potential) is the cathode
  • The electrode with lower E° is the anode
  • E°cell > 0 → spontaneous reaction → ΔG° < 0
  • E°cell < 0 → non-spontaneous

Daniel cell example: E°cell = E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) − E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = 0.34 − (−0.76) = 1.10 V

⚠ E° does NOT scale with coefficients
Standard electrode potential is an intensive property — it doesn't change when you multiply the half-reaction by 2, 3, etc. E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34 V whether you write Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu or 2Cu²⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2Cu. (ΔG° does scale, because ΔG° = −nFE° and n doubles.)

06ΔG°, K, and E° — The Three-Way Link

The Master Connection

ΔG° = −nFE°cell = −RT ln K

n = moles of electrons transferred  |  F = 96500 C/mol

> 0< 0> 1Forward reaction spontaneous ✅

< 0> 0< 1Non-spontaneous ❌ (reverse is spontaneous)
💡 Finding K from E°
From ΔG° = −nFE° and ΔG° = −RT ln K:

log K = nE° / 0.0592 (at 25°C)

Example: Daniel cell E° = 1.10 V, n=2 → log K = 2×1.10/0.0592 = 37.2 → K = 10^37.2 ≈ 1.6×10³⁷ (reaction essentially goes to completion)

07Nernst Equation

The Nernst equation gives the cell potential at non-standard concentrations:

Nernst Equation

E = E° − (RT/nF) ln Q  =  E° − (0.0592/n) log Q at 25°C

Q = reaction quotient (same form as Kc, using current concentrations/pressures)

⚡ Nernst Equation Calculator

For reaction: Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu (n=2, E°=1.10 V)

⚠ 0.0592 factor is ONLY at 25°C
The 0.0592/n factor comes from 2.303RT/F evaluated at 298 K. At any other temperature, use the full form: E = E° − (2.303RT/nF) log Q. JEE problems always specify 25°C when they want you to use 0.0592.

08Concentration Cells

A concentration cell uses the same electrode material and same electrolyte at different concentrations in each half-cell. E° = 0 (same electrodes), but E ≠ 0 because of concentration difference.

Concentration Cell EMF

E = (0.0592/n) × log([high]/[low]) at 25°C

The more concentrated half-cell is the cathode (reduction favoured when more ions available).

Example: Cu|Cu²⁺(0.01M)||Cu²⁺(1M)|Cu (n=2)

E = (0.0592/2) × log(1/0.01) = 0.0296 × 2 = 0.059 V

09Electrolysis vs Galvanic Cell

Galvanic Cell
  • Spontaneous redox reaction (ΔG < 0)
  • Chemical energy → electrical energy
  • Anode = −ve (electrons leave)
  • No external power source needed
  • Examples: batteries, fuel cells, Daniel cell
Electrolytic Cell
  • Non-spontaneous reaction (ΔG > 0)
  • Electrical energy → chemical energy
  • Anode = +ve (connected to +ve terminal)
  • External power source required
  • Examples: electroplating, electrolysis of water, NaCl electrolysis
⚠ Anode Sign Convention Trap
In BOTH cells, oxidation occurs at the anode and reduction at the cathode. However, the sign of the anode differs: in a galvanic cell, the anode is the negative electrode (electrons flow out); in an electrolytic cell, the anode is connected to the positive terminal of the battery (forces oxidation).

10Faraday's Laws — The Mole Chain

Faraday's Laws — Unified Formula

m = (M × I × t) / (n × F)

m = mass deposited (g)  |  M = molar mass (g/mol)  |  I = current (A)  |  t = time (s)

n = electrons per ion  |  F = 96500 C/mol

The Mole Chain — Always Follow This Sequence
  1. Find charge passed: Q = I × t (coulombs)
  2. Find moles of electrons: mol e⁻ = Q / F = Q / 96500
  3. Find moles of substance deposited: mol substance = mol e⁻ / n (electrons per ion)
  4. Find mass or volume: mass = mol × M  or  volume at STP = mol × 22.4 L

💡 Water Electrolysis Stoichiometry
2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂. Moles of H₂ produced = 2 × moles of O₂. For every 4 moles of electrons, 2 mol H₂ and 1 mol O₂ are produced. At cathode: 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂; at anode: 2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻.

Strong Electrolytes (e.g. KCl, NaCl)
  • Fully dissociated in solution
  • Λm increases slightly on dilution
  • Due to reduced interionic attraction at lower concentration
  • Debye-Hückel-Onsager equation: Λm = Λ°m − b√C
  • Λ°m found by extrapolation to zero concentration
Weak Electrolytes (e.g. CH₃COOH, NH₄OH)
  • Partially dissociated — α increases with dilution
  • Λm increases greatly on dilution (more ions formed)
  • Cannot extrapolate to find Λ°m — use Kohlrausch's law instead
  • α = Λm/Λ°m at given concentration
  • Ka = Cα²/(1−α) from measured Λm

13Kohlrausch's Law

Kohlrausch's Law of Independent Migration of Ions

Λ°m = ν₊λ°₊ + ν₋λ°₋

Λ°m = limiting molar conductivity (at infinite dilution)  |  λ° = ionic conductivity

Each ion contributes independently to the total conductance. This allows Λ°m of weak electrolytes (which can't be measured directly) to be calculated from strong electrolyte data.

Classic JEE Application — Λ°m(CH₃COOH)

Λ°m(CH₃COOH) cannot be found by extrapolation (conductance curve is too steep for weak acids).

Instead, use: Λ°m(CH₃COOH) = Λ°m(HCl) + Λ°m(CH₃COONa) − Λ°m(NaCl)

Proof: HCl contributes (λ°H⁺ + λ°Cl⁻); CH₃COONa contributes (λ°CH₃COO⁻ + λ°Na⁺); NaCl contributes (λ°Na⁺ + λ°Cl⁻). Subtracting NaCl cancels Na⁺ and Cl⁻, leaving (λ°H⁺ + λ°CH₃COO⁻) = Λ°m(CH₃COOH).

With given values: 426 + 91 − 126 = 391 S·cm²/mol

💡 Finding Ka from Conductance
At concentration C: α = Λm/Λ°m → Ka = Cα²/(1−α). This is how Ka of weak acids is determined experimentally. JEE sometimes gives conductance data and asks for Ka.

15Worked Examples

Example 1 — E°cell and ΔG° (Easy)

Q: E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = −0.76 V, E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34 V. Find E°cell and ΔG°.

Zn is the anode (lower E°); Cu is the cathode. n = 2 electrons.

E°cell = 0.34 − (−0.76) = 1.10 V

ΔG° = −nFE° = −2 × 96500 × 1.10 = −212,300 J = −212.3 kJ/mol

Example 2 — Nernst Equation (Medium)

Q: Find EMF of Daniel cell when [Zn²⁺] = 0.1 M and [Cu²⁺] = 0.01 M at 25°C. E° = 1.10 V.

Reaction: Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu  |  Q = [Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺] = 0.1/0.01 = 10

E = 1.10 − (0.0592/2) × log(10) = 1.10 − 0.0296 × 1 = 1.070 V

Example 3 — Faraday's Law (Medium)

Q: 2A flows through CuSO₄ solution for 30 min. Find mass of Cu deposited. (MCu = 63.5, n = 2)

Step 1: Q = I × t = 2 × 30 × 60 = 3600 C

Step 2: mol e⁻ = 3600/96500 = 0.0373 mol

Step 3: mol Cu = 0.0373/2 = 0.01866 mol (n=2 for Cu²⁺)

Step 4: mass = 0.01866 × 63.5 = 1.185 g

Example 4 — Kohlrausch + Ka (Hard)

Q: Conductivity of 0.1 M CH₃COOH = 0.0041 S/m. Λ°m(CH₃COOH) = 391 S·cm²/mol. Find Ka.

Convert κ: 0.0041 S/m = 0.041 S/cm (×100 since 1m = 100cm... wait: S/m × 0.01 = S/cm) → 0.0041 S/m = 0.000041 S/cm

Λm = κ×1000/C = 0.000041 × 1000 / 0.1 = 0.41 S·cm²/mol

Wait — more carefully: κ = 0.0041 S/m = 4.1×10⁻³ S/m = 4.1×10⁻⁵ S/cm; C = 0.1 mol/L

Λm = (4.1×10⁻⁵ S/cm × 1000 cm³/L) / 0.1 mol/L = 0.41 S·cm²/mol

α = Λm/Λ°m = 0.41/391 = 0.00105 = 0.105%

Ka = Cα²/(1−α) ≈ Cα² = 0.1 × (0.00105)² = 1.1×10⁻⁷

Note: textbooks often use κ in S/cm directly; always check units carefully.

16JEE Previous-Year Questions

📋 Most Tested Electrochemistry Patterns
  1. E°cell from standard reduction potentials; identify anode/cathode
  2. Nernst equation: find E at given concentrations
  3. Find K from E° (log K = nE°/0.0592)
  4. Faraday's law: mass deposited or gas evolved given I and t
  5. Kohlrausch's law: find Λ°m of weak electrolyte from three strong electrolytes
  6. Best reducing/oxidising agent from SRP table
PYQ — JEE Main 2023

Q: 5A current is passed for 1 hour through AgNO₃ solution. Find mass of Ag deposited. (MAg=108, n=1)

Q = 5 × 3600 = 18000 C  |  mol e⁻ = 18000/96500 = 0.1865  |  mol Ag = 0.1865 (n=1)  |  mass = 0.1865 × 108 = 20.1 g

PYQ — JEE Main 2022

Q: For the cell Zn|Zn²⁺(0.1M)||Cu²⁺(0.01M)|Cu at 25°C, E°cell = 1.10 V. The cell potential E is:

Q = [Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺] = 0.1/0.01 = 10  |  E = 1.10 − (0.0592/2)log(10) = 1.10 − 0.0296 = 1.07 V

17Common Traps & Mistakes

❌ Trap 1: E°cell = E°anode + E°cathode (reversing the anode sign)
The correct formula is E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode, where BOTH are taken as reduction potentials. A common mistake is to flip the anode sign and add: this is equivalent to E°cell = E°(red,cathode) + E°(ox,anode) = E°(red,cathode) − E°(red,anode). The result is the same, but students often double-flip and get wrong answers.
❌ Trap 2: Multiplying E° by stoichiometric factor
Doubling a half-reaction does NOT double E°. E° is an intensive property (like temperature — doubling a hot body doesn't make it twice as hot). However, n doubles, so ΔG° = −nFE° does double.
❌ Trap 3: Using minutes instead of seconds in Faraday's law
Q = I × t requires t in seconds. 30 minutes = 1800 seconds. This error multiplies the answer by 60 — very costly.
❌ Trap 4: Anode is positive in galvanic cell
In a galvanic cell, the anode is the NEGATIVE electrode (electrons flow out to external circuit). In an electrolytic cell, the anode is connected to the positive terminal. Same chemistry, different sign labelling.
❌ Trap 5: Units in molar conductance
Λm = κ × 1000/C requires κ in S/cm and C in mol/L. If κ is given in S/m, divide by 100 to convert to S/cm (not multiply). SI unit confusion is the #1 source of errors in conductance numericals.
