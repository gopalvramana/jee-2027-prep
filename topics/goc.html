01Why GOC is the Foundation

Organic Chemistry is 20–25% of the JEE Chemistry paper — the single largest topic. Unlike Physical Chemistry where each unit is somewhat independent, all of Organic builds on GOC. You cannot predict a reaction product in Phase 3 without understanding electronic effects (inductive, resonance) and mechanism types (SN1, SN2, E1, E2, addition, EAS).

⚠ Everything Downstream Depends on This Unit
Named reactions, carbonyl chemistry, amines, polymers, biomolecules — every Phase 3 Organic topic will ask "why does this reaction happen?" and the answer will always be one of the effects or mechanisms covered here. Rushing through GOC costs marks in every Organic question.
✅ The GOC Mental Model
Every organic reaction question follows: (1) What are the electronic conditions? (Nucleophile/electrophile, lone pairs, π electrons) → (2) What mechanism is triggered? (SN1/SN2/E1/E2/addition/EAS) → (3) What is the major product? (Stability of intermediate determines regiochemistry). Train this three-step thinking and Organic becomes a decision tree, not memorisation.

02Bond Cleavage — Homolytic vs Heterolytic

Homolytic Cleavage
  • Each atom gets one electron from the bond pair
  • Produces free radicals (uncharged, one unpaired electron)
  • Happens under UV light, heat, or with radical initiators (peroxides ROOR)
  • R–X → R• + X•
  • Governs radical reactions (anti-Markovnikov HBr addition, halogenation of alkanes)
Heterolytic Cleavage
  • One atom gets both electrons from the bond
  • Produces ions (carbocations + anions, or carbanions + cations)
  • Happens in polar conditions, polar solvents, with ionic reagents
  • R–X → R⁺ + X⁻ (if X is electronegative)
  • Governs ionic mechanisms: SN1, SN2, E1, E2, electrophilic addition

03Inductive Effect (I)

The inductive effect is the transmission of electron density through the σ-bond framework due to electronegativity differences. It decreases rapidly with distance (negligible beyond 3 bonds).

−I Effect (Electron Withdrawing)
  • Groups more electronegative than carbon
  • Pull electron density away from carbon chain
  • Increasing −I: F > OH > Cl > Br > I > C≡N > NO₂ > COOH > halogen
  • Effect: increases acidity of adjacent COOH (stabilises carboxylate A⁻)
  • Effect: decreases basicity of adjacent NH₂
  • Destabilises carbocations
+I Effect (Electron Donating)
  • Groups less electronegative than carbon (alkyl groups)
  • Push electron density toward carbon chain
  • Increasing +I: (CH₃)₃C > (CH₃)₂CH > C₂H₅ > CH₃ > H
  • Effect: decreases acidity (destabilises carboxylate by adding electrons)
  • Effect: increases basicity of adjacent NH₂
  • Stabilises carbocations (explains 3° > 2° > 1°)
Acidity from −I Effect — Worked Pattern

More −I substituents → more stable carboxylate anion → stronger acid (larger Ka, smaller pKa)

04Resonance / Mesomeric Effect (M)

The mesomeric effect (also called resonance effect) involves delocalisation of electrons through a π system or through lone pairs conjugated with a π system. It operates through bonds (not just through space) and is generally stronger than inductive effect when both are present.

+M Groups (Electron Donors)
  • Push lone pair electrons into π system
  • −NH₂, −NR₂, −OH, −OR, −OCOR
  • Halogens: −F, −Cl, −Br, −I (weak +M, also have −I)
  • Increase electron density at ortho and para positions (EAS → o/p director)
  • Increase basicity of the group itself (lone pair delocalised → less available)
  • Aniline: −NH₂ lone pair delocalised → less basic than cyclohexylamine
−M Groups (Electron Withdrawers)
  • Pull π electrons toward the group
  • −NO₂, −CN, −CHO, −COR, −COOH, −COOR, −SO₃H
  • Decrease electron density at ortho and para → meta director in EAS
  • Stabilise carbanions (withdraw electrons from adjacent carbanion)
  • Increase acidity: phenol > cyclohexanol (phenoxide O⁻ delocalised by benzene ring)
💡 Resonance > Inductive when both are present
For −NH₂ attached to a benzene ring: −I of N (slight) is dominated by +M. For −NO₂: strong −M dominates. When deciding the overall electronic effect of a substituent, resonance (M) usually wins over inductive (I) — except halogens where the balance gives an unusual result (−I deactivates, but +M still directs ortho/para).

05Hyperconjugation

Hyperconjugation is the delocalisation of σ C–H (or C–C) bonding electrons into an adjacent empty p orbital (carbocation), π* orbital (double bond), or radical orbital. It requires the C–H bond and the p/π system to be in the correct geometry (roughly parallel).

Hyperconjugation and Carbocation Stability

More α-hydrogen atoms → more hyperconjugative structures → more stable carbocation

  • CH₃⁺: 0 α-H atoms, 0 hyperconjugation structures → least stable
  • CH₃CH₂⁺: 3 α-H → 3 hyperconjugation structures
  • (CH₃)₂CH⁺: 6 α-H → 6 hyperconjugation structures
  • (CH₃)₃C⁺: 9 α-H → 9 hyperconjugation structures → most stable tertiary carbocation

Number of hyperconjugating structures = number of α-C-H bonds (Baker-Nathan rule)

💡 Hyperconjugation Also Explains Alkene Stability
More substituted alkenes are more stable: CH₂=CH₂ < CH₃CH=CH₂ < (CH₃)₂C=CH₂ < (CH₃)₂C=CHCH₃. Each additional alkyl group adds hyperconjugating C–H bonds. This is why Zaitsev's rule (more substituted alkene = major product) works.

06Acidity & Basicity from Electronic Effects

Golden Rule: Stability of Conjugate Base = Acidity of Acid

The more stable the anion (conjugate base), the stronger the acid. Factors that stabilise the anion increase acidity.

07Carbocations — Stability & Structure

A carbocation (carbonium ion) is a carbon with only 3 bonds and a positive formal charge. It is sp² hybridised with an empty p orbital perpendicular to the plane.

Stability Order (Most → Least Stable)

Allyl ≈ Benzyl > 3° > 2° > 1° > Vinyl ≈ Methyl ≈ Phenyl

  • Allyl (CH₂=CH–CH₂⁺): resonance with π bond → two equivalent structures; very stable
  • Benzyl (C₆H₅CH₂⁺): resonance with benzene ring (5 structures) → very stable
  • 3° (R₃C⁺): 3 alkyl groups give +I and 9 hyperconjugating C–H bonds
  • 2° (R₂HC⁺): 6 hyperconjugating C–H bonds
  • 1° (RH₂C⁺): 3 hyperconjugating C–H bonds
  • Vinyl (CH₂=CH⁺), Phenyl (C₆H₅⁺): sp² carbon cannot stabilise positive charge — very unstable

08Carbanions — Stability (Opposite Order!)

A carbanion carries a negative charge on carbon (lone pair). Stability requires withdrawing electron density from the carbanion centre — opposite logic to carbocations.

Carbanion Stability Order (Most → Least Stable)

sp > sp² (vinyl, aryl) > Benzyl ≈ Allyl > 1° > 2° > 3° > Methyl

  • sp carbanion (HC≡C⁻): sp orbital has 50% s-character → electrons held close to nucleus → most stable; terminal alkynes are surprisingly acidic (pKa≈25)
  • Benzyl/Allyl: negative charge delocalised by resonance — stable despite being "extended" carbon
  • 1° > 2° > 3°: alkyl groups (+I) destabilise the negative charge; fewer alkyl groups = more stable carbanion
  • Electron-withdrawing groups (−I, −M) stabilise carbanions — e.g. malonates, β-keto esters used in synthesis because EWG stabilises carbanion on α-carbon
⚠ JEE Trap: Carbanion is OPPOSITE to Carbocation for Alkyl Groups
Carbocation: 3° > 2° > 1° (alkyl +I stabilises positive charge). Carbanion: 1° > 2° > 3° (alkyl +I destabilises negative charge). This reversal is tested every few years.

09Free Radicals

A free radical has an odd number of electrons — one unpaired electron. Like carbocations, radicals are sp² hybridised with a singly-occupied p orbital.

Radical Stability Order — Same as Carbocation

Allyl ≈ Benzyl > 3° > 2° > 1° > Methyl

  • Alkyl groups stabilise radicals by hyperconjugation (+I contributes less than for carbocations)
  • Allylic/benzylic radicals are resonance-stabilised — most stable
  • Vinyl/phenyl radicals are unstable (unpaired electron in sp² orbital)
💡 Radical Reactions: Initiated by Light or Peroxides
Radical chain reactions have three phases: Initiation (ROOR → 2RO• by UV/heat), Propagation (RO• + H–R' → ROH + R'•; R'• + X₂ → R'X + X•), Termination (radical + radical → stable molecule). Anti-Markovnikov HBr addition follows this chain.

⚠ Nucleophilicity vs Basicity in Protic Solvents
In polar protic solvents (water, alcohol), nucleophilicity ≠ basicity. Large, polarisable ions are better nucleophiles even if weaker bases because they are less solvated: I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻ (nucleophilicity in protic). In polar aprotic solvents, Nu⁻ are not solvated, so nucleophilicity ≈ basicity: F⁻ > Cl⁻ > Br⁻ > I⁻.

Zaitsev vs Hofmann — When Each Applies
Zaitsev Product (More Substituted Alkene)
  • Small base (NaOH, KOH, NaOEt)
  • More substituted alkene is thermodynamically more stable
  • More hyperconjugation → lower energy alkene
  • Example: 2-bromobutane + KOH → but-2-ene (major) + but-1-ene (minor)
Hofmann Product (Less Substituted Alkene)
  • Bulky base (t-BuOK, LDA)
  • Bulky base cannot approach crowded β-H → attacks less hindered H
  • Kinetically controlled — less stable but more accessible product
  • Example: 2-bromobutane + t-BuOK → but-1-ene (major)

💡 Practical Rules
  • 1° substrate + strong Nu + polar aprotic → SN2
  • 3° substrate + weak Nu + polar protic + heat → SN1 + E1
  • 3° substrate + strong base → E2 (SN2 too hindered)
  • 2° substrate + strong base + polar aprotic → SN2 and/or E2 (competition)

13Electrophilic Addition — Markovnikov's Rule

Alkenes undergo addition of electrophiles (H⁺, X₂, X–Y) across the C=C double bond. The mechanism goes through a carbocation (or bromonium ion) intermediate.

Markovnikov's Rule

H adds to the carbon that already has more hydrogens (equivalently: the electrophile adds to give the more stable carbocation intermediate).

Propene + HBr: H⁺ adds to CH₂= (C1, has 2H) → secondary carbocation on C2 (more stable) → Br⁻ attacks C2 → 2-bromopropane (not 1-bromopropane)

14Anti-Markovnikov & Other Addition Modes

Anti-Markovnikov HBr Addition (Peroxide Effect)

Condition: HBr + ROOR (peroxide) or UV light

Mechanism: Radical chain — Br• adds first (not H⁺)

Why Br• adds to less substituted C: Gives the more stable secondary radical (not primary)

Product: Br on C with fewer H (anti-Markovnikov) → 1-bromopropane from propene

Only HBr shows this effect. HCl and HI do NOT show anti-Markovnikov addition.

⚠ Peroxide Effect Only for HBr — Not HCl or HI
HCl: Cl• addition is endothermic → radical chain won't propagate. HI: I• is too stable (weak bond formed) → termination faster than propagation. Only HBr has the right balance of bond strengths for radical chain addition.

15Aromaticity — Hückel's Rule

Hückel's Criteria for Aromaticity
  1. Cyclic (all atoms in the ring)
  2. Planar
  3. Fully conjugated (continuous π system around the ring — every atom must have a p orbital)
  4. (4n + 2) π electrons where n = 0, 1, 2, 3… (2, 6, 10, 14, 18…)

Anti-aromatic: cyclic, planar, conjugated but 4n π electrons (4, 8, 12…). More destabilised than non-conjugated structure.

16EAS — Mechanism & Directing Effects

Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS): an electrophile (E⁺) attacks the benzene ring, forming a σ-complex (arenium ion, Wheland intermediate), which loses H⁺ to restore aromaticity.

EAS General Mechanism (2 Steps)
  1. Slow (rate-determining): E⁺ attacks ring → arenium ion (σ-complex, cyclohexadienyl cation) — aromaticity lost, sp³ carbon formed
  2. Fast: Base removes H⁺ from sp³ carbon → restores aromaticity and π system

Note: Addition (not substitution) gives the arenium ion. The second step is elimination of H⁺ (not addition of nucleophile) — key difference from alkene addition.

ReactionElectrophile generatedConditions
NitrationNO₂⁺ (nitronium ion)Conc. HNO₃ + Conc. H₂SO₄ (mixed acid)
HalogenationBr⁺ or Cl⁺ (via X₂ + Lewis acid)Br₂/FeBr₃ or Cl₂/AlCl₃
SulfonationSO₃ or HSO₃⁺Fuming H₂SO₄ (oleum)
Friedel-Crafts alkylationR⁺ (carbocation)RCl + AlCl₃ (Lewis acid)
Friedel-Crafts acylationRCO⁺ (acylium ion)RCOCl + AlCl₃

⚠ The Halogen Paradox — Most Tested EAS Trap
Halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) DEACTIVATE the ring (−I effect withdraws electrons → ring is less reactive than benzene) BUT still direct ortho/para (+M lone pair donation stabilises o/p arenium ion more than meta). So the ring reacts more slowly than benzene, but the product is still predominantly ortho/para. Never confuse activation with direction.

18Worked Examples

Example 1 — Acidity Order (Easy)

Q: Arrange in increasing acidity: CH₃COOH, FCH₂COOH, F₂CHCOOH, F₃CCOOH.

More F atoms = stronger −I effect = more stable COO⁻ = stronger acid.

Answer: CH₃COOH < FCH₂COOH < F₂CHCOOH < F₃CCOOH

(pKa: 4.74 > 2.59 > 1.24 > 0.23)

Example 2 — Mechanism Prediction (Medium)

Q: (CH₃)₃CBr reacts with H₂O. Predict mechanism and product.

Analysis: 3° substrate + weak nucleophile (H₂O) + polar protic solvent → SN1

Step 1: (CH₃)₃CBr → (CH₃)₃C⁺ + Br⁻ (slow; stable 3° carbocation)

Step 2: (CH₃)₃C⁺ + H₂O → (CH₃)₃COH₂⁺ → (CH₃)₃COH + H⁺ (fast)

Stereochemistry: Racemisation (flat sp² carbocation attacked from both faces)

Example 3 — Directing Effects (Medium)

Q: Nitration of toluene — predict major product.

–CH₃ is an ortho/para director (+I, hyperconjugation) and mildly activating.

Major products: 2-nitrotoluene (ortho) and 4-nitrotoluene (para).

In practice, para predominates due to steric hindrance at ortho.

Answer: p-nitrotoluene is the major product

Example 4 — Carbocation Stability + Rearrangement (Hard)

Q: Neopentyl bromide ((CH₃)₃CCH₂Br) undergoes SN1 slowly. The product is not neopentyl alcohol but 2-methyl-2-butanol. Explain.

Step 1: (CH₃)₃CCH₂Br → (CH₃)₃CCH₂⁺ (primary carbocation — very unstable)

Step 2: Hydride shift: CH₃ migrates from C2 to C1 → (CH₃)₂C⁺CH(CH₃) (tertiary carbocation — stable!)

Step 3: H₂O attacks tertiary carbocation → 2-methyl-2-butanol

This is a 1,2-hydride (or methyl) shift — always occurs to form more stable carbocation. Only possible in SN1, not SN2.

PYQ — JEE Main 2023

Q: Which of the following is the most stable carbocation?

(A) CH₃CH₂⁺   (B) CH₂=CHCH₂⁺   (C) (CH₃)₃C⁺   (D) C₆H₅CH₂⁺

Analysis: A = primary; B = allyl (resonance); C = tertiary; D = benzyl (5 resonance structures with benzene ring)

Answer: D (benzyl) ≥ B (allyl) > C (3°) > A (1°)

Benzyl has 5 resonance structures; allyl has 2. Both are more stable than 3°.

PYQ — JEE Main 2022

Q: The compound that does NOT show positive Baeyer's test (KMnO₄) is:

(A) Propene   (B) Propyne   (C) Propane   (D) Propenyl benzene

Answer: C (propane) — Propane has no C=C or C≡C, so no oxidation by cold KMnO₄. All others have unsaturation and are oxidised (decolourise purple KMnO₄).

20Common Traps & Mistakes

❌ Trap 1: Carbanion stability = carbocation stability (they're opposite)
Carbocation: 3° > 2° > 1° (alkyl donates electrons = stabilises +). Carbanion: 1° > 2° > 3° (alkyl donates electrons = destabilises −). Mixing up these orders is the #1 GOC error.
❌ Trap 2: Halogens deactivate AND direct meta
Halogens deactivate the ring (−I) but are ortho/para directors (+M wins for direction). They do NOT direct meta. Students confuse "deactivating" with "meta-directing" — only groups that withdraw by −M only (no +M) direct meta.
❌ Trap 3: Peroxide effect works for HCl and HI too
The peroxide/anti-Markovnikov effect is ONLY for HBr. HCl radical addition is endothermic (Cl• propagation step); HI radical addition is irreversible in wrong direction. Never apply peroxide effect to HCl or HI.
❌ Trap 4: SN2 with 3° substrate is possible
SN2 is essentially impossible with 3° substrates due to steric hindrance — the nucleophile cannot approach the back lobe of the C–X bond. 3° always goes SN1 (or E2 with strong base). Writing SN2 for (CH₃)₃CBr is wrong.
❌ Trap 5: Pyridine N lone pair is in the π system
In pyridine, the lone pair on N is in an sp² orbital in the plane of the ring — it is NOT part of the π system and is available for protonation (pyridine is a base). In pyrrole, the N lone pair IS part of the 6π aromatic system and is NOT available for protonation (pyrrole is a very weak base).
❌ Trap 6: More substituted alkene = more stable (not always the major E2 product)
With bulky base (t-BuOK), the less substituted alkene (Hofmann product) is major. More stable alkene = Zaitsev, but only with small base. With bulky base, kinetics (accessibility of β-H) dominates thermodynamics.

21One-Page Cheat Card

🔬 GOC — Rapid Reference

Stability Orders

  • Carbocation & Radical: Bn ≈ Allyl > 3° > 2° > 1°
  • Carbanion: sp > sp² > Bn ≈ Allyl > 1° > 2° > 3°
  • Acidity ↑ with −I; Basicity ↑ with +I

SN1 vs SN2

  • SN1: 3°, weak Nu, protic, racemisation
  • SN2: 1°(best), strong Nu, aprotic, inversion
  • LG ability: I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻

Elimination

  • E2: strong base, anti-periplanar H required
  • Zaitsev: small base → more sub. alkene
  • Hofmann: bulky base (t-BuOK) → less sub.

EAS Directors

  • o/p activating: −NH₂, −OH, −OR, −alkyl
  • o/p deactivating: −F, −Cl, −Br, −I (trap!)
  • meta deactivating: −NO₂, −CN, −CHO, −COOH
  • Hückel: 4n+2 π e⁻ → aromatic
