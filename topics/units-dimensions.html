01Why Units & Dimensions Matter

Every formula in physics is a sentence in a language whose grammar is dimensions. If a formula is dimensionally wrong, it cannot be physically right — full stop. JEE rewards this knowledge in three concrete ways:

  1. 1–2 direct questions per paper on dimensional formulas, error analysis, or unit conversion. Free marks if you've practised.
  2. Formula recovery. Forgot the time period of a pendulum mid-exam? Reconstruct it dimensionally in under a minute.
  3. Answer sanity check. Out of four MCQ options, one is dimensionally absurd — knock it out before you compute.
💰 Highest ROI in the syllabus
About 6 hours of practice unlocks ~4 marks/paper for the rest of your life as a JEE student. No other Physics topic gives this return per hour invested.

02SI Base Units & Prefixes

Every measurement in physics reduces to seven base quantities. Memorise these three columns — name, symbol, and SI unit:

QuantitySymbolSI UnitSymbol
Length[L]metrem
Mass[M]kilogramkg
Time[T]seconds
Electric current[A]ampereA
Thermodynamic temperature[K]kelvinK
Amount of substance[mol]molemol
Luminous intensity[cd]candelacd

Two supplementary (dimensionless) quantities

  • Plane angle — radian (rad). Defined as arc/radius.
  • Solid angle — steradian (sr). Defined as area/radius².

Both are dimensionless ratios. JEE loves to ask "what are the dimensions of an angle?" — the answer is [M⁰L⁰T⁰], i.e. a pure number.

SI prefixes — know all twenty

Prefixes are powers of 10 that scale a base unit. JEE-Main occasionally asks "1 femtometre = how many metres?" or "express 250 GW in MW." Memorise the powers, not flashcards.

Larger than 1 deca (da) 10¹ · hecto (h) 10² · kilo (k) 10³ · mega (M) 10⁶ · giga (G) 10⁹ · tera (T) 10¹² · peta (P) 10¹⁵ · exa (E) 10¹⁸
Smaller than 1 deci (d) 10⁻¹ · centi (c) 10⁻² · milli (m) 10⁻³ · micro (μ) 10⁻⁶ · nano (n) 10⁻⁹ · pico (p) 10⁻¹² · femto (f) 10⁻¹⁵ · atto (a) 10⁻¹⁸
⚠ Capital matters
M = mega (10⁶), m = milli (10⁻³). The capital/lowercase distinction is twelve orders of magnitude. JEE has misled students with "Mm" (megametre) vs "mm" (millimetre).

03Dimensional Formulas — How To Build One

A dimensional formula expresses any physical quantity in terms of the seven base dimensions. The notation is square brackets around M, L, T, A, K raised to whatever powers.

🎯 The 3-step derivation method
  1. Write the defining equation in terms of more basic quantities.
  2. Substitute each quantity's dimensional formula.
  3. Combine exponents — every M, L, T, A, K combines via normal exponent algebra.

Worked derivations

① Velocity

Definition: v = displacement / time. Dimensions: [L]/[T] = [LT⁻¹].

② Force (Newton's 2nd law)

Definition: F = m · a. Dimensions: [M] · [LT⁻²] = [MLT⁻²].

③ Work / Energy

Definition: W = F · d. Dimensions: [MLT⁻²] · [L] = [ML²T⁻²].

④ Pressure

Definition: P = F / A. Dimensions: [MLT⁻²]/[L²] = [ML⁻¹T⁻²].

⑤ Universal Gravitational Constant G

From F = G·m₁m₂/r² ⇒ G = F·r²/(m₁m₂). Dimensions: [MLT⁻²]·[L²]/[M²] = [M⁻¹L³T⁻²].

⑥ Planck's constant h

From E = hν ⇒ h = E/ν. Frequency has dimensions [T⁻¹]. So h: [ML²T⁻²]/[T⁻¹] = [ML²T⁻¹]. Same as angular momentum.

📌 Drill
Pick any 10 formulas you can remember (kinetic energy, capacitance, viscosity, etc.) and derive their dimensions. After 30 reps, the algebra becomes automatic and the table at the end of this guide is just a sanity check.

04The Three Uses of Dimensional Analysis

Use 1 — Verify a formula (necessary but not sufficient)

Any equation must have identical dimensions on both sides. If they differ, the equation is wrong. If they match, it might be right (you can't catch dimensionless errors like a missing factor of 2π).

Example: Is v² = u² + 2as dimensionally correct?

  • LHS: v² → [LT⁻¹]² = [L²T⁻²].
  • RHS term 1: u² → [L²T⁻²]. ✓
  • RHS term 2: 2as → [LT⁻²]·[L] = [L²T⁻²]. ✓

All three terms have dimensions [L²T⁻²] → the formula is dimensionally consistent.

Use 2 — Derive a formula from variables it depends on

If you know which quantities a result depends on, you can guess the formula up to a dimensionless constant.

🎯 Derivation procedure
  1. Write y = k · a^p · b^q · c^r where k is dimensionless.
  2. Substitute dimensional formulas of a, b, c, y.
  3. Equate powers of M, L, T separately → 3 equations.
  4. Solve for p, q, r.

Classic JEE example: Derive the time period T of a simple pendulum, given that it depends only on length L and gravity g.

Set T = k · L^p · g^q. Dimensions:

[T¹] = [L]^p · [LT⁻²]^q = [L^(p+q) · T^(−2q)].

Equate: −2q = 1 ⇒ q = −1/2. p + q = 0 ⇒ p = 1/2.

So T = k · √(L/g). Dimensional analysis gives the form; the famous 2π comes from the actual physics (oscillation theory).

Use 3 — Convert between unit systems

To convert a quantity from one system to another, multiply by the ratio of their dimensional formulas.

Example: Convert 1 J (SI) into ergs (CGS).

Energy has dimensions [ML²T⁻²]. So:

1 J = 1 kg · 1 m² · 1 s⁻²
= (10³ g) · (10² cm)² · (1 s)⁻² = 10⁷ g·cm²·s⁻² 1 J = 10⁷ erg

05Limitations & Same-Dimension Traps

Dimensional analysis is powerful but blind in three specific ways:

  1. Cannot find dimensionless constants. The 2π, ½, 3 in real physical formulas come from the actual derivation — dimensions can't see them.
  2. Cannot derive formulas with sums or trig. If y = a + b·sin(ωt), dimensional analysis can only tell you a, b, y match — not the form of the trig argument.
  3. Cannot distinguish quantities with the same dimensions. The famous traps:
Same DimensionsGroup AGroup B
[ML²T⁻²]Energy / WorkTorque · Heat · ½kx²
[ML²T⁻¹]Angular momentumPlanck's constant h
[T⁻¹]FrequencyAngular velocity · Decay constant · Velocity gradient
[ML⁻¹T⁻²]PressureStress · Young's modulus · Energy density
[L]WavelengthDisplacement · Radius · Path length
[M⁰L⁰T⁰]StrainRefractive index · Angle · Coefficient of friction · Relative density
🛑 The torque–energy trap
Both have dimensions [ML²T⁻²]. JEE asks "which has different dimensions?" and includes torque among the energy-like options. They're physically different (torque is a vector, energy is a scalar) but dimensionally identical. Read the question carefully — the answer is often "they all have the same dimensions" rather than picking the odd one out.

06Significant Figures

Significant figures encode how precisely a number was measured. JEE expects you to know the rules cold.

Counting rules

  • All non-zero digits are significant. 123.45 → 5 sig figs.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant. 10045 → 5 sig figs.
  • Leading zeros (before the first non-zero digit) are not significant. 0.0023 → 2 sig figs.
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal are significant. 2.300 → 4 sig figs.
  • Trailing zeros without a decimal are ambiguous. 2300 → 2, 3, or 4 sig figs depending on context. Use scientific notation to avoid ambiguity: 2.30 × 10³ = 3 sig figs.

Arithmetic rules

Addition / subtraction Round result to the same number of decimal places as the term with the fewest.
12.34 + 1.2 = 13.5 (not 13.54).
Multiplication / division Round result to the same number of significant figures as the value with the fewest.
2.34 × 1.5 = 3.5 (not 3.51).
⚠ Don't round mid-calculation
Carry one or two extra digits through every step, round only at the end. Premature rounding propagates and amplifies error.

07Errors in Measurement

Types of error

  • Systematic errors — consistent, one-sided. Caused by a faulty instrument (zero error), poor calibration, or a flawed method. Reduced by checking and recalibrating.
  • Random errors — fluctuating, unpredictable. Caused by judgement variation, environmental fluctuation. Reduced by repeating the measurement many times and averaging.
  • Gross errors — flat-out human mistakes (wrong reading, miscount). Spotted by re-checking.

Quantifying error

For a quantity measured n times giving readings a₁, a₂, ..., aₙ:

Mean: ā = (a₁ + a₂ + ... + aₙ) / n
Absolute error of i-th reading: |Δaᵢ| = |aᵢ − ā|
Mean absolute error: Δā = (|Δa₁| + |Δa₂| + ... + |Δaₙ|) / n
Relative error = Δā / ā    Percentage error = (Δā / ā) × 100%

Reporting a measurement

Always report as a = ā ± Δā. The plus-minus is part of the answer, not optional.

08Error Propagation Rules

When you compute a result from measured quantities, each measurement's error contributes to the result's error. Memorise these four rules — they cover every JEE error question.

Rule 1 — Sum or difference

If z = a ± b, absolute errors add:

Δz = Δa + Δb Absolute errors add — even for subtraction!

Rule 2 — Product or quotient

If z = a · b or z = a / b, relative errors add:

Δz/z = Δa/a + Δb/b

Rule 3 — Power

If z = aⁿ, relative error is multiplied by the power:

Δz/z = |n| · Δa/a

Rule 4 — Combined formula

For z = a^p · b^q / c^r:

Δz/z = |p|·Δa/a + |q|·Δb/b + |r|·Δc/c All contributions enter as positive (worst case)
⚠ Subtraction amplifies relative error
Sum/difference adds absolute errors. But if a and b are close, the result a − b is small while Δa + Δb stays the same — so the relative error (Δa+Δb)/(a−b) can blow up. Avoid measurement designs that subtract two similar large numbers.

Practice — common JEE style

For x = a²b/√c with errors a: 2%, b: 3%, c: 4%:

Apply rule 4: Δx/x = 2·(2%) + 1·(3%) + ½·(4%) = 4% + 3% + 2% = 9%.

09Vernier, Screw Gauge & Least Count

JEE consistently asks one question on instrument readings — usually finding the diameter from a vernier or screw-gauge sketch.

Least count — the universal definition

Least count = (smallest main-scale division) / (number of vernier or circular divisions) The smallest length the instrument can resolve

Vernier callipers

A standard vernier with LC = 0.1 mm. The vernier divisions span 9 main divisions but read as 10.

Reading a vernier

  1. Read the main scale just before the vernier zero. Call this M (in mm).
  2. Find which vernier division coincides exactly with a main-scale mark. Call this index n.
  3. Reading = M + n × LC.

Example: Main scale just before zero = 1.4 cm. The 6th vernier division coincides with a main mark. LC = 0.1 mm = 0.01 cm. Reading = 1.4 cm + 6 × 0.01 cm = 1.46 cm.

Screw gauge (micrometer)

Screw gauge: main-scale + circular-scale × LC, then add (or subtract) zero-error.

Reading a screw gauge

  1. Read the main scale (sleeve) in mm. Call it M.
  2. Read the circular scale division aligning with the reference line. Call it n.
  3. Reading = M + n × LC ± zero error.

Pitch: the linear distance moved per full turn of the thimble. LC = pitch / number of circular divisions. A typical screw gauge: pitch = 1 mm, 100 divisions → LC = 0.01 mm.

Zero error — sign matters

  • Positive zero error (zero of circular is below reference line when jaws closed): subtract from observed reading.
  • Negative zero error (zero is above reference line): add to observed reading.

Memory aid: "Always subtract the (signed) zero error" — i.e. corrected = observed − (signed zero error).

11Worked Examples — Easy → Hard

Easy Example 1 — Find the dimensional formula

Find the dimensional formula of surface tension.

Definition

Surface tension σ = F / L (force per unit length).

Substitute

σ = [MLT⁻²] / [L] = [MT⁻²].

[MT⁻²]
Easy Example 2 — Convert units

Convert 72 km/h into m/s.

Method

Multiply by conversion factors that equal 1: 72 × (1000 m / 1 km) × (1 h / 3600 s).

Compute

= 72 × 1000 / 3600 = 20 m/s.

20 m/s
Moderate Example 3 — Find dimensions of a, b in F = av + bt²

If F = a·v + b·t² describes the force on a particle (v = velocity, t = time), find the dimensions of a and b.

Setup — additive terms must share dimensions

Each term on the right must have dimensions of force, [MLT⁻²].

For a

a·v = [MLT⁻²] → a = [MLT⁻²]/[LT⁻¹] = [MT⁻¹].

For b

b·t² = [MLT⁻²] → b = [MLT⁻²]/[T²] = [MLT⁻⁴].

[a] = [MT⁻¹], [b] = [MLT⁻⁴]
Moderate Example 4 — Derive Stokes' law

The viscous force on a sphere depends on its radius r, velocity v, and the medium's viscosity η. Derive the form of the viscous force using dimensional analysis.

Setup

F = k · η^p · r^q · v^s. Dimensions: [MLT⁻²] = [ML⁻¹T⁻¹]^p · [L]^q · [LT⁻¹]^s.

Equate exponents

M: p = 1. L: −p + q + s = 1. T: −p − s = −2.

Solve

From T: s = 1. From L: q = 1.

F = k · η · r · v  (Stokes: k = 6π)
Moderate Example 5 — Error in a derived quantity

The volume of a cylinder is V = πr²h. The radius r is measured with 2% error and h with 1% error. Find the maximum percent error in V.

Apply rule 4

ΔV/V = 2·Δr/r + 1·Δh/h = 2·(2%) + 1·(1%) = 5%.

5%
Hard Example 6 — Vernier reading with zero error

A vernier callipers has 20 divisions on the vernier scale, matching 19 main-scale divisions. The main-scale division is 1 mm. While measuring a rod, the main scale reads 5.4 cm and the 8th vernier division coincides with a main-scale mark. The instrument shows a zero error of +0.02 mm. Find the rod's true length.

Least count

20 VSD = 19 MSD = 19 mm → 1 VSD = 19/20 mm. LC = 1 MSD − 1 VSD = 1 − 0.95 = 0.05 mm.

Observed reading

5.4 cm + 8 × 0.05 mm = 54 mm + 0.4 mm = 54.4 mm.

Correct for zero error

True = observed − zero error = 54.4 − 0.02 = 54.38 mm.

54.38 mm
Hard Example 7 — Combined error in g from a pendulum

The acceleration due to gravity is determined from a pendulum using g = 4π²L/T². The length L is measured to within 0.1 cm out of 100 cm. Time for 50 oscillations is 100 s with stopwatch precision 0.1 s. Find the percent error in g.

Errors

ΔL/L = 0.1/100 = 0.1%. Period T = (total time)/50, so ΔT/T = 0.1/100 = 0.1%.

Apply rule 4

Δg/g = 1·(ΔL/L) + 2·(ΔT/T) = 0.1% + 0.2% = 0.3%.

Δg/g = 0.3%

The dimensions of stress are the same as the dimensions of:
(A) Energy    (B) Force    (C) Pressure    (D) Strain

Stress = F/A → [ML⁻¹T⁻²]. This matches pressure exactly. Strain is dimensionless.

In the relation P = α/β · e^(−αZ/(kT)), P is pressure, Z is distance, k is Boltzmann constant, T is temperature. The dimensions of β are:
▼ Show solution

The exponent αZ/(kT) must be dimensionless. [α] = [kT]/[Z] = [ML²T⁻²]/[L] = [MLT⁻²].

Now P = α/β → [β] = [α]/[P] = [MLT⁻²]/[ML⁻¹T⁻²] = [L²]… wait, let me redo: [β] = [MLT⁻²] / [ML⁻¹T⁻²] = [L²].

Hmm — but the standard JEE answer here is [M⁰L²T⁰] = pure area dimensions.

13Common Traps & Mistakes

⚠
Forgetting that strain, refractive index, and angles are dimensionless. Asked "what is the dimensional formula of strain?" — write [M⁰L⁰T⁰], not blank.
⚠
Mixing up M (mega) with m (milli). Always check capitalisation. 1 MW ≠ 1 mW.
⚠
Forgetting that subtraction adds absolute errors, not relative. Δ(a−b) = Δa + Δb — never Δa − Δb.
⚠
Treating torque and energy as identical. They have the same dimensions but are physically distinct (vector vs scalar). Read the question — sometimes "different" means physically different.
⚠
Forgetting zero error sign. Always: corrected = observed − (signed zero error). Positive zero error → subtract; negative → add.
⚠
Pretending dimensional analysis can find dimensionless constants. The 2π in pendulum period, the ½ in KE, the 6π in Stokes — none come from dimensions. State up front: "k is a dimensionless constant."
⚠
Rounding mid-calculation. Carry one or two extra digits, round only at the very end. Sig-fig rules apply to the final answer.
⚠
Confusing pitch with least count on a screw gauge. Pitch = distance per turn (mm). LC = pitch / number of circular divisions.
⚠
Adding errors as relative when they should be absolute (or vice versa). Sum/difference: absolute. Product/quotient/power: relative.
⚠
Forgetting the kelvin/coulomb dimensions exist. Quantities like R (gas constant), q (charge), B (magnetic field) need K and A. Not all formulas live in M-L-T-only land.
