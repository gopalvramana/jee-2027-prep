01Why This Unit Matters

Chemical Bonding is the bridge between atomic structure and everything else. The shape of a molecule determines its polarity. Polarity determines intermolecular forces. Intermolecular forces determine boiling points, solubility, and reactivity. In organic chemistry, VSEPR and hybridisation explain why sp² carbons are planar and sp³ carbons are tetrahedral — critical for stereochemistry.

📊 JEE Appearance: 2–3 Questions Per Paper
Bonding appears consistently. Most common: VSEPR geometry prediction, hybridisation state, MO bond order, and polarity comparisons. These are all pattern-recognition problems once you know the rules.
✅ The Four-Step Chain
For almost any bonding question: (1) Draw Lewis structure → (2) Count electron pairs → (3) Determine geometry (VSEPR) → (4) Determine hybridisation. This chain solves 80% of bonding problems.

02Types of Chemical Bonds

Ionic Bond
  • Electron transfer from metal to non-metal
  • ΔEN > 1.7 (rough rule)
  • Forms crystal lattice (not discrete molecules)
  • High melting point; conducts electricity when dissolved
  • Examples: NaCl, KBr, MgO, CaF₂
Covalent Bond
  • Electron sharing between non-metals
  • ΔEN < 1.7 (rough rule); pure covalent if ΔEN ≈ 0
  • Forms discrete molecules
  • Lower melting point than ionic (exceptions: diamond, SiO₂)
  • Examples: H₂O, CO₂, CH₄, N₂
Coordinate (Dative) Bond
  • Both electrons donated by one atom
  • Donor: has lone pair; Acceptor: has empty orbital
  • Once formed, identical to covalent bond
  • Examples: NH₄⁺ (N→H⁺), BF₃·NH₃ (N→B)
ΔEN (Electronegativity Difference)Bond Type
0.0 – 0.4Non-polar covalent (e.g. H₂, Cl₂)
0.4 – 1.7Polar covalent (e.g. HCl, H₂O)
> 1.7Ionic (e.g. NaCl, MgO)

03Lewis Structures — Step-by-Step Method

5-Step Lewis Structure Method
  1. Count total valence electrons: Sum up valence e⁻ of all atoms. Add 1 per negative charge; subtract 1 per positive charge.
  2. Choose central atom: Least electronegative non-hydrogen atom (usually) goes in centre.
  3. Connect atoms with single bonds: Each bond uses 2 electrons.
  4. Complete octets on outer atoms first (except H, which needs only 2).
  5. Place remaining electrons on central atom. If central atom lacks octet, form double/triple bonds using lone pairs from outer atoms.
Worked: Lewis Structure of SO₂

Step 1: Valence e⁻ = S(6) + O(6) + O(6) = 18 electrons

Step 2: S is central (less electronegative than O)

Step 3: S–O–S skeleton: uses 4 electrons → 14 remaining

Step 4: Complete octets on both O: each needs 6 more e⁻ → uses 12 → 2 remaining

Step 5: Place 2 e⁻ as lone pair on S → S has only 6 electrons. Form one double bond (S=O). Result: O=S–O (with lone pair on S)

Final: SO₂ has resonance — both S–O bonds are equivalent with bond order 1.5

⚠ Octet Rule Exceptions — JEE Frequently Tests These
  • H, He: Need only 2 electrons (duet)
  • Be: Often has only 4 e⁻ (BeCl₂ — linear, incomplete octet)
  • B: Often has only 6 e⁻ (BF₃ — trigonal planar, incomplete octet); acts as Lewis acid
  • P, S, Cl, Xe: Can have expanded octets (10, 12 electrons) using d-orbitals: PCl₅, SF₆, XeF₄

04Formal Charge & Resonance

Formal Charge Formula

FC = V − N − B/2

V = valence electrons of free atom  |  N = non-bonding (lone pair) electrons  |  B = bonding electrons

The best Lewis structure has: (1) smallest formal charges, (2) negative FC on most electronegative atom, (3) sum of all FC = overall charge on molecule.

💡 Resonance
When more than one valid Lewis structure exists, the true structure is a resonance hybrid — an average of all contributing structures. Resonance lowers energy and delocalises electrons.
  • CO₃²⁻: 3 equivalent resonance structures → C–O bond order = 4/3 ≈ 1.33
  • SO₂: 2 resonance structures → S–O bond order = 1.5
  • Benzene (C₆H₆): 2 Kekulé structures → C–C bond order = 1.5
Bond order from resonance = total bonds / number of bonds of that type.

05VSEPR Theory — All Geometries

VSEPR (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion): electron pairs arrange themselves to minimise repulsion. Count all electron pairs (bonding + lone) around the central atom.

Repulsion order: Lone pair–Lone pair > Lone pair–Bonding pair > Bonding pair–Bonding pair

This is why lone pairs compress bond angles (NH₃: 107° vs CH₄: 109.5°; H₂O: 104.5° vs NH₃: 107°)

06Hybridisation — sp to sp³d²

Hybridisation is the mixing of atomic orbitals to form new hybrid orbitals of equal energy. The number of hybrid orbitals = total electron pairs (bonding + lone) around central atom.

Hybridisation Quick Formula

H = ½(V + M − C + A)

V = valence e⁻ of central atom  |  M = monovalent atoms  |  C = cations removed  |  A = anions added

Then: H=2→sp, H=3→sp², H=4→sp³, H=5→sp³d, H=6→sp³d²

sp1s + 1pLinear180°BeCl₂, CO₂, C₂H₂ (acetylene)
sp²1s + 2pTrigonal planar120°BF₃, SO₃, C₂H₄ (ethylene), benzene
sp³1s + 3pTetrahedral (or bent/pyramidal with LP)109.5°CH₄, NH₃, H₂O, CCl₄
sp³d1s + 3p + 1dTrigonal bipyramidal90°/120°PCl₅, SF₄, ClF₃
sp³d²1s + 3p + 2dOctahedral (or sq. planar with LP)90°SF₆, XeF₄, BrF₅
💡 σ vs π Bonds
  • Single bond = 1σ bond (uses hybridised orbital, allows rotation)
  • Double bond = 1σ + 1π bond (π uses unhybridised p orbital, restricts rotation)
  • Triple bond = 1σ + 2π bonds
  • sp² C has one unhybridised p orbital → forms π bonds → flat geometry
  • sp³ C has no unhybridised p → no π bonds → can rotate freely
⚠ JEE Trap: NH₃ vs NF₃ Hybridisation
Both NH₃ and NF₃ are sp³ hybridised with trigonal pyramidal geometry. But their polarity and dipole moments differ — in NH₃ the N–H bond dipoles and lone pair dipole add up; in NF₃ they partially cancel. NF₃ has lower dipole moment than NH₃.

07MO Theory & Bond Order

Molecular Orbital (MO) theory combines atomic orbitals to form bonding (σ, π) and antibonding (σ*, π*) molecular orbitals. Bonding MOs are lower in energy; antibonding higher.

Bond Order Formula

BO = (Nb − Na) / 2

Nb = electrons in bonding MOs  |  Na = electrons in antibonding MOs

BO = 0 → molecule doesn't exist; BO = 1 → single bond; BO = 1.5 → between single and double; BO = 2 → double; BO = 3 → triple

08Molecular Polarity & Dipole Moment

A molecule is polar if the vector sum of all bond dipoles is non-zero. Individual polar bonds can cancel if the geometry is symmetric.

Non-Polar (dipoles cancel)
  • CO₂ — linear, O=C=O, dipoles cancel
  • BF₃ — trigonal planar, perfect symmetry
  • CCl₄ — tetrahedral, all C–Cl dipoles cancel
  • SF₆ — octahedral, symmetric
  • XeF₂ — linear (has LP, but non-polar)
Polar (dipoles don't cancel)
  • H₂O — bent, lone pairs break symmetry
  • NH₃ — pyramidal, lone pair adds to dipole
  • HCl, HF — diatomic with ΔEN
  • CHCl₃ — tetrahedral but asymmetric
  • SO₂ — bent geometry, dipoles add
⚠ Key Rule: Symmetry = Non-Polar
Any molecule with a centre of symmetry where all substituents are identical is non-polar — even if individual bonds are very polar. CCl₄ has 4 very polar C–Cl bonds but is non-polar overall because they point in directions that sum to zero.

09Intermolecular Forces

Strength order: Ion-ion > Ion-dipole > H-bonding > Dipole-dipole > London dispersion (van der Waals)

💡 Why H₂O Boils at 100°C but H₂S at −60°C
H₂O has extensive H-bonding (each molecule can form 4 H-bonds via 2 O–H bonds and 2 lone pairs on O). H₂S has no H-bonding — S is not electronegative enough. This single factor raises the boiling point of H₂O by ~160°C.
⚠ JEE Trap: HF Boils Lower Than H₂O Despite Stronger H-bonds
HF has stronger individual H-bonds per pair than H₂O (F is more electronegative than O). But HF can only form 2 H-bonds per molecule (1 donor H, 2 acceptor lone pairs — but chain structure forms, not network). H₂O forms a 3D network with 4 H-bonds per molecule. More H-bonds overall → higher BP for H₂O.

11Worked Examples

Example 1 — VSEPR + Hybridisation of XeF₄

Q: Predict the geometry, hybridisation, and polarity of XeF₄.

Step 1 — Valence electrons: Xe(8) + 4×F(7) = 36 electrons

Step 2 — Lewis structure: 4 Xe–F bonds (uses 8 e⁻) + complete octets on F (uses 24 e⁻) + 4 e⁻ remain → 2 lone pairs on Xe

Step 3 — VSEPR: 4 bonding pairs + 2 lone pairs = 6 total pairs → octahedral arrangement. Lone pairs go to opposite positions (minimise repulsion) → Square planar geometry

Step 4 — Hybridisation: 6 total pairs → sp³d²

Step 5 — Polarity: 4 Xe–F bond dipoles cancel due to square planar symmetry → Non-polar

Example 2 — Bond Order Comparison

Q: Arrange CO, CO₂, CO₃²⁻ in order of increasing C–O bond length.

CO: Triple bond (C≡O), BO = 3

CO₂: Two C=O double bonds, BO = 2

CO₃²⁻: Resonance over 3 bonds, BO = 4/3 ≈ 1.33

Higher BO = shorter bond. Order of increasing length: CO < CO₂ < CO₃²⁻

Example 3 — O₂ Paramagnetism (MO Theory)

Q: O₂ is paramagnetic. Why? VB theory fails to explain this.

VB theory: Draws O₂ as O=O with all electrons paired → predicts diamagnetic. This is WRONG.

MO theory: O₂ has 16 electrons. Filling MOs: ...σ2p²(π2p)⁴(π*2p)² — the two π* electrons go into separate degenerate orbitals (Hund's rule) → 2 unpaired electrons → paramagnetic.

This is the classic example where MO theory succeeds where VB theory fails.

PYQ — JEE Main 2023

Q: The geometry of the molecule with maximum number of lone pairs on the central atom is:

(a) ClF₃   (b) XeF₄   (c) SF₄   (d) BrF₅

Solution: ClF₃: 3 BP + 2 LP = 5 (sp³d, T-shaped, 2 LP) | XeF₄: 4 BP + 2 LP = 6 (sp³d², sq. planar, 2 LP) | SF₄: 4 BP + 1 LP = 5 (sp³d, see-saw, 1 LP) | BrF₅: 5 BP + 1 LP = 6 (sp³d², sq. pyramidal, 1 LP)

Answer: XeF₄ and ClF₃ both have 2 LP — XeF₄ is the correct answer as the question asks for the one molecule (option b).

PYQ — JEE Main 2022

Q: Among the following, the incorrect statement about O₂ is:

Solution: O₂ is paramagnetic (correct — 2 unpaired electrons in π*). O₂ bond order = 2 (correct). O₂ is a diradical (correct). O₂ can be explained by VB theory alone — this is INCORRECT. VB theory predicts diamagnetic O₂, which is experimentally wrong.

13Common Traps & Mistakes

❌ Trap 1: Confusing electronic geometry with molecular geometry
H₂O has 4 electron pairs (tetrahedral electron geometry) but the molecular geometry is BENT (only 2 bonding pairs define shape). Always report molecular geometry, not electron geometry.
❌ Trap 2: Thinking all polar bonds = polar molecule
CO₂ has two very polar C=O bonds but is non-polar because it's linear and the dipoles cancel exactly. Always check geometry before concluding polarity.
❌ Trap 3: NF₃ more polar than NH₃
NF₃ is LESS polar than NH₃. In NH₃: N–H bond dipoles and lone pair dipole point in same direction (add up). In NF₃: N–F dipoles point away from N (toward F), opposing the lone pair on N → partial cancellation.
❌ Trap 4: Assuming H-bond strength = boiling point
HF has stronger H-bonds per pair than H₂O, but H₂O boils higher. Number of H-bonds per molecule matters: H₂O forms 4 H-bonds (3D network); HF forms fewer per molecule (chain structure).
❌ Trap 5: BF₃ doesn't violate the octet rule
BF₃ DOES violate the octet rule — B has only 6 electrons. That's why BF₃ is a Lewis acid (accepts lone pair from Lewis bases). Don't write a double bond to satisfy B's octet — formal charge analysis shows the structure with all single bonds is better.

🔗 Chemical Bonding — Rapid Reference

VSEPR (total pairs → geometry)

  • 2 → linear (180°)
  • 3,0LP → trig. planar (120°)
  • 4,0LP → tetrahedral (109.5°)
  • 4,1LP → trig. pyramidal (107°)
  • 4,2LP → bent (104.5°)
  • 5,0LP → trig. bipyramidal
  • 6,0LP → octahedral (90°)
  • 6,2LP → square planar

Hybridisation

  • sp → linear (BeCl₂, CO₂)
  • sp² → trig. planar (BF₃, C₂H₄)
  • sp³ → tetrahedral (CH₄, H₂O)
  • sp³d → trig. bipyramidal (PCl₅)
  • sp³d² → octahedral (SF₆)

MO Bond Orders

  • N₂: BO=3 (diamagnetic)
  • O₂: BO=2 (paramagnetic ⚠)
  • F₂: BO=1 (diamagnetic)
  • O₂⁺: BO=2.5 | O₂⁻: BO=1.5

Key Traps

  • CO₂, CCl₄, BF₃, SF₆ → non-polar
  • NF₃ less polar than NH₃
  • H₂O bp > HF bp (more H-bonds)
  • O₂ is paramagnetic
