1. Magnetic Field — The Concept

Magnetic field B exists around: permanent magnets, current-carrying conductors, and moving charges. SI unit: Tesla (T) = kg/(A·s²).
Right-Hand Rules — Summary
  • Straight wire: Thumb in current direction → fingers curl in B field direction (concentric circles)
  • Coil/solenoid: Curl fingers in current direction → thumb points to North pole (direction of B inside)
  • Force on positive charge (F = qv×B): Point fingers in v direction, curl toward B → thumb points in F direction
  • For negative charge: Reverse the thumb direction (opposite to positive)

2. Biot-Savart Law

dB = (μ₀/4π) × (I dl × r̂) / r²
Magnitude: dB = (μ₀/4π) × I dl sinφ / r² where φ = angle between dl and r̂.
μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A (permeability of free space)
When to Use Biot-Savart vs Ampere's Law
  • Biot-Savart: Curved wires, arcs, finite length segments, circular loops
  • Ampere's Law: High-symmetry configurations — infinite straight wire, infinite solenoid, toroid
  • JEE tip: If the question involves a circular arc or loop → Biot-Savart. If infinite/symmetric → Ampere.

3. Key Magnetic Field Results

SourceFormulaNotes
Infinite straight wireB = μ₀I / 2πrr = perpendicular distance from wire
Finite wire (angle subtended α, β)B = μ₀I(sinα + sinβ) / 4πrSemi-infinite: B = μ₀I/4πr
Centre of circular loopB = μ₀I / 2RR = radius of loop
Axis of circular loopB = μ₀IR² / 2(R²+x²)^(3/2)x = distance along axis from centre
Inside solenoidB = μ₀nIn = N/L = turns per metre
Inside toroidB = μ₀NI / 2πrr = mean radius
At centre of semi-circular arc of radius R: B = μ₀I/4R (half the full circle value). For arc of angle θ (radians): B = μ₀Iθ/4πR.

4. Ampere's Law

∮ B · dl = μ₀ I_enc
Line integral of B around a closed loop (Amperian loop) = μ₀ × total enclosed current.
Applying Ampere's Law
  1. Choose an Amperian loop that passes through the field point with B uniform and parallel to dl everywhere
  2. For a circular loop around infinite straight wire: B × 2πr = μ₀I → B = μ₀I/2πr
  3. Inside a solenoid: rectangular loop with one side inside (B = μ₀nI), one side outside (B = 0)
  4. Only works cleanly when B has high symmetry (cylindrical, planar)

5. Solenoid & Toroid

Solenoid
  • B_inside = μ₀nI (uniform)
  • B_outside ≈ 0 (for long solenoid)
  • n = N/L = turns per unit length
  • Self-inductance: L = μ₀n²Al (H)
Toroid
  • B_inside = μ₀NI/2πr (varies with r)
  • B_outside = 0 (B confined inside toroid)
  • N = total turns, r = radius at point
  • No external magnetic field leakage

6. Lorentz Force on a Moving Charge

F = q(v × B)
Magnitude: F = qvB sinθ (θ = angle between v and B). Direction: right-hand rule for positive charge; reverse for negative.
ConditionForceResult
v ∥ B (θ = 0°)F = 0No magnetic force — particle moves in straight line
v ⊥ B (θ = 90°)F = qvB (max)Circular motion (centripetal force)
v at angle θ to BF = qvB sinθHelical motion
Magnetic force does NO work on a moving charge (F ⊥ v always → W = F·d = 0). Magnetic field cannot change the speed or kinetic energy of a particle — only its direction.

7. Circular Motion in Magnetic Field

Centripetal force = magnetic force: mv²/r = qvB
Radius: r = mv/qB · Period: T = 2πm/qB · Frequency: f = qB/2πm
Cyclotron Principle — Time Period is Independent of Speed!

T = 2πm/qB — independent of v and r. Faster particles move in larger circles but complete each revolution in the same time.

This is why the cyclotron works: the alternating electric field can stay in sync with the particle regardless of its energy.

8. Velocity Selector

When electric force = magnetic force: qE = qvB → v = E/B
Only particles with velocity v = E/B pass through undeflected. Used in mass spectrometers.
Velocity Selector Setup

Electric field E and magnetic field B applied perpendicular to each other and to particle velocity.

Slow particles (qE > qvB): deflected by E field. Fast particles: deflected by B field. Selected velocity v = E/B passes straight through.

9. Force on a Current-Carrying Wire

F = I L × B → magnitude: F = BIL sinθ
θ = angle between current direction and B field. Force ⊥ to both I and B.
ConditionForce
Wire ∥ B (θ = 0°)F = 0
Wire ⊥ B (θ = 90°)F = BIL (maximum)
Closed loop in uniform BF_net = 0 (forces cancel)

10. Force Between Parallel Wires

F/L = μ₀I₁I₂ / 2πd
Force per unit length between two infinite parallel wires separated by distance d.
Same Direction Currents
  • Wires attract each other
  • Definition of Ampere: 1 A produces 2×10⁻⁷ N/m at 1 m separation
Opposite Direction Currents
  • Wires repel each other
  • Memory: SADA → Same currents Attract, Different currents Repel

11. Torque on a Current Loop

τ = NIAB sinθ = m × B
N = turns, I = current, A = area of loop, B = field, θ = angle between coil PLANE and B (not normal!)
JEE Trap on θ: θ in τ = NIAB sinθ is the angle between the coil PLANE and B. When coil plane is parallel to B (θ = 90°) → max torque. When coil plane ⊥ B (θ = 0°) → zero torque. Some books define φ as the angle between B and the NORMAL — then τ = NIAB cosφ.
Magnetic Dipole Moment

m = NIA (vector — direction by right-hand rule along normal to coil)

Torque: τ = m × B (magnitude: τ = mB sinα, α = angle between m and B)

Potential energy: U = −m · B = −mB cosα

12. Moving Coil Galvanometer

At equilibrium: Deflecting torque = Restoring torque
NIAB = Cφ → φ = NIAB/C (φ = deflection, C = restoring couple per unit angle)
ConversionMethodEffect
AmmeterLow resistance shunt S in parallelI_g × G = (I − I_g) × S
VoltmeterHigh resistance R in seriesV = I_g(G + R)
Ammeter: low resistance (ideal = 0) — must not disturb circuit current. Voltmeter: high resistance (ideal = ∞) — must not draw current.

13. Magnetic Dipole in External Field

PropertyFormula
Torque on dipoleτ = mB sinα (α = angle with B)
Potential energyU = −mB cosα
Stable equilibriumα = 0° (m ∥ B, U = −mB, minimum)
Unstable equilibriumα = 180° (m anti-∥ B, U = +mB, maximum)

14. Worked Examples

Example 1 — B at Centre of Semicircular Arc

A wire is bent into a semicircle of radius R=0.1 m. Current I=5 A. Straight portions extend to infinity on both sides (in same line). Find B at centre.

B (semicircular arc) = μ₀I/4R = (4π×10⁻⁷×5)/(4×0.1) = π×10⁻⁶/0.4 = 7.85×10⁻⁶ T
B (two semi-infinite straight wires) = 2 × μ₀I/4πr = 0 (at the endpoint, perpendicular segment contributes 0)
Wait — the semi-infinite straight segments DO contribute if they're NOT along the line to the centre.
If straight parts extend along the same diameter line: they're along direction of r̂ → sinφ=0 → B=0 from straight parts
Total B = 7.85 × 10⁻⁶ T ≈ 7.85 μT
Example 2 — Proton in Magnetic Field

A proton (m=1.67×10⁻²⁷ kg, q=1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C) enters B=0.5 T perpendicular at v=2×10⁶ m/s. Find radius and time period.

r = mv/qB = (1.67×10⁻²⁷ × 2×10⁶) / (1.6×10⁻¹⁹ × 0.5)
r = 3.34×10⁻²¹ / 8×10⁻²⁰ = 0.0418 m ≈ 4.18 cm
T = 2πm/qB = 2π×1.67×10⁻²⁷ / (1.6×10⁻¹⁹×0.5) = 1.31×10⁻⁷ s
Example 3 — Torque on Current Loop

Rectangular coil: 100 turns, 10 cm × 20 cm, carrying 2 A. Placed in uniform B = 0.5 T. Plane of coil parallel to B. Find torque.

A = 0.10 × 0.20 = 0.02 m²
Coil plane parallel to B → θ = 90° (angle between plane and B)
τ = NIAB sinθ = 100 × 2 × 0.02 × 0.5 × sin90° = 100 × 2 × 0.02 × 0.5 × 1 = 2 N·m

15. Common JEE Traps

Trap 1: Magnetic force does ZERO work on a moving charge. Speed and KE never change due to magnetic force alone. Only direction changes.
Trap 2: Time period T = 2πm/qB is INDEPENDENT of speed. Do not confuse with radius r = mv/qB (which depends on speed).
Trap 3: In τ = NIAB sinθ — θ is angle between coil PLANE and B, NOT between the normal and B. Max torque when plane is parallel to B (not perpendicular).
Trap 4: Parallel wires with currents in SAME direction ATTRACT. Different directions REPEL. (Opposite to electric charges — like charges attract for current!)
Trap 5: Net magnetic force on a closed current loop in a UNIFORM field = 0 (forces on opposite sides cancel). But torque ≠ 0 (they form a couple).
Trap 6: Ammeter has LOW resistance (added in parallel as shunt to divert most current). Voltmeter has HIGH resistance (added in series to draw minimal current). Swapping them gives wrong measurement.
Trap 7: B inside a solenoid is uniform and equals μ₀nI. B OUTSIDE is approximately zero (not μ₀nI). For a toroid, B inside varies with radius r (not uniform).
