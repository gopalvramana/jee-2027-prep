1. Work — Definition and Formula

Work is done by a force when the force has a component along the direction of displacement.

W = F · d · cosθ
θ = angle between the force vector and the displacement vector. Work is a scalar — it has sign but no direction.
Positive Work
  • Force and displacement in same direction (θ < 90°)
  • Applied force pulling block forward
  • Gravity on falling object
Zero Work
  • Force ⊥ displacement (θ = 90°)
  • Normal force — always zero work
  • Centripetal force — perpendicular to velocity
Negative Work
  • Force opposes displacement (θ > 90°)
  • Friction — always negative work
  • Gravity on rising object
Variable Force — Work from F-x Graph

When F varies with x: W = area under F-x graph

Area above x-axis = positive work. Area below = negative work. Net work = algebraic sum.

JEE frequently shows trapezoidal or triangular F-x graphs — just compute the area.

Path Independence — only for Conservative Forces: Work done by gravity depends only on vertical height change, NOT the path. W_gravity = mgh (h = vertical drop), regardless of slope, curve, or zigzag path.

2. Work-Energy Theorem

W_net = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mu²
Net work done by ALL forces on an object = change in its kinetic energy.
How to Apply the Work-Energy Theorem
  1. Identify all forces acting on the object
  2. Compute work done by EACH force: W_gravity, W_friction, W_applied, W_normal (= 0)
  3. Sum all works: W_net = W₁ + W₂ + W₃ + …
  4. Set equal to ΔKE: W_net = ½mv² − ½mu²
  5. Solve for unknown (usually v or distance)
When to use Energy vs F=ma: If time is not involved, energy is almost always faster. Ask: "Do I need time?" If no → energy method. If yes → kinematics/Newton.

Example: 2 kg block slides 5 m down a 30° incline with μ=0.2. Find final speed (starts at rest).

W_gravity = mgh = 2×10×(5 sin30°) = 2×10×2.5 = 50 J
N = mg cos30° = 2×10×0.866 = 17.3 N
W_friction = −μN×d = −0.2×17.3×5 = −17.3 J
W_net = 50 − 17.3 = 32.7 J = ½×2×v²
v² = 32.7 → v ≈ 5.7 m/s

3. Conservative vs Non-Conservative Forces

Conservative Forces
  • Work done is path-independent
  • Work in a closed loop = 0
  • Can define potential energy
  • Examples: Gravity, spring force, electrostatic force
Non-Conservative Forces
  • Work done is path-dependent
  • Energy is dissipated (converted to heat)
  • Cannot define potential energy
  • Examples: Friction, air resistance, applied force
With non-conservative forces:
KE_f + PE_f = KE_i + PE_i − |W_friction| − |W_air resistance|
(mechanical energy decreases by the work done against friction)

4. Gravitational Potential Energy

Reference Level is your choice: You can set h=0 anywhere (ground, lowest point, table). Potential energy values change, but ΔPE is always the same. Always state your reference!

5. Spring Potential Energy

PE_spring = ½kx²
k = spring constant (N/m), x = compression or extension from natural length. Always positive (energy is stored).
Spring-Block System — Key Results
  • Natural length position: max speed (all energy is KE, PE = 0)
  • Max compression/extension: speed = 0 (all energy is PE, KE = 0)
  • Energy conservation: ½kx₁² + ½mv₁² = ½kx₂² + ½mv₂²
  • Spring force: F = −kx (restoring, opposite to x)
  • Work by spring: W = −ΔPE_spring = −(½kx₂² − ½kx₁²)

6. Conservation of Mechanical Energy

KE + PE = constant
Valid ONLY when no friction or non-conservative forces do work. KE₁ + PE₁ = KE₂ + PE₂
🔢 Interactive — Energy Conservation Calculator

Given mass and initial/final heights, find speed at any point.

Pendulum — Quick result: v at lowest point = √(2gL(1 − cosθ)), where θ is the initial angle from vertical and L is the pendulum length. Derived directly from mgh = ½mv².

9. Elastic Collisions (1D)

Both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved. Coefficient of restitution e = 1.
QuantityFormulaNotes
Final velocity of m₁v₁ = (m₁−m₂)u₁/(m₁+m₂)m₂ initially at rest
Final velocity of m₂v₂ = 2m₁u₁/(m₁+m₂)m₂ initially at rest
Equal Masses (m₁=m₂)
  • v₁ = 0 (stops completely)
  • v₂ = u₁ (takes all velocity)
  • Classic Newton's cradle
m₁ ≫ m₂ (heavy hits light)
  • v₁ ≈ u₁ (barely slows)
  • v₂ ≈ 2u₁ (bounces at 2× speed)
m₁ ≪ m₂ (light hits heavy)
  • v₁ ≈ −u₁ (bounces back)
  • v₂ ≈ 0 (barely moves)

10. Inelastic Collisions

Inelastic (general)
  • Momentum conserved
  • KE NOT conserved (some lost to heat/deformation)
  • 0 < e < 1
  • Objects separate after collision
Perfectly Inelastic
  • Momentum conserved
  • Maximum KE loss
  • e = 0
  • Objects stick together: v_common = m₁u₁/(m₁+m₂)
Loss in KE (perfectly inelastic):
ΔKE = ½ × m₁m₂/(m₁+m₂) × u₁² = ½μu₁² where μ is the reduced mass

11. Coefficient of Restitution

e = v_separation / v_approach = (v₂ − v₁) / (u₁ − u₂)
e = 1: perfectly elastic · e = 0: perfectly inelastic · 0 < e < 1: partially elastic
🔢 Interactive — Collision Calculator
Ball Bouncing off Floor (e given)

After n bounces from height h₀: h_n = e²ⁿ × h₀

Example: e=0.8, dropped from h=10 m. After 2nd bounce: h = (0.8)⁴ × 10 = 0.4096 × 10 = 4.1 m

12. Centre of Mass

FormulaMeaning
x_cm = (m₁x₁ + m₂x₂)/(m₁+m₂)CM position (two particles)
v_cm = (m₁v₁ + m₂v₂)/(m₁+m₂)CM velocity
F_ext = (m₁+m₂)a_cmExternal force on system
p_total = M × v_cmTotal momentum
CM of a system moves as if ALL external forces act on total mass at CM. Internal forces (Newton's 3rd pair) cancel and don't affect CM motion.

13. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Pendulum Speed

A pendulum of length 2 m is released from 60° to vertical. Find speed at lowest point.

h = L − L cosθ = L(1 − cosθ) = 2(1 − cos60°) = 2(1 − 0.5) = 1 m
Energy conservation: mgh = ½mv² → v = √(2gh) = √(2×10×1) = √20 ≈ 4.47 m/s
Example 2 — Work from F-x Graph

F-x graph: F increases linearly from 0 to 20 N as x goes from 0 to 4 m, then drops to 0. Find work done.

Area = ½ × base × height = ½ × 4 × 20 = 40 J
(Triangle under the line)
Example 3 — Collision and Compression

2 kg ball at 6 m/s hits stationary 4 kg ball attached to spring k=100 N/m. Max compression?

At max compression: both move at v_cm (momentum conservation)
v_cm = m₁u₁/(m₁+m₂) = 2×6/6 = 2 m/s
Energy in CM frame = ½(m₁+m₂)v_cm² = ½×6×4 = 12 J
But total initial KE = ½×2×36 = 36 J; KE at compression = ½×6×4 = 12 J
Energy stored in spring = 36 − 12 = 24 J = ½kx²
x = √(2×24/100) = √0.48 ≈ 0.69 m

14. JEE PYQs

PYQ 1 — (JEE Main 2020)

A particle of mass 2 kg is moving with velocity (2î + 3ĵ) m/s. A constant force F = (3î − 2ĵ) N acts for 4 s. Find the work done by the force.

Impulse = FΔt = (3î − 2ĵ) × 4 = (12î − 8ĵ) N·s
v_f = v_i + Ft/m = (2î+3ĵ) + (12î−8ĵ)/2 = (2+6)î + (3−4)ĵ = 8î − ĵ
ΔKE = ½×2×[(8²+1²) − (2²+3²)] = [65 − 13] = 52 J
PYQ 2 — (JEE Main 2019)

Two identical balls A and B, each mass 0.1 kg, are attached to two identical springs of spring constant 0.1 N/m. The system is arranged as shown in figure (A pushed toward B on frictionless surface). If A is displaced 0.1 m from mean, find the energy of the system.

PE = ½kx² = ½ × 0.1 × (0.1)² = 0.0005 J = 5 × 10⁻⁴ J

15. Common JEE Traps

Trap 1: "Work done by friction" — friction always does NEGATIVE work on the moving object (opposing direction). But don't confuse with heat generated = |W_friction by kinetic friction|. Heat = f_k × d (always positive).
Trap 2: Normal force does zero work on a block on an incline. It's perpendicular to the displacement (along incline). W_N = 0 always.
Trap 3: In a perfectly inelastic collision, KE is NOT zero after collision — the objects still move together. KE = 0 only if both initial momenta cancel (head-on, equal momenta).
Trap 4: Spring PE = ½kx² uses the EXTENSION or COMPRESSION from natural length — NOT the total length of the spring.
Trap 5: Coefficient of restitution: e = v_separation / v_approach — always positive. Don't mix up "after" and "before" speeds.
Trap 6: KE = p²/2m. If two particles have the same momentum, the lighter one has MORE kinetic energy. If same KE, lighter particle has MORE momentum.
Trap 7: Energy conservation is valid only when no non-conservative forces do work. With friction: always subtract |W_friction|.
Trap 8: Power P = Fv uses instantaneous force and instantaneous velocity. For average power, use P_avg = Total_work / Total_time.
