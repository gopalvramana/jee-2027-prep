01Why This Unit Matters

Chemical Equilibrium is the highest-yield Physical Chemistry unit for JEE. It appears in 2–3 questions per paper, sometimes more, and the question types are highly predictable. Every numerical reduces to setting up an ICE table. Every conceptual question is Le Chatelier. Learn these two tools and this unit is nearly solved.

⚠ Most Skipped → Most Regretted
Students often rush through equilibrium because "I'll understand it when I study acids and bases." This is backwards. Ionic equilibrium (C6), electrochemistry (C7), and even organic reaction rates all use equilibrium concepts. Shaky C5 = shaky everything downstream.

02What is Equilibrium?

When a reversible reaction proceeds in a closed system, eventually the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction. At this point, concentrations stop changing — this is dynamic equilibrium.

What IS equilibrium
  • Both forward and reverse reactions still occurring
  • Concentrations of reactants and products are constant (not equal)
  • Rateforward = Ratereverse
  • System is in a minimum Gibbs energy state (ΔG = 0)
  • Can be approached from either direction
What is NOT equilibrium
  • Concentrations are equal (they almost never are)
  • Reactions have stopped
  • The system is "stuck" — it will respond to disturbances
  • Only applies to one direction
💡 Equilibrium vs. Completion
Reactions with very large K (K >> 1) effectively "go to completion" — virtually all reactants convert to products. Reactions with very small K (K << 1) barely proceed. Intermediate K → true equilibrium mixture of both.

03Writing Kc Expressions

For a general reaction: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

Law of Mass Action

Kc = [C]c [D]d / [A]a [B]b

Concentrations in mol/L (M)  |  Only at equilibrium concentrations

⚠ Pure Solids and Liquids are EXCLUDED from K
The activities of pure solids and pure liquids = 1, so they are not included in K expressions.
Examples:
  • CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g)  →  Kc = [CO₂] (solids excluded)
  • H₂O(l) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)  →  Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] (liquid water excluded)
  • Fe₂O₃(s) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2Fe(s) + 3H₂O(g)  →  Kc = [H₂O]³/[H₂]³

04Kp and the Kp–Kc Relation

For gas-phase reactions, it is often more convenient to express equilibrium in terms of partial pressures rather than concentrations:

Kp Expression and Relation to Kc

Kp = Kc (RT)Δng

R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K  |  T in Kelvin  |  Δng = moles gaseous products − gaseous reactants

ReactionΔngKp vs Kc
N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃2−4 = −2Kp = Kc(RT)⁻² → Kp < Kc
PCl₅ ⇌ PCl₃ + Cl₂2−1 = +1Kp = Kc(RT)¹ → Kp > Kc
H₂ + Cl₂ ⇌ 2HCl2−2 = 0Kp = Kc
N₂O₄ ⇌ 2NO₂2−1 = +1Kp = Kc(RT)

05Manipulating K — Reverse, Scale, Combine

Three Algebraic Rules for K
OperationEffect on KExample
Reverse the reactionKnew = 1/KIf A⇌B has K=4, then B⇌A has K=0.25
Multiply coefficients by nKnew = KnIf A⇌B has K=4, then 2A⇌2B has K=16
Add two reactions (Hess for K)Ktotal = K₁ × K₂K for A→C = K(A→B) × K(B→C)
💡 K is Temperature Dependent Only
K changes only with temperature. Changing concentration, pressure, or adding a catalyst does NOT change K — it changes the equilibrium position (the actual amounts at equilibrium) but the ratio defined by K stays fixed.

07Le Chatelier's Principle — All Stresses

Le Chatelier's Principle: When a stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system shifts in the direction that partially relieves that stress.

StressDirection of ShiftEffect on K
Add reactantForward (to consume added reactant)No change
Remove reactantReverse (to replenish reactant)No change
Add productReverse (to consume added product)No change
Remove productForward (to replenish product)No change
Increase pressure (decrease V)Towards side with fewer gas molesNo change
Decrease pressure (increase V)Towards side with more gas molesNo change
Increase temperatureEndothermic direction (absorbs heat)Increases if endo → products; decreases if exo
Decrease temperatureExothermic direction (releases heat)Decreases if endo; increases if exo
Add catalystNo shift — reaches equilibrium fasterNo change
Add inert gas at constant VNo shift (partial pressures unchanged)No change
Add inert gas at constant PTowards side with more gas moles (partial pressures decrease)No change
⚠ The Inert Gas Trap — Most Common JEE Confusion

Constant volume: Adding inert gas → total pressure rises, but partial pressures of reactants/products unchanged → no shift.

Constant pressure: Adding inert gas → volume must expand to maintain pressure → partial pressures of all species decrease → system shifts towards more moles of gas.

08Haber Process — Master Example

The industrial synthesis of ammonia is the single most-tested Le Chatelier application in JEE. Understand it deeply and you can answer any Le Chatelier question.

N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)    ΔH = −92 kJ (exothermic)
FactorFor more NH₃Industrial compromise
PressureHigh P (4→2 gas moles)150–300 atm (higher is costly)
TemperatureLow T (exothermic → low T shifts forward)450–500°C (balance with rate)
ConcentrationExcess N₂ and H₂; remove NH₃NH₃ liquefied and removed continuously
CatalystDoesn't change yield, only speedFe₂O₃ with K₂O, Al₂O₃ promoters
💡 Why Not Use Even Lower Temperature?
Low temperature means slower reaction rate. The catalyst works best around 450°C. Below ~300°C, the rate is so slow that equilibrium is essentially never reached in a practical reactor — so the high yield (from low T) is worthless because it takes too long. 450°C is the sweet spot where rate is acceptable and yield is reasonable (~15%).

09ICE Table — The Definitive Method

ICE = Initial concentrations, Change in concentrations, Equilibrium concentrations. Set up this table before every single equilibrium numerical — no exceptions.

ICE Table Template — A(g) ⇌ B(g) + C(g)
ABC
Initial (M)a00
Change (M)−x+x+x
Equilibrium (M)a−xxx

Then: Kc = [B][C]/[A] = x²/(a−x). Solve for x. If Kc << 1, use the small x approximation: a−x ≈ a → x ≈ √(Kc·a). Otherwise solve the quadratic exactly.

10Degree of Dissociation — PCl₅ & N₂O₄

The degree of dissociation (α) is the fraction of the initial moles of a reactant that has dissociated at equilibrium. 0 ≤ α ≤ 1.

PCl₅ Dissociation — Definitive Derivation

Reaction: PCl₅(g) ⇌ PCl₃(g) + Cl₂(g)

Start with a moles PCl₅ in volume V:

PCl₅PCl₃Cl₂Total
Initiala00a
Change−aα+aα+aα—
Equilibriuma(1−α)aαaαa(1+α)

Kc = [PCl₃][Cl₂]/[PCl₅] = (aα/V)² / (a(1−α)/V) = a α²/V(1−α)

Kp = α² P / (1−α²)   (when total pressure = P and Δng=1)

Higher pressure (smaller V or larger P) → smaller α (equilibrium shifts backward, fewer gas moles)

N₂O₄ Dissociation

Reaction: N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g)

Start with 1 mol N₂O₄:

N₂O₄NO₂Total
Initial101
Equilibrium1−α2α1+α

Mole fractions: χ(N₂O₄) = (1−α)/(1+α); χ(NO₂) = 2α/(1+α)

Partial pressures: P(N₂O₄) = P(1−α)/(1+α); P(NO₂) = 2Pα/(1+α)

Kp = [P(NO₂)]² / P(N₂O₄) = 4α²P / (1−α²)

12Worked Examples

Example 1 — Finding Kp from Kc (Easy)

Q: For PCl₅ ⇌ PCl₃ + Cl₂, Kc = 1.8×10⁻⁷ at 250°C (523 K). Find Kp.

Step 1: Δng = (1+1) − 1 = +1

Step 2: Kp = Kc(RT)^Δng = 1.8×10⁻⁷ × (0.082 × 523)¹

Step 3: Kp = 1.8×10⁻⁷ × 42.9 = 7.7×10⁻⁶ atm

Example 2 — ICE Table Numerical (Medium)

Q: For A(g) ⇌ 2B(g), Kp = 4 atm. Initial pressure of A = 2 atm. Find equilibrium pressures.

AB
Initial (atm)20
Change−x+2x
Equilibrium2−x2x

Kp = (2x)²/(2−x) = 4

4x² = 4(2−x) = 8 − 4x → 4x² + 4x − 8 = 0 → x² + x − 2 = 0 → (x+2)(x−1) = 0 → x = 1

P(A) = 1 atm, P(B) = 2 atm

Example 3 — N₂O₄ Degree of Dissociation (Hard)

Q: N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g). α = 0.5 and total pressure P = 1 atm at 25°C. Find Kp.

Using derived formula: Kp = 4α²P / (1−α²)

Kp = 4(0.5)²(1) / (1−0.25) = 4(0.25)/0.75 = 1.0/0.75 = 1.33 atm

Example 4 — Q to Predict Direction

Q: For N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃, Kc = 0.148 at 500°C. Current concentrations: [N₂]=2M, [H₂]=3M, [NH₃]=4M. Which direction does the reaction proceed?

Qc = [NH₃]² / ([N₂][H₂]³) = (4)² / (2 × 27) = 16/54 = 0.296

Q = 0.296 > K = 0.148 → Q > K → reaction proceeds in reverse (NH₃ decomposes)

13JEE Previous-Year Questions

📋 Most Tested Equilibrium Patterns
  1. Write Kc and Kp; find one from other using ΔngRT
  2. ICE table numerical: given initial conditions and K, find equilibrium concentrations/pressures
  3. Le Chatelier: predict shift direction for given stress (especially temperature and inert gas)
  4. Degree of dissociation: PCl₅ or N₂O₄ numerical
  5. Q vs K comparison to find reaction direction
  6. Effect of changing conditions on K value (only temperature changes K)
PYQ — JEE Main 2023

Q: The value of Kc for the reaction SO₂(g) + ½O₂(g) ⇌ SO₃(g) at 727°C is 9.9 L½mol−½. Kp at 1000 K is (R = 0.082 L·atm/mol·K):

Δng = 1 − (1 + 0.5) = −0.5

Kp = Kc(RT)^(−0.5) = 9.9 × (0.082 × 1000)^(−0.5) = 9.9 / √82 = 9.9/9.06 ≈ 1.09 atm^(−0.5)

PYQ — JEE Main 2022

Q: For PCl₅(g) ⇌ PCl₃(g) + Cl₂(g), the degree of dissociation is α at a pressure P. At the same temperature, what happens to α if pressure is halved?

Kp = α²P/(1−α²) = constant (K only changes with T).

If P halves, α² must increase proportionally to keep Kp constant → α increases.

This agrees with Le Chatelier: lower pressure → more gas moles favoured → more dissociation.

14Common Traps & Mistakes

❌ Trap 1: Including solids/liquids in K expression
CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g) — Kc = [CO₂] only. Neither solid goes in the expression. Adding them multiplies by 1 (no numerical error) but shows conceptual misunderstanding.
❌ Trap 2: Thinking catalyst shifts equilibrium
A catalyst makes reactions reach equilibrium faster, but does not change the equilibrium position or K. Both forward and reverse rates increase equally.
❌ Trap 3: Inert gas at constant pressure vs constant volume
At constant volume: no shift. At constant pressure: shifts toward more gas moles. Always read the condition carefully — this is a classic JEE question.
❌ Trap 4: Forgetting to use concentration (not moles) in Kc
If 0.5 mol PCl₃ is formed in a 5L vessel, [PCl₃] = 0.5/5 = 0.1 M. Don't plug moles directly into Kc — divide by volume first.
❌ Trap 5: Assuming equilibrium means equal concentrations
Equilibrium concentrations of reactants and products are almost never equal. Equal concentrations is a coincidence, not a definition. Large K → products dominate; small K → reactants dominate.

15One-Page Cheat Card

⚖ Chemical Equilibrium — Rapid Reference

Core Formulas

  • Kp = Kc(RT)Δng
  • Krev = 1/K  |  K×n = Kn
  • Q < K → forward; Q > K → reverse
  • Kp(PCl₅) = α²P/(1−α²)
  • Kp(N₂O₄) = 4α²P/(1−α²)

Le Chatelier Rules

  • ↑P → fewer gas moles side
  • ↑T → endothermic side (K changes)
  • Catalyst → no shift, no K change
  • Inert gas, const V → no shift
  • Inert gas, const P → more gas moles side

ICE Table Steps

  1. Write balanced equation
  2. Set up I, C, E rows
  3. Express K in terms of x
  4. Solve for x; check: x < initial

K Tells You

  • K >> 1 → products favoured
  • K << 1 → reactants favoured
  • K changes only with T
  • ΔG° = −RT ln K
