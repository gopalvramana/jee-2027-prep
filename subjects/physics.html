P0 Units & Dimensions — Do This First March Week 1 3–5% · Free Marks ▼

Why first: Every Physics formula has dimensions. Dimensional analysis lets you verify formulas, derive relationships, convert units, and directly score 1–2 JEE questions per paper with minimal effort. Highest ROI topic in Physics.

S0-A Fundamental Units, SI System and Dimensional Formulas 1.5 hrs ▼
Concepts to Cover
  • 7 fundamental quantities and SI units: Length (m), Mass (kg), Time (s), Temperature (K), Current (A), Luminous intensity (cd), Amount of substance (mol)
  • Derived quantities: expressed in terms of the 7 base quantities
  • SI prefixes: pico (10-12), nano (10-9), micro (10-6), milli (10-3), kilo (103), mega (106), giga (109)
  • Dimensional formula: velocity = [LT-1], acceleration = [LT-2], force = [MLT-2]
  • Energy/Work = [ML2T-2], Pressure = [ML-1T-2], Power = [ML2T-3]
  • Momentum = [MLT-1], Torque = [ML2T-2] (same as energy — JEE trap!)
  • Dimensionless quantities: angle (radian), strain, relative density
Study Tip: Always derive dimensional formulas from the definition. Force = mass × acceleration → [M][LT-2] = [MLT-2]. Derive every time until automatic.
Practice Problems
  1. Find dimensional formula of: (a) momentum (b) torque (c) surface tension (d) viscosity
  2. Gravitational constant G has unit N m²/kg². Find its dimensional formula.
  3. Planck constant h appears in E = hν. Find dimensions of h.
  4. Which has the same dimensions as energy: Force×velocity, Force×displacement, Force×time, or Pressure×volume?
S0-B Dimensional Analysis — 3 Uses + Error Analysis 1.5 hrs ▼
Concepts to Cover
  • Use 1 — Check formula: both sides must have identical dimensions (necessary, not sufficient)
  • Use 2 — Derive formula: write y = k·ax·by·cz, match dimensions both sides, solve for x, y, z
  • Use 3 — Unit conversion: multiply by conversion factor (same dimension ratio = 1)
  • Limitation: cannot find dimensionless constants (π, 2, ½); cannot distinguish Energy from Torque
  • Error in sum/difference: absolute errors ADD — Δ(a+b) = Δa + Δb
  • Error in product/quotient: relative errors ADD — Δ(ab)/ab = Δa/a + Δb/b
  • Error in power: multiply relative error by power — Δ(an)/an = n × Δa/a
Study Tip: For derivation: write y = k·ap·bq·cr, equate powers of M, L, T separately. Three equations → three unknowns.
Practice Problems
  1. Check if v² = u² + 2as is dimensionally correct.
  2. Period T of pendulum depends only on L and g. Derive T using dimensional analysis.
  3. Force F = av + bt². Find dimensions of a and b.
  4. x = a²b/√c. Errors: a=2%, b=3%, c=4%. Find % error in x.
  5. Convert 1 joule into ergs using dimensional analysis.

S11D Motion — Equations and Graphs1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Distance vs displacement, speed vs velocity — scalar vs vector
  • Equations of motion: v=u+at, s=ut+½at², v²=u²+2as — derive from definitions
  • Motion under gravity: free fall (a=g downward), vertical throw
  • v-t graph: area = displacement, slope = acceleration
  • s-t graph: slope = velocity — straight = constant v, curve = acceleration
  • Displacement in nth second: sn = u + a(2n-1)/2
  • Sign convention: define positive direction first, use consistently
Tip: Write your positive direction at the top of every solution. Single habit that eliminates sign errors.
Practice Problems
  1. Ball thrown up at 20 m/s. Find max height and time to return.
  2. From trapezoidal v-t graph, find displacement in 0–5s and 5–10s.
  3. Two trains 100 m apart, both 10 m/s, one decelerates at 2 m/s². Collision?
  4. Particle: a = 2t−4. At t=0, v=3 m/s. Find v when displacement = 0.
S2Projectile Motion1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Horizontal and vertical motions are completely independent
  • Horizontal: vx = u cosθ constant throughout (zero acceleration)
  • Vertical: vy = u sinθ − gt (uniform downward acceleration)
  • Time of flight: T = 2u sinθ / g
  • Max height: H = u² sin²θ / 2g
  • Range: R = u² sin2θ / g — max at θ = 45°
  • Horizontal throw from height h: initial vy = 0
Tip: Draw the trajectory. Label horizontal and vertical components separately. Never mix them — they are independent.
Practice Problems
  1. Ball projected at 30° with 40 m/s. Find range, max height, time of flight.
  2. Ball thrown horizontally at 20 m/s from 80 m height. Find landing point.
  3. At what angle should a projectile be thrown so range = 4 × max height?
  4. Compare range, time of flight, and max height for 30° and 60°.

Concepts

  • Rain-man problems: find umbrella angle from vector diagram

  3. Rain at 10 m/s vertically, man walks east at 5 m/s. Umbrella angle?
  4. Two objects approach each other at 30 m/s and 20 m/s, 500 m apart. When do they meet?

P2 Laws of Motion April 2026 8–9% of paper ▼
S4Newton's Laws & Free Body Diagrams1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Newton 1st: object continues in its state unless net external force acts
  • Newton 2nd: Fnet = ma — vector equation, apply per direction separately
  • Newton 3rd: action and reaction are equal, opposite, on DIFFERENT objects
  • FBD: isolate ONE object, draw ALL forces acting ON it only
  • Normal force: always perpendicular to contact surface — not always equal to mg
  • Tension: pulls both ends; same throughout a massless string
Tip: Draw one FBD per object before writing any equation. Never mix forces from two different objects.
Practice Problems
  1. 5 kg block on frictionless table, force 20 N. Find acceleration.
  2. 3 kg and 5 kg blocks connected by string, pulled by 40 N. Find tension.
  3. Man 70 kg in lift accelerating up at 2 m/s². Find apparent weight.
  4. Three blocks A,B,C in contact on frictionless surface. 30 N applied on A. Find force between B and C.
S5Friction1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Static friction: self-adjusting from 0 to μsN maximum
  • Kinetic friction: constant — fk = μk × N
  • Always: μs > μk — more force to START than SUSTAIN motion
  • Inclined plane: weight along plane = mg sinθ, normal = mg cosθ
  • Sliding condition: tanθ > μ
  • Pseudo force in non-inertial frame: F = −maframe
Tip: On inclines, resolve forces ALONG and PERPENDICULAR to the incline surface — never horizontal/vertical.
Practice Problems
  1. Block on surface μs=0.4, μk=0.3, m=10 kg. Force to start? Force to sustain?
  2. Block on 30° incline, μ=0.4. Will it slide? Find acceleration if yes.
  3. Block just starts sliding at θ=45°. Find μs.
  4. 5 kg block pushed against wall horizontally. μ=0.3. Min force to stop sliding?
S6Pulley Systems & Connected Bodies1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Atwood machine: a = (m1−m2)g/(m1+m2)
  • Atwood tension: T = 2m1m2g / (m1+m2)
  • Inextensible string: magnitudes of accelerations are equal
  • Mass on incline + hanging mass: resolve weight along incline
  • Write F=ma separately for each mass, then combine equations
Tip: Write F=ma in full for EACH mass separately. Solve simultaneous equations. Never shortcut.
Practice Problems
  1. Atwood machine m1=3 kg, m2=5 kg. Find acceleration and tension.
  2. 4 kg on 30° incline (μ=0.2) connected via pulley to 3 kg hanging. Find acceleration.
  3. Three-block pulley system. Find acceleration and tension in each string.
  4. Table block (μ=0.3) connected via pulley to hanging block. Min hanging mass to cause motion?

P3 Work, Energy & Power May 2026 7–8% of paper ▼
S7Work & Work-Energy Theorem1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • W = F·d·cosθ — only force component along displacement does work
  • Work by gravity = mgh (path independent — only vertical height matters)
  • Work by normal force = 0 always (perpendicular to displacement)
  • Work by friction = negative (always opposes displacement)
  • Work-Energy Theorem: Wnet = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mu²
  • Spring: PE = ½kx² stored as elastic potential energy
Tip: Find work by EACH force separately, then sum for Wnet.
Practice Problems
  1. Block pulled 5 m on surface (μ=0.3) by 20 N at 30°. Find net work done.
  2. Spring k=200 N/m compressed 0.1 m. Find speed of 0.5 kg block when released.
  3. From F-x graph, find work done from x=0 to x=6 m.
  4. 2 kg block slides down 5 m incline (30°, μ=0.2). Find speed at bottom.
S8Conservation of Energy & Collisions1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • KE + PE = constant (when no friction/non-conservative forces)
  • With friction: KEf + PEf = KEi + PEi − |Wfriction|
  • Elastic collision: KE + momentum conserved
  • Inelastic: only momentum conserved; KE lost
  • Perfectly inelastic: objects stick together — max KE loss
  • 1D elastic: v1=(m1−m2)u1/(m1+m2), v2=2m1u1/(m1+m2)
Tip: Energy conservation is faster than F=ma for height-speed problems. Ask: can I use energy here?
Practice Problems
  1. Ball dropped from 10 m. Find speed just before hitting ground.
  2. Pendulum length L released from 60°. Find speed at lowest point.
  3. 2 kg ball at 10 m/s collides elastically with stationary 6 kg ball. Final velocities?
  4. Ball dropped from h, e=0.8 with floor. Height after 2nd bounce?

S9Ohm's Law, Resistance & Basic Circuits1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • V = IR — Ohm's law for ohmic conductors
  • R = ρL/A — resistance depends on material, length, cross-section
  • Series: Rtotal = R1+R2+R3 (same current, voltage divides)
  • Parallel: 1/Rtotal = 1/R1+1/R2+1/R3 (same voltage, current divides)
  • P = VI = I²R = V²/R — know all three forms
  • Vterminal = ε − Ir (r = internal resistance)
Tip: Simplify series/parallel first. Only then apply Kirchhoff's laws.
Practice Problems

  4. Three identical bulbs: series vs parallel. Compare brightness and power.
S10Kirchhoff's Laws1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • KCL: sum of currents into any node = sum out (charge conservation)
  • KVL: sum of all voltage changes around any closed loop = 0 (energy conservation)
  • KVL: crossing resistor in current direction = drop (−IR)
  • KVL: crossing battery from − to + = gain (+ε)
  • Wheatstone bridge balanced: R1/R2 = R3/R4
Tip: Label all currents with assumed directions FIRST. Wrong assumption gives negative answer — still correct magnitude.
Practice Problems
  1. Two-loop circuit. Find all branch currents using Kirchhoff's laws.
  2. Wheatstone bridge R1=2, R2=4, R3=3Ω. Find R4 for balance.
  3. Find current through galvanometer in unbalanced Wheatstone bridge.
  4. Battery with internal r, connected to two parallel external resistors. Find current in each.
S11Cells, Metre Bridge & Potentiometer1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Series cells: εtotal = ε1+ε2, rtotal = r1+r2
  • Parallel cells (n identical): εtotal = ε, rtotal = r/n
  • Metre bridge: X = R·l/(100−l)
  • Potentiometer: E1/E2 = l1/l2
  • Potentiometer for internal resistance: r = R(l1−l2)/l2
Tip: Understand the null method principle — at balance, no current through galvanometer, so no loading on unknown EMF.

  3. Potentiometer wire 10 m. 1.02V cell balances at 400 cm. Find EMF balancing at 360 cm.
  4. 100 W bulb, 5 hrs/day, 30 days. Energy in kWh and cost at ₹5/unit.

P5 Rotational Motion July 2026 6–8% of paper ▼
R1Torque, Angular Quantities & Newton's 2nd for Rotation1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Angular displacement θ, angular velocity ω = dθ/dt, angular acceleration α = dω/dt
  • Torque τ = r × F = r F sinθ — rotational analogue of force
  • Newton's 2nd law for rotation: τ_net = Iα
  • Equations of rotational motion: ω = ω₀+αt, θ = ω₀t+½αt², ω² = ω₀²+2αθ
  • Work done by torque: W = τθ; Power = τω
Tip: Every translational quantity has a rotational analogue: mass→I, force→τ, velocity→ω, acceleration→α. Use the exact same equations.
Practice Problems
  1. Wheel starts from rest, α=2 rad/s². Find ω and θ after 5 s.
  2. Force 10 N acts tangentially on rim of wheel r=0.5 m. Find torque.
  3. Disk I=2 kg·m². Starting from rest, torque 4 N·m applied for 5 s. Find final KE.
  4. Wheel rotating at 600 rpm brought to rest in 10 s. Find α and angular displacement.
R2Moment of Inertia — Standard Bodies & Theorems1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • I = Σmᵢrᵢ² — sum of mass×distance² from axis
  • Solid disk: I = ½MR²; Solid sphere: 2/5 MR²; Rod (centre): ML²/12
  • Hollow cylinder: MR²; Hollow sphere: 2/3 MR²
  • Parallel axis theorem: I = I_cm + Md²
  • Perpendicular axis theorem (laminas): Iz = Ix + Iy
Tip: Memorise only the 3 most common: disk (½MR²), solid sphere (2/5 MR²), rod about centre (ML²/12). Use parallel axis for everything else.
Practice Problems
  1. Find I of solid disk M=2 kg, R=0.3 m about central axis.
  2. Use parallel axis theorem: find I of disk about tangent axis parallel to diameter.
  3. I of uniform rod (L=1 m, M=3 kg) about one end?
  4. Two disks I₁=2, I₂=3 kg·m² joined together. Find combined I and ω when torque 10 N·m applied for 5 s.
R3Angular Momentum & Conservation1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Angular momentum L = Iω (for rigid body); L = r × p (for particle)
  • τ = dL/dt — torque = rate of change of angular momentum
  • Conservation: if τ_net = 0, L = Iω = constant
  • Application: skater pulling in arms → ω increases (I decreases, L constant)
  • Rolling without slipping: v = Rω, KE_total = ½Mv² + ½Iω²
Tip: Angular momentum conservation is the rotational analogue of linear momentum conservation. Same approach: if no external torque, Iω = constant.
Practice Problems
  1. Ice skater I=5 kg·m² spinning at 3 rad/s pulls in arms to I=2 kg·m². New ω?
  2. Disk I=4 kg·m² rotating 6 rad/s. Ring I=3 drops on it. Final ω?
  3. Solid sphere rolls down slope without slipping. Fraction of KE in rotation?
  4. Particle of mass 0.1 kg moves at 5 m/s. Angular momentum about point 2 m away (perpendicular)?

B1Biot-Savart Law & Ampere's Law1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Biot-Savart: dB = μ₀/(4π) · I dl × r̂/r² — magnetic field from current element
  • B at centre of circular loop: B = μ₀I/2R
  • B on axis of circular loop: B = μ₀IR²/2(R²+x²)^(3/2)
  • Ampere's law: ∮B·dl = μ₀I_enc — for symmetric configurations
  • B inside infinite solenoid: B = μ₀nI (n = turns per unit length)
  • B inside toroid: B = μ₀NI/2πr
Tip: Biot-Savart for curved wires; Ampere's law for straight wires with high symmetry (solenoid, toroid, infinite wire). Know when to use which.

B2Force on Current & Motion of Charges in B Field1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • F on current: F = BIL sinθ (θ = angle between I and B)
  • Lorentz force on charge: F = q(v × B) — magnitude qvB sinθ
  • Circular motion in B field: r = mv/qB; period T = 2πm/qB (independent of v)
  • Velocity selector: qE = qvB → v = E/B

Tip: For charged particle in B field: if velocity ⊥ B → circular motion. If velocity ∥ B → no force. If at angle → helical motion.

P7 Electromagnetic Induction & AC Circuits September 2026 7–8% of paper ▼
E1Faraday's Law, Lenz's Law & Motional EMF1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Magnetic flux: Φ = BA cosθ
  • Faraday's law: ε = −dΦ/dt (magnitude: rate of flux change)
  • Lenz's law: induced current opposes the change causing it
  • Motional EMF: ε = BLv (conductor of length L moving at velocity v in B)
  • Eddy currents: induced in bulk conductors; cause heating; used in braking
Tip: Lenz's law: the induced current creates a B field that opposes the change in flux. If flux increases, induced B opposes; if decreases, induced B supplements.
Practice Problems

E2Self & Mutual Inductance1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Self inductance L: ε = −L dI/dt; L = μ₀N²A/l (solenoid)
  • Energy stored in inductor: U = ½LI²
  • Mutual inductance M: ε₂ = −M dI₁/dt; M = k√(L₁L₂), k ≤ 1
  • Transformer: V₁/V₂ = N₁/N₂; ideal transformer: V₁I₁ = V₂I₂
Tip: Transformer step-up/step-down: more turns on secondary = higher voltage. Current does the opposite (power conservation).
Practice Problems

  2. Energy stored in 2 H inductor carrying 5 A.

E3AC Circuits — LCR, Resonance & Power Factor1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • AC: V = V₀ sinωt; I = I₀ sin(ωt+φ); V_rms = V₀/√2
  • Impedance: Z = √(R²+(X_L−X_C)²)
  • Reactances: X_L = ωL; X_C = 1/ωC
  • Resonance: ω₀ = 1/√(LC), Z = R (minimum), I = maximum
  • Power factor: cos φ = R/Z; P = V_rms I_rms cos φ
  • Pure L or C circuit: power = 0 (current and voltage 90° out of phase)
Tip: At resonance: X_L = X_C. The circuit behaves as a pure resistor. Power factor = 1. Current is maximum. This is the most important AC result.
Practice Problems
  1. Series LCR: R=5Ω, L=0.1 H, C=200μF, ω=100 rad/s. Find Z and phase angle.
  2. Find resonant frequency of LCR circuit L=2 mH, C=50 μF.
  3. Series LCR circuit at resonance: V_R=10 V, V_L=40 V. What is V_C? Find Q-factor.
  4. AC circuit: V=200 V_rms, I=5 A_rms, phase angle 60°. Find P consumed.

P8 Ray Optics October 2026 7–8% of paper ▼
O1Reflection — Mirrors (Plane & Curved)1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Laws of reflection: angle of incidence = angle of reflection (same plane)
  • Plane mirror: image is virtual, erect, same size, behind mirror
  • Mirror formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f; f = R/2
  • Sign convention: distances measured from pole; incident ray direction = positive
  • Magnification: m = −v/u (negative = real, inverted image)
  • Concave mirror: f < 0; Convex: f > 0
Tip: Sign convention is the most common source of errors in optics. Fix positive direction = incident ray, then ALWAYS apply signs before substituting into formula.
Practice Problems
  1. Object 30 cm from concave mirror f=20 cm. Find image position and magnification.
  2. Convex mirror f=15 cm, object 30 cm in front. Find image.
  3. Concave mirror forms real image twice the size of object. If image is 60 cm from mirror, find f.
  4. Find the minimum distance between object and real image for a concave mirror of f=20 cm.
O2Refraction — Snell's Law, TIR & Lenses1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Snell's law: n₁ sinθ₁ = n₂ sinθ₂
  • TIR: occurs when n₁ > n₂ and θ ≥ θ_c; sinθ_c = n₂/n₁
  • Lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f (note: different sign convention from mirror!)
  • Lens maker's equation: 1/f = (n−1)(1/R₁ − 1/R₂)
  • Power of lens: P = 1/f (in dioptres, f in metres)
  • Combination: 1/f = 1/f₁ + 1/f₂ (in contact); P = P₁ + P₂
Tip: Mirror formula and lens formula look similar but use different sign conventions! Lens: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f (note the minus). Mirror: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f (note the plus).
Practice Problems
  1. Light goes from water (n=1.33) to glass (n=1.5) at 30°. Find refracted angle.
  2. Find critical angle for glass-air interface (n_glass=1.5).
  3. Convex lens f=20 cm, object at 30 cm. Find image. Then find magnification.
  4. Two lenses +5 D and −2 D in contact. Find combined power and focal length.
O3Prism, Dispersion & Wave Optics Basics1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Prism deviation: δ = (i₁ + i₂) − A; at minimum deviation: i₁=i₂, r₁=r₂=A/2
  • n = sin((A+δₘ)/2) / sin(A/2)
  • Dispersion: different wavelengths refract different amounts (violet bends most)
  • Young's double slit: fringe width β = λD/d
  • Constructive: path diff = nλ; Destructive: path diff = (2n−1)λ/2
  • Diffraction: single slit first minimum at a sinθ = λ
Tip: In YDSE: β = λD/d — remember this formula. If wavelength or distance changes, fringe width scales proportionally.
Practice Problems
  1. Prism A=60°, δₘ=40°. Find refractive index.
  2. YDSE: λ=600 nm, D=1.5 m, d=0.5 mm. Find fringe width.
  3. In YDSE, 5th bright fringe is at same position as 3rd bright fringe of another wavelength 660 nm. Find first wavelength.
  4. Light of wavelength 500 nm incident on slit 0.1 mm wide. Find angle of first diffraction minimum.

P-P3 Thermodynamics & Revision November 2026 5–6% · Quick ROI topic ▼
TH1Thermodynamics — Laws, PV Diagrams & Carnot Cycle3 hrs (2 sessions)▼
Concepts
  • Zeroth law: thermal equilibrium defines temperature
  • First law: ΔU = Q − W; W = ∫PdV
  • Isothermal: T constant, W = nRT ln(V₂/V₁)
  • Adiabatic: no heat exchange, PV^γ = const, TV^(γ-1) = const
  • Isochoric: V constant, W=0, Q=ΔU; Isobaric: P constant, W=PΔV
  • Carnot efficiency: η = 1 − T_L/T_H (ideal maximum)
  • Entropy: ΔS = Q_rev/T; irreversible processes increase total entropy
Tip: For PV diagram questions: area under curve = work done BY gas. Work done ON gas = negative of this. Clockwise cycle = positive net work.

⚠ Common JEE Traps — Physics Phase 1

UnitThe TrapHow to Avoid It
Units & DimsCalling a formula "correct" just because it is dimensionally consistentDimensional correctness is necessary but NOT sufficient. A formula can pass the dimension test and still be wrong.
Units & DimsUsing % error in x = a³ as 3% when error in a is 3%In x = aⁿ, percentage error in x = n × percentage error in a. Write the multiplier explicitly.
KinematicsApplying v=u+at when acceleration is NOT constantEquations of motion ONLY work for uniform (constant) acceleration. Variable a → use calculus or graphs.
KinematicsMixing horizontal and vertical in projectile motionWrite horizontal and vertical equations on separate lines. Never combine them.
NLMDrawing friction in the wrong direction on FBDAsk: which way would object tend to move WITHOUT friction? Friction opposes that tendency.
NLMAssuming normal force = mg on inclines or in accelerating framesDerive normal force from FBD every single time. It is NOT always mg.
Work-EnergyUsing KE + PE = constant when friction is presentWith friction: KEf + PEf = KEi + PEi − |Wfriction|. Subtract energy lost to friction.
ElectricityUsing R = R1 + R2 for parallel resistorsParallel: 1/R = 1/R1 + 1/R2. Write this formula before substituting numbers — every time.
ElectricityWrong sign when crossing a battery in KVLFix a direction. Crossing from − to + terminal of battery = GAIN (+ε). Crossing + to − = drop (−ε).
