C1 Mole Concept & Stoichiometry March 2026 7–9% · Foundation of all Phys. Chem ▼

Critical unit: Mole Concept is the foundation of ALL Physical Chemistry. Nothing in Phase 2 chemistry (equilibrium, electrochemistry) works without this unit being fully mastered. Spend extra time here if needed.

S1Mole, Atomic Mass & Avogadro's Number1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Atomic mass unit: 1 amu = 1/12 mass of C-12 atom
  • 1 mole = 6.022 × 1023 particles (Avogadro's number NA)
  • Molar mass: mass in grams of 1 mole = numerical value of molecular weight
  • n = mass (g) / molar mass (g/mol)
  • Number of particles = n × NA
  • Gas at STP: 1 mole = 22.4 L (molar volume)
  • Empirical vs molecular formula: find from % composition
Tip: Keep a table of common molar masses: H=1, C=12, N=14, O=16, Na=23, Cl=35.5, Ca=40. Refer constantly until memorised.
Practice Problems
  1. Find moles and number of molecules in 44 g of CO₂.
  2. Find mass of 3 moles of H₂SO₄.
  3. Compound is 40% C, 6.67% H, 53.33% O. Find empirical formula.
  4. Find number of atoms in 0.1 mol of Na₂SO₄.
S2Stoichiometry & Limiting Reagent1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Balanced equation gives mole ratios of reactants and products
  • Stoichiometry: use mole ratios to find mass/moles of products
  • Limiting reagent: reactant consumed first — limits product amount
  • Excess reagent: left over after reaction is complete
  • % yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100
  • % purity = (pure substance / total mass) × 100
Tip: For limiting reagent: divide moles of each reactant by its stoichiometric coefficient. The smaller ratio is the limiting reagent.
Practice Problems
  1. 10 g H₂ reacts with 80 g O₂ to form water. Find limiting reagent and mass of water.
  2. 20 g CaCO₃ heated. Find mass of CO₂ produced (80% yield).
  3. N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃. If 28 g N₂ and 9 g H₂ are mixed, find moles of NH₃.
  4. 5 g impure NaOH requires 200 mL of 0.5M HCl. Find % purity of NaOH.
S3Concentration Terms & Solutions1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Molarity M = moles of solute / volume of solution (L)
  • Molality m = moles of solute / mass of solvent (kg)
  • Mole fraction: XA = nA / (nA + nB)
  • % by mass: (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 100
  • Dilution: M1V1 = M2V2 (moles constant)
  • Molarity changes with temperature (volume changes); Molality does NOT
Tip: Molarity uses volume of solution (temperature-sensitive). Molality uses mass of solvent (temperature-independent). JEE exploits this distinction.
Practice Problems
  1. Dissolve 9.8 g H₂SO₄ in 500 mL solution. Find molarity.
  2. Find molality of 20% NaOH solution (density 1.22 g/mL).
  3. 200 mL of 0.5M HCl + 300 mL of 0.2M HCl. Find resulting molarity.
  4. Find mole fraction of ethanol in 46 g ethanol + 180 g water.

S4Bohr Model & Atomic Spectra1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Bohr postulates: electrons orbit in fixed energy levels; energy emitted/absorbed when jumping
  • Energy of nth orbit: En = −13.6/n² eV (for hydrogen)
  • Radius of nth orbit: rn = 0.529 × n² Å (for hydrogen)
  • Rydberg equation: 1/λ = RH(1/n1² − 1/n2²)
  • Lyman series: UV, n→1; Balmer: visible, n→2; Paschen: IR, n→3
  • de Broglie: λ = h/mv — wave nature of particles
  • Heisenberg uncertainty: Δx·Δp ≥ h/4π
Tip: Lyman series ends at n=1 (UV), Balmer ends at n=2 (visible). Memorise this — JEE tests it directly.
Practice Problems
  1. Find energy and radius of 3rd orbit of hydrogen.
  2. Electron jumps from n=4 to n=2. Find wavelength of emitted photon. Which series?
  3. How many spectral lines are possible when electron falls from n=5 to n=1?
  4. Find de Broglie wavelength of electron moving at 2×10⁶ m/s.
S5Quantum Numbers & Orbitals1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Principal quantum number n: shell, determines size and energy
  • Azimuthal l: 0 to (n-1), determines subshell shape — s(0), p(1), d(2), f(3)
  • Magnetic ml: −l to +l, determines orientation
  • Spin ms: +½ or −½, two electrons per orbital
  • Pauli exclusion: no two electrons have all 4 quantum numbers same
  • Max electrons: shell n → 2n²; subshells: s=2, p=6, d=10, f=14
Tip: Quantum number rules must be memorised perfectly — they appear in almost every Atomic Structure JEE question.
Practice Problems
  1. Write all possible quantum numbers for electrons in 3d subshell.
  2. How many electrons can have n=3, l=1?
  3. Which set of quantum numbers is NOT possible: (2,1,0,+½), (3,3,0,+½), (2,0,0,−½)?
  4. Find total orbitals and max electrons in n=4 shell.
S6Electron Configuration & Periodic Trends1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Aufbau: fill in order 1s<2s<2p<3s<3p<4s<3d<4p<5s<4d...
  • Hund's rule: fill each orbital singly before pairing
  • Exceptions: Cr = [Ar]3d⁵4s¹ and Cu = [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹
  • Atomic radius: decreases across period, increases down group
  • Ionisation energy: increases across period, decreases down group
  • Electronegativity: F > O > N > Cl > Br > S > C > H
Tip: Cr and Cu exceptions appear almost every year. Memorise: Cr=[Ar]3d⁵4s¹, Cu=[Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹. Half-filled and fully-filled d are extra stable.
Practice Problems
  1. Write electron configuration of Fe (Z=26) and Fe²⁺.
  2. Arrange in order of increasing atomic radius: Na, Mg, Al, Si.
  3. Which has highest 1st ionisation energy: Li, Be, B, N, O?
  4. Why does Cr have [Ar]3d⁵4s¹ instead of [Ar]3d⁴4s²?

S7Ionic & Covalent Bonding, Lewis Structures1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Ionic bond: electron transfer between metal and non-metal
  • Covalent bond: electron sharing between non-metals
  • Bond order: single=1, double=2, triple=3 — higher order = shorter and stronger
  • Formal charge = valence e⁻ − non-bonding e⁻ − ½(bonding e⁻)
  • Lewis structures: satisfy octet rule; exceptions: H (2), Be, B (incomplete octets), P, S (expanded)
  • Resonance: multiple valid Lewis structures — actual structure is average
Tip: Draw Lewis structure before attempting VSEPR or hybridisation. The structure tells you everything.
Practice Problems
  1. Draw Lewis structures of SO₂, PCl₅, XeF₄.
  2. Find formal charge on each atom in CO₂ and CO.
  3. Which has shorter C-O bond: CO, CO₂, or CO₃²⁻? Explain using bond order.
  4. Why does BF₃ violate the octet rule? Draw its Lewis structure.
S8VSEPR Theory & Hybridisation1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • VSEPR: electron pairs repel — geometry minimises repulsion
  • Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs: LP-LP > LP-BP > BP-BP
  • sp: linear (2 BP, 0 LP) — e.g. CO₂, BeH₂
  • sp²: trigonal planar (3 BP, 0 LP) — e.g. BF₃, SO₃
  • sp³: tetrahedral (4 BP, 0 LP) — e.g. CH₄; bent if lone pairs — H₂O
  • sp³d: trigonal bipyramidal (PCl₅); sp³d²: octahedral (SF₆)
Tip: Count all electron pairs (bonding + lone) around central atom. Pairs: 2→linear, 3→trigonal, 4→tetrahedral, 5→trig. bipyramidal, 6→octahedral.
Practice Problems
  1. Predict geometry and hybridisation of NH₃, H₂O, SF₆, PCl₅.
  2. Why is bond angle in H₂O (104.5°) less than in NH₃ (107°)?
  3. Which is more polar — NF₃ or NH₃? Justify using VSEPR.
  4. Predict geometry of XeF₂, XeF₄, XeOF₄.
S9Polarity & Intermolecular Forces1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Molecular polarity = vector sum of all bond dipoles
  • CO₂ is non-polar (linear, dipoles cancel); H₂O is polar (bent, dipoles add)
  • Van der Waals: London (all molecules), dipole-dipole (polar), H-bonding
  • H-bond: F-H, O-H, N-H with electronegative atom — stronger than vdW
  • Stronger intermolecular forces → higher boiling point
  • H₂O has anomalously high boiling point due to H-bonding network
Tip: Symmetrical molecules are non-polar even if bonds are polar — CCl₄, CO₂, BF₃. Asymmetrical with polar bonds → polar.
Practice Problems
  1. Which is more polar and why: HF or HCl? CCl₄ or CHCl₃?
  2. Arrange in order of increasing boiling point: CH₄, NH₃, HF, H₂O.
  3. Why does HF have lower boiling point than H₂O despite stronger H-bonds per molecule?
  4. Which molecules have H-bonding: CH₃OH, CH₃F, NH₃, HCN?

C4 Thermochemistry June 2026 5–6% of paper ▼
S10Enthalpy, Heat & First Law of Thermodynamics1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • First Law: ΔU = q + w (internal energy = heat + work)
  • Work at constant pressure: w = −PΔV
  • Enthalpy: ΔH = ΔU + ΔngRT (for reactions involving gases)
  • Exothermic: ΔH < 0 (heat released); Endothermic: ΔH > 0 (heat absorbed)
  • Standard enthalpy ΔH° at 298 K, 1 atm
  • ΔH°f of any element in standard state = 0
Tip: Sign convention: q > 0 means heat flows INTO system. ΔH < 0 means reaction releases heat (exothermic).
Practice Problems
  1. For 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l), ΔH = −572 kJ. Find ΔH for formation of 1 mol H₂O.
  2. Find ΔU for a reaction where ΔH = −100 kJ and Δng = 2 at 300 K.
  3. Gas expands from 5L to 10L at constant pressure of 2 atm. Find work done by gas.
  4. Classify: combustion of CH₄, dissolving NH₄NO₃ in water — exothermic or endothermic?
S11Hess's Law & Bond Energies1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Hess's Law: ΔH change for a reaction is same regardless of path taken
  • Reverse a reaction → ΔH changes sign
  • Multiply reaction by n → ΔH multiplied by n
  • Standard ΔH°rxn = Σ ΔH°f(products) − Σ ΔH°f(reactants)
  • Bond energy method: ΔH°rxn = Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds formed)
  • Bond breaking: endothermic (+); bond forming: exothermic (−)
Tip: For Hess's law: write target equation first. Manipulate given equations piece by piece to match it.
Practice Problems
  1. Using Hess's law: find ΔH for C₂H₂ combustion from given ΔH values for C and H₂ combustion.
  2. Using bond energies, find ΔH for H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl. (H-H=436, Cl-Cl=242, H-Cl=431 kJ/mol)
  3. ΔH°f: CO₂=−393, H₂O=−286, C₆H₆=+49 kJ/mol. Find ΔH of combustion of benzene.
  4. Find enthalpy of sublimation of carbon using Hess's law.
S12Entropy, Gibbs Free Energy & Spontaneity1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Entropy S: measure of disorder — gases > liquids > solids
  • Spontaneous processes increase total entropy of universe
  • ΔS > 0: gas produced, dissolving, heating; ΔS < 0: gas absorbed, crystallisation
  • Gibbs free energy: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS
  • Spontaneous if ΔG < 0; non-spontaneous if ΔG > 0
  • ΔG° = −RT ln K (connects thermodynamics to equilibrium)
Tip: Entropy: more moles of gas = more disorder = positive ΔS. This is the most frequently tested entropy concept in JEE.
Practice Problems
  1. Predict sign of ΔS: (a) ice melts (b) 2 gases → 1 gas (c) solid dissolves.
  2. ΔH = −200 kJ, ΔS = −100 J/K at 500 K. Find ΔG. Is reaction spontaneous?
  3. ΔH = +30 kJ, ΔS = +100 J/K. At what temperature does reaction become spontaneous?
  4. For each combination of (+/−) ΔH and ΔS, classify spontaneity at high and low temperatures.

C5 Chemical Equilibrium July 2026 7–9% · High-yield Physical Chem ▼
E1Kp, Kc & Reaction Quotient Q1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Law of mass action: Kc = [products]^p / [reactants]^r at equilibrium
  • Kp = Kc(RT)Δng; Δng = moles of gaseous products − moles of gaseous reactants
  • Reaction quotient Q: same expression as K but at non-equilibrium concentrations
  • Q < K → reaction proceeds forward; Q > K → proceeds backward; Q = K → at equilibrium
  • K for reverse reaction = 1/K; multiply reaction by n → Kn
  • Kp has no units when standard pressures are used (dimensionless K in thermodynamics)
Tip: Q tells direction; K tells extent. If Q < K, need more products. If Q > K, need more reactants.
Practice Problems
  1. N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃. Write Kc and Kp. Find Kp if Kc=0.5 at 400 K.
  2. 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃, Kc=4. [SO₂]=0.4M, [O₂]=0.3M. Find [SO₃] at equilibrium.
  3. For PCl₅ ⇌ PCl₃ + Cl₂, Kc=1.8×10−7 at 250°C. Find Kp. (R=0.082)
  4. At equilibrium [N₂]=2M, [H₂]=3M, [NH₃]=4M and Kc=0.148. Find Q and predict direction.
E2Le Chatelier's Principle — All Effects1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Le Chatelier: equilibrium shifts to oppose any stress applied to the system
  • Concentration: adding reactant → shifts forward; adding product → shifts backward
  • Pressure: increase pressure (decrease V) → shifts to side with fewer gas moles
  • Temperature: exothermic rxn → increase T shifts backward (treat heat as product)
  • Temperature: endothermic rxn → increase T shifts forward
  • Catalyst: speeds up both directions equally — K unchanged, equilibrium reached faster
Tip: Haber process (N₂+3H₂→2NH₃, exothermic): high P→more NH₃; low T→more NH₃ but slow; catalyst is the practical compromise.
Practice Problems
  1. N₂+3H₂⇌2NH₃, ΔH=−92kJ. Predict shift when: (a) pressure increased (b) T increased (c) NH₃ removed.
  2. CO(g)+H₂O(g)⇌CO₂(g)+H₂(g). Effect of increasing pressure on equilibrium position?
  3. PCl₅(g)⇌PCl₃(g)+Cl₂(g). Addition of inert gas at constant total pressure — which way does it shift?
  4. Fe₃O₄+4H₂⇌3Fe+4H₂O, endothermic. How do you maximise Fe yield? State all conditions.
E3Degree of Dissociation & ICE Tables1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Degree of dissociation α: fraction of initial moles that dissociate (0 to 1)
  • ICE table: Initial, Change, Equilibrium — set up from stoichiometry before every numerical
  • PCl₅ ⇌ PCl₃ + Cl₂: initial = a; at eq: (a−x), x, x → Kc = x²/(V(a−x))
  • Kp for PCl₅ dissociation: Kp = α²P/(1−α²) when Δng=1
  • N₂O₄ ⇌ 2NO₂: common JEE numerical — set up ICE with α and total pressure P
  • Higher pressure → smaller α (equilibrium shifts to fewer gas moles side)
Tip: Always set up ICE table before solving any equilibrium numerical — never skip it even for "simple" problems.
Practice Problems
  1. PCl₅→PCl₃+Cl₂. Initial 2 mol PCl₅ in 5L. At eq, 0.5 mol PCl₃. Find Kc.
  2. A(g) ⇌ 2B(g), Kp=4 atm, initial pressure of A = 2 atm. Find equilibrium pressures.
  3. N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g). α=0.5 and total pressure=1 atm at 25°C. Find Kp.
  4. Find degree of dissociation of PCl₅ at 2 atm if Kp=1.78 atm.

C6 Ionic Equilibrium August 2026 5–7% · Acid-Base always in JEE ▼
I1pH Scale, Strong & Weak Acids/Bases1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • pH = −log[H⁺]; pOH = −log[OH⁻]; pH + pOH = 14 at 25°C
  • Strong acids (HCl, HNO₃, H₂SO₄): [H⁺] = concentration (fully dissociates)
  • Weak acid Ka: HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻; Ka = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA]
  • Weak acid: [H⁺] = √(Ka·C); pH = ½(pKa − log C) when α << 1
  • Ostwald's dilution law: α = √(Ka/C) — degree of dissociation increases on dilution
  • Lewis acids: electron pair acceptors (BF₃, AlCl₃); Lewis bases: electron donors
Tip: For weak acid: [H⁺]=√(KaC) is valid only when α << 1. If C is very small or Ka is large, solve the quadratic exactly.
Practice Problems
  1. Find pH of 0.01 M HCl and 0.01 M NaOH.
  2. Find pH of 0.1 M acetic acid (Ka=1.8×10⁻⁵).
  3. pH of 0.1 M weak monobasic acid is 3. Find Ka and degree of dissociation.
  4. For 0.001 M Ba(OH)₂ solution, find pH. (Ba(OH)₂ is strong, diprotic base)
I2Buffer Solutions & Henderson Equation1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Buffer: resists pH change — weak acid + its conjugate base (e.g. CH₃COOH / CH₃COO⁻Na⁺)
  • Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
  • Best buffer: pH ≈ pKa; equal moles acid and conjugate base
  • Basic buffer: weak base + its salt (e.g. NH₄OH/NH₄Cl); pOH = pKb + log([salt]/[base])
  • Salt hydrolysis: CH₃COONa in water → basic solution (A⁻ is basic); NH₄Cl → acidic
  • Hydrolysis constant: Kh = Kw/Ka or Kw/Kb
Tip: Adding strong acid to buffer: acid reacts with A⁻ → reduce [A⁻], increase [HA]. Recalculate pH from new ratio with Henderson equation.
Practice Problems
  1. Find pH of buffer: 0.1 mol CH₃COOH + 0.1 mol CH₃COONa in 1L. (Ka=1.8×10⁻⁵)
  2. Find pH change when 0.01 mol HCl is added to 1L of the above buffer.
  3. What is pH of 0.1 M NH₄Cl solution? (Kb of NH₃=1.8×10⁻⁵, Kw=10⁻¹⁴)
  4. An acetic acid/sodium acetate buffer has pH=5.0. Find [A⁻]/[HA] ratio. (pKa=4.74)
I3Solubility Product Ksp & Common Ion Effect1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Ksp = [Mn+]^a [Xm−]^b for MaXb(s) ⇌ aMn+ + bXm−
  • Solubility s: MX → s, s (Ksp=s²); MX₂ → s, 2s (Ksp=4s³)
  • Common ion effect: adding common ion decreases solubility (shifts left)
  • Precipitation: ionic product IP > Ksp → precipitate forms; IP < Ksp → remains dissolved
  • Selective precipitation: add precipitating agent slowly to separate ions with different Ksp
Tip: Ksp doesn't change with common ion — only solubility changes. Don't confuse "ion product" with Ksp.
Practice Problems
  1. Find molar solubility of BaSO₄ in pure water. (Ksp=1.08×10⁻¹⁰)
  2. Find molar solubility of AgCl in 0.1 M NaCl solution. (Ksp=1.8×10⁻¹⁰)
  3. Mg(OH)₂ Ksp=1.8×10⁻¹¹. Find molar solubility and pH of saturated solution.
  4. Will BaSO₄ precipitate when 100 mL of 0.001 M Ba²⁺ is mixed with 100 mL of 0.001 M SO₄²⁻?

C7 Electrochemistry September 2026 6–8% · Very high yield ▼
EC1Galvanic Cells — EMF & Cell Notation1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Galvanic cell: spontaneous redox reaction converts chemical energy → electrical energy
  • Anode: oxidation (negative electrode); Cathode: reduction (positive electrode)
  • Cell notation: Anode | solution || solution | Cathode (e.g. Zn|Zn²⁺||Cu²⁺|Cu)
  • E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode (both as standard reduction potentials)
  • Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE): E° = 0 V (reference electrode)
  • Spontaneous cell: E°cell > 0 ↔ ΔG° < 0 ↔ K > 1
Tip: Always: E°cell = E°red(cathode) − E°red(anode). Never reverse both signs and add — that's the classic JEE mistake.
Practice Problems
  1. E°(Zn²⁺/Zn)=−0.76V, E°(Cu²⁺/Cu)=+0.34V. Find E°cell for Daniel cell.
  2. Write cell notation for Daniel cell. Identify anode and cathode reactions.
  3. E°(Ag⁺/Ag)=+0.80V, E°(Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺)=+0.77V. Which is better reducing agent? Why?
  4. Will Cu displace Ag from AgNO₃? E°(Cu²⁺/Cu)=+0.34V, E°(Ag⁺/Ag)=+0.80V.
EC2Nernst Equation & Concentration Cells1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Nernst equation: E = E° − (0.0592/n) log Q at 25°C
  • n = number of electrons transferred in the balanced redox equation
  • At equilibrium: E = 0 → log K = nE°/0.0592
  • ΔG = −nFE; ΔG° = −nFE° (F = 96500 C/mol)
  • Concentration cell: both electrodes same metal, different concentrations — E° = 0
  • Concentration cell EMF: E = (0.0592/n) log([high]/[low])
Tip: 0.0592/n factor is ONLY at 25°C. For other temperatures, use (2.303RT/nF). JEE usually assumes 25°C unless stated.
Practice Problems
  1. Find EMF of Daniel cell when [Zn²⁺]=0.1M, [Cu²⁺]=1M. (E°=1.10V, n=2)
  2. Find equilibrium constant K for Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu at 25°C. (E°=1.10V)
  3. Concentration cell: Cu|Cu²⁺(0.01M)||Cu²⁺(0.1M)|Cu. Find EMF at 25°C.
  4. ΔG° = −212 kJ/mol, n=2. Find E° of the cell.
EC3Electrolysis & Faraday's Laws1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Electrolysis: non-spontaneous redox using external electrical energy
  • Faraday's First Law: mass deposited ∝ charge passed (Q = It)
  • Faraday's Second Law: same charge → deposits equivalent masses of different substances
  • m = (M × I × t) / (n × F); F = 96500 C/mol, n = electrons per ion
  • 1 Faraday = 1 mole of electrons = 96500 C
  • During water electrolysis: cathode → H₂; anode → O₂; moles H₂ : O₂ = 2:1
Tip: Sequence: find charge Q=It → moles of electrons = Q/F → use stoichiometry → mass or volume at STP.
Practice Problems
  1. Find mass of Cu deposited when 2A flows through CuSO₄ for 30 min. (MCu=63.5)
  2. How many Faradays are needed to deposit 1 mole of Al from Al³⁺?
  3. 5A flows for 1 hr through AgNO₃. Find mass of Ag deposited and volume of O₂ at STP at anode.
  4. In electrolysis, 0.5 mol each of Ag⁺ and Cu²⁺ are present. Which deposits more with same charge?
EC4Conductance & Kohlrausch's Law1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Resistance R = ρ·l/A; Conductance G = 1/R (unit: Siemens S)
  • Specific conductivity κ = G × (l/A); Molar conductivity Λm = κ×1000/C (C in mol/L)
  • Strong electrolytes: Λm slightly increases with dilution (interionic forces decrease)
  • Weak electrolytes: large increase in Λm at high dilution (more dissociation)
  • Kohlrausch's Law: Λ°m = Σ λ°(cation) + Σ λ°(anion) — limiting molar conductivity
  • Find Ka of weak acid from conductance: α = Λm/Λ°m; Ka = Cα²/(1−α)
Tip: Kohlrausch's law is additive — find Λ°m of a weak electrolyte from strong electrolyte data. Classic JEE application.
Practice Problems
  1. Find Λ°m(CH₃COOH) from: Λ°m(HCl)=426, Λ°m(CH₃COONa)=91, Λ°m(NaCl)=126 S·cm²/mol.
  2. Conductivity of 0.1 M CH₃COOH = 0.0041 S/m. Λ°m=390 S·cm²/mol. Find α and Ka.
  3. Conductance of 0.01 M NaCl = 1.19×10⁻³ S. Cell constant = 1.15 cm⁻¹. Find molar conductivity.
  4. Molar conductivity of weak acid increases from 20 to 300 S·cm²/mol on dilution. Find α.

C8 Organic Chemistry — General Organic Chemistry (GOC) October 2026 20–25% · Largest single topic area ▼

Organic is the biggest scoring area. GOC provides the conceptual foundation — electronic effects and reaction mechanisms. Master this unit and all organic reactions in Phase 3 become much easier.

OC1GOC — Inductive, Resonance & Hyperconjugation Effects2 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Inductive effect (I): through σ bonds — withdrawal (−I: F, Cl, NO₂, CN) or donation (+I: alkyl)
  • −I effect increases acidity (stabilises carboxylate anion): CF₃COOH >> CH₃COOH
  • Mesomeric/resonance effect (M): delocalisation through π system or lone pairs
  • +M groups (donors): −NH₂, −OH, −OR; −M groups (withdrawers): −NO₂, −CN, −CHO
  • Hyperconjugation: σ C-H electrons delocalize into adjacent π orbital — stabilises carbocations
  • Stability order: resonance > hyperconjugation > inductive effect
Tip: More −I substituents → more acidic acid. More +I (alkyl groups) → more basic amine/alcohol. Always consider both inductive and resonance together.
Practice Problems
  1. Arrange in increasing acidity: CH₃COOH, FCH₂COOH, F₂CHCOOH, F₃CCOOH.
  2. Compare +M effect: −NH₂ vs −OH. Which is stronger electron donor? Justify.
  3. Why is C₂H₅⁺ more stable than CH₃⁺? Explain using hyperconjugation.
  4. Explain why phenol is more acidic than cyclohexanol despite both having −OH group.
OC2Reaction Intermediates — Carbocations, Carbanions & Radicals1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Carbocation (R⁺): sp² hybridised, empty p orbital; stabilised by +I, +M, hyperconjugation
  • Carbocation stability: 3° > 2° > 1° > methyl; allyl ≈ benzyl > 3° (resonance stabilised)
  • Carbanion (R⁻): lone pair; stabilised by −I and −M groups (electron withdrawers)
  • Carbanion stability: methyl > 1° > 2° > 3° — OPPOSITE of carbocation
  • Free radical: odd electron, sp²; stability: 3° > 2° > 1° (same as carbocation)
  • sp-hybridised carbanion (HC≡C⁻) is more stable due to higher s-character
Tip: Carbocation and radical both follow 3°>2°>1°. Carbanion is OPPOSITE: methyl > 1° > 2° > 3°. Don't confuse them in JEE questions.
Practice Problems
  1. Arrange in increasing stability: (CH₃)₃C⁺, CH₃⁺, (CH₃)₂CH⁺, CH₃CH₂⁺.
  2. Why is allylic carbocation more stable than simple 2° carbocation?
  3. Arrange in increasing stability of carbanion: CH₃⁻, (CH₃)₂CH⁻, C₆H₅CH₂⁻, HC≡C⁻.
  4. Which intermediate is involved in reaction of 3° alkyl halide with H₂O? Give mechanism type.
OC3SN1 & SN2 — Mechanism & Stereochemistry1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • SN2: bimolecular, one step, backside attack → inversion of configuration (Walden inversion)
  • SN2 favoured by: 1° substrate, strong nucleophile, polar aprotic solvent (DMSO, acetone)
  • SN1: unimolecular, two steps via carbocation → racemisation (mixture of enantiomers)
  • SN1 favoured by: 3° substrate, weak nucleophile, polar protic solvent (water, alcohol)
  • Nucleophilicity vs basicity: in protic solvents, large ions better nucleophiles (I⁻ > F⁻)
  • Leaving group ability: I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻ (inverse of base strength)
Tip: 1° → SN2; 3° → SN1; 2° → depends on conditions. Polar aprotic → SN2; polar protic → SN1. This pattern is tested directly in JEE.
Practice Problems
  1. State mechanism (SN1/SN2) for: (a) CH₃Br + OH⁻ (b) (CH₃)₃CBr + H₂O.
  2. What stereochemical outcome is expected in SN2 reaction of (R)-2-bromobutane?
  3. Reactivity in SN2: CH₃Cl, C₂H₅Cl, (CH₃)₂CHCl, (CH₃)₃CCl — arrange in decreasing order.
  4. Why does neopentyl bromide undergo SN2 very slowly despite being a 1° halide?
OC4Elimination Reactions — E1 & E21.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Elimination: remove HX from adjacent carbons to form C=C double bond
  • E2: bimolecular, one step, anti-periplanar H and LG required; rate = k[RX][B⁻]
  • E1: unimolecular, two steps via carbocation; rate = k[RX]; favoured by 3° substrate
  • Zaitsev's rule: more substituted (more stable) alkene is major product
  • Hofmann product: bulky base → less substituted alkene (steric hindrance)
  • Competition SN vs E: strong base + high T → E favoured; weak nucleophile + low T → SN
Tip: t-BuOK (bulky) → Hofmann product; NaOH/KOH (small) → Zaitsev product. Strong base + 3° substrate → E2 predominates.
Practice Problems
  1. Write products for 2-bromobutane + KOH/ethanol. Identify Zaitsev product.
  2. Why does 2-bromobutane + t-BuOK give 1-butene (less substituted) as major product?
  3. For 2-bromo-2-methylbutane + KOH: write all possible elimination products. State major one.
  4. Draw the E2 transition state and explain the anti-periplanar requirement.
OC5Addition Reactions — Markovnikov & Anti-Markovnikov1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Electrophilic addition to alkenes: proton adds to C with more H (Markovnikov's rule)
  • Markovnikov: ionic mechanism — forms more stable carbocation intermediate
  • Anti-Markovnikov (HBr + peroxide ROOR): radical mechanism — Br goes to C with more H
  • Halogenation: Br₂/CCl₄ → trans addition (anti-addition) via bromonium ion intermediate
  • Acid-catalysed hydration: H₂O/H⁺ → Markovnikov alcohol via carbocation
  • Hydroboration-oxidation: BH₃ then H₂O₂/NaOH → anti-Markovnikov, syn addition
Tip: "Anti-Markovnikov" always means peroxide (ROOR) present = radical mechanism. Without peroxide = ionic mechanism = Markovnikov.
Practice Problems
  1. Predict major products: (a) propene + HBr (b) propene + HBr/ROOR (c) propene + Br₂/CCl₄.
  2. What is the product of acid-catalysed hydration of 2-methylpropene?
  3. Why does addition of HBr to propene give 2-bromopropane (not 1-bromopropane) without peroxide?
  4. What product forms on addition of Cl₂ to (Z)-but-2-ene? What is the stereochemistry?
OC6Aromaticity & Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution1.5 hrs▼
Concepts
  • Hückel's rule: aromatic if planar, cyclic, fully conjugated, (4n+2) π electrons (n=0,1,2…)
  • Anti-aromatic: 4n π electrons (cyclobutadiene=4, cyclooctatetraene=8)
  • EAS mechanism: arenium ion (σ-complex) intermediate; electrophile attacks ring
  • Activating, ortho/para directors (+M groups): −OH, −NH₂, −OR (electron donors)
  • Deactivating, meta directors (−M only): −NO₂, −CN, −CHO, −COOH, −SO₃H
  • Deactivating but o/p directors: −F, −Cl, −Br, −I (−I deactivates, +M still directs o/p)
Tip: Halogens deactivate ring but are o/p directors — this JEE trap is worth memorising. The +M lone pair donation overrides −I deactivation for directing.
Practice Problems
  1. Classify as aromatic/antiaromatic/non-aromatic: benzene, cyclobutadiene, [18]annulene, pyrrole, furan.
  2. Nitration of toluene — predict the major product and justify using directing effects.
  3. Why do halogens deactivate the ring but direct ortho/para? Explain the apparent contradiction.
  4. Predict product of sulfonation of aniline. Is aniline more or less reactive than benzene?

⚠ Common JEE Traps — Chemistry

UnitThe TrapHow to Avoid It
Mole ConceptUsing mass instead of moles in stoichiometryAlways convert mass → moles first. Ratios in balanced equations are MOLE ratios, not mass ratios.
Mole ConceptConfusing Molarity and Molality in temperature-sensitive problemsMolarity uses volume (changes with T). Molality uses mass of solvent (T-independent). Know when to use which.
Atomic StructureWriting wrong electron config for Cr and CuMemorise: Cr=[Ar]3d⁵4s¹, Cu=[Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹. Half-filled and fully-filled d subshells are extra stable.
Chemical BondingCalling CO₂ polar because C=O bonds are polarCO₂ is LINEAR — the two equal and opposite dipoles cancel exactly. Symmetry makes it non-polar.
Chemical BondingConfusing bond angle decrease due to lone pairsLone pairs compress angles more than bonding pairs. NH₃ (107°) < CH₄ (109.5°), H₂O (104.5°) < NH₃.
ThermochemistryGetting sign wrong when reversing equations in Hess's lawWhen you REVERSE a reaction, the sign of ΔH flips. Write "−ΔH" explicitly. Don't do it mentally.
ThermochemistryUsing non-zero ΔH°f for elements in standard stateStandard enthalpy of formation of any element in its standard state = 0. Always. No exceptions.
